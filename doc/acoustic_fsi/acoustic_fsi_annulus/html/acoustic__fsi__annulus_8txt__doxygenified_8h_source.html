3 \mainpage Time-harmonic acoustic fluid-structure interaction problems

5 
6 In this document we discuss the solution of
7 time-harmonic acoustic fluid-structure interaction problems.
8  We start by reviewing the relevant theory and then
9 present the solution of a simple model problem -- the sound radiation
10 from an oscillating circular cylinder that is coated with a compressible
11 elastic layer.
12 
13 This problem combines the problems discussed in the tutorials
14 illustrating
15 - <a href="../../../time_harmonic_linear_elasticity/elastic_annulus/html/index.html">the solution of the time-harmonic equations of linear elasticity</a>
16 .
17 and
18 - <a href="../../../helmholtz/scattering/html/index.html">the Helmholtz
19 equation.</a>

24 \section theory Theory: Time-harmonic acoustic fluid-structure interaction problems
25 
26 The figure below shows a sketch of a representative model problem:
27 a circular cylinder is immersed in an inviscid compressible fluid and
28 performs a prescribed two-dimensional harmonic oscillation of radian
29 frequency \f$\omega\f$. The cylinder is coated with a compressible elastic
30 layer. We wish to compute the displacement field in the elastic coating
31 (assumed to be described by the <a href="../../../time_harmonic_linear_elasticity/elastic_annulus/html/index.html">equations of
32 time-harmonic linear elasticity</a>) and the pressure distribution
33 in the fluid (governed by
34 <a href="../../../helmholtz/scattering/html/index.html">the Helmholtz
35 equation</a>). The two sets of equations interact at the interface
36 between fluid and solid: the fluid pressure exerts a traction
37 onto the elastic layer, while the motion of the elastic layer
38 drives the fluid motion via the non-penetration condition.
39

41 \image latex coated_pipe.eps "Sketch of the model problem: Forced oscillations of a circular cylinder (black) deform an elastic coating layer (pink) which is surrounded by a compressible fluid. " width=0.6\textwidth

43 \subsection fluid The fluid model: the Helmholtz equation
44 We describe the behaviour of the fluid in terms of the displacement
45 field, \f$ d_i^*(x_j^*,t^*)\f$, of the fluid particles. As usual
46 we employ index notation and the summation convention,
47 and use asterisks to distinguish dimensional quantities from
48 their non-dimensional equivalents. The fluid is inviscid
49 and compressible, with a bulk modulus \f$ B \f$, such
50 that the acoustic pressure is given by
51 \f$ P^* = - B \ \partial d_j^*/\partial x_j^* \f$
52 We assume that the fluid motion is irrotational and can be described by a
53 displacement potential \f$\Phi^*\f$, such that \f$ d^*_j = \partial
54 \Phi^*/\partial x_j^*. \f$ We consider steady-state time-harmonic
55 oscillations and write the displacement potential and the pressure
56 as \f$\Phi^*(x_j^*,t^*) =
57 \mbox{Re} \{\phi^*(x_j^*) \exp(-{\rm i} \omega t^*)\} \f$ and
58 \f$P^*(x_j^*,t^*) = \mbox{Re}\{p^*(x_j^*) \exp(-{\rm i} \omega
59 t^*)\}\f$, respectively,
60 where \f$\mbox{Re}\{\ldots \}\f$ denotes the real part.
61 For small disturbances, the linearised Euler equation reveals that the
62 time-harmonic pressure is related to the displacement potential
63 via \f$ p^* = \rho_{\rm f} \omega^2 \phi^*\f$ where
64 \f$ \rho_{\rm f} \f$ is the ambient fluid density.
65 We non-dimensionalise all lengths
66 on a problem-specific lengthscale \f$ {\cal L}\f$ (e.g. the
67 outer radius of the coating layer) such that
68 \f$x_j^* = {\cal L} x_j,\ d_j^* = {\cal L} d_j\f$ and \f$\phi^*
69 = {\cal L}^2 \phi\f$. The non-dimensional displacement potential \f$\phi\f$
70 is then governed by the Helmholtz equation
71 \f[
72 \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x_j^{2}} + k^2 \phi = 0,
73 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1)
74 \f]
75 where the square of the non-dimensional wavenumber,
76 \f[
77 k^2 = \frac{\rho_f (\omega {\cal L})^2}{B},
78 \f]
79 represents the ratio of the typical
80 inertial fluid pressure induced by the wall oscillation to the
81 `stiffness' of the fluid.
82 
83 \subsection elastic The solid model: the time harmonic equations of linear elasticity
84 We model the coating layer as a linearly elastic solid, described
85 in terms of a displacement field \f$U^*_i(x_j^*,t^*)\f$, with stress tensor
86 \f[
87 \tau_{ij}^* = E \left[ \frac{\nu}{(1+\nu)(1-2\nu)} \, \frac{\partial
88  U_k^*}{\partial x_k^*} \, \delta_{ij}
89 +\frac{1}{2(1+\nu)}\left(
90 \frac{\partial U_i^*}{\partial x_j^*}+
91 \frac{\partial U_j^*}{\partial x_i^*}
92 \right) \right],
93 \f]
94 where \f$E\f$ and \f$\nu\f$ are the material's Young's modulus and Poisson's
95 ratio, respectively.
96 As before, we assume a time-harmonic solution with frequency
97 \f$\omega\f$ so that
98 \f$U_i^*(x_j^*,t^*) = {\rm Re} \{ u_i^*(x_j^*) \exp(-{\rm i}\omega
99 t^*)\}\f$,
100 and we non-dimensionalise the displacements on \f${\cal L}\f$ and the
101 stress on Young's modulus, \f$E\f$, so that \f$u_j^* = {\cal L} u_j\f$ and
102 \f$\tau_{ij}^* = E \tau_{ij}\f$. The deformation of the elastic coating
103 is then governed by the time-harmonic Navier-Lame equations
104 \f[
105 \frac{\partial \tau_{ij}}{\partial x_j} + \Omega^2 u_i = 0,
106 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (2)
107 \f]
108 which depend (implicitly) on Poisson's ratio \f$\nu\f$, and on
109 the (square of the) non-dimensional wavenumber
110 \f[
111 \Omega^2 = \frac{\rho_{\rm s} (\omega {\cal L})^2}{E},
112 \f]
113 where \f$ \rho_{\rm s} \f$ is the solid density.
114 The parameter \f$ \Omega^2 \f$ represents the ratio of the typical
115 inertial solid pressure induced by the wall oscillation to the
116 stiffness of the elastic coating. We note that for a `light'
117 coating we have \f$\Omega \ll 1\f$.
118 
119 
120 \subsection bc Boundary conditions
121 The inner surface of the elastic coating, \f$ \partial D_{\rm s} \f$,
122 is subject to the prescribed
123 displacement imposed by the oscillating cylinder. For instance, if the inner
124 cylinder performs axisymmetric oscillations of non-dimensional
125 amplitude \f$ \epsilon\f$, we have
126 \f[
127 \mbox{\bf u} = \epsilon \, \mbox{\bf e}_r
128 \mbox{\ \ \ \ \ on $\partial D_{\rm s}$},
129 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (3)
130 \f]
131 where \f$ \mbox{\bf e}_r \f$ is the unit vector in the radial direction.
132 The fluid-loaded surface of the elastic coating, \f$ \partial D_{\rm
133 f} \f$,
134 is subject to the
135 fluid pressure. The non-dimensional traction exerted by the
136 fluid onto the solid (on the solid stress scale) is therefore given by
137 \f[
138 t_i^{\rm [solid]} = \tau_{ij}^{\rm [solid]} n_j = - \phi \ Q \ n_i
139 \mbox{\ \ \ \ \ on $\partial D_{\rm f}$},
140 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (4)
141 \f]
142 where the \f$n_i\f$ are the components of the outer unit normal on the solid
143 boundary \f$\partial D_{\rm f}\f$ and
144 \f[
145 Q=\frac{\rho_{\rm f}({\cal L}\omega)^2}{E}
146 \f]
147 is the final non-dimensional parameter in the problem. It represents the
148 ratio of the typical inertial fluid pressure induced by the wall
149 oscillation to the stiffness of the elastic coating. The parameter \f$Q\f$
150 therefore provides a measure of the strength of the fluid-structure
151 interaction (FSI) in the sense that for \f$Q \to 0\f$
152 the elastic coating does not `feel' the presence of the fluid.
153 
154 The fluid is forced by the normal displacement of the solid. Imposing
155 the non-penetration condition \f$(d_j - u_j) n_j = 0\f$ on \f$\partial
156 D_{\rm f}\f$ yields a Neumann condition for the displacement
157 potential,
158 \f[
159 \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial n} = u_j n_j
160 \mbox{\ \ \ \ \ on $\partial D_{\rm f}$}.
161 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (5)
162 \f]
163 Finally, the displacement potential for the fluid must satisfy the
164 Sommerfeld radiation condition
165 \f[
166 \lim_{r \to \infty} \sqrt{r}\left( \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial r} -
167  {\rm i} k \phi \right) = 0
168 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (6)
169 \f]
170 which ensures that the oscillating cylinder does not generate any incoming
171 waves.

176 \section impl Implementation
177 The implementation of the coupled problem follows the usual procedure
178 for multi-domain problems in \c oomph-lib. We discretise the
179 constituent single-physics problems using the existing single-physics
180 elements, here \c oomph-lib's
181 - <a href="../../../helmholtz/scattering/html/index.html">Helmholtz
182 elements</a>
183 .
184 and
185 - <a href="../../../time_harmonic_linear_elasticity/elastic_annulus/html/index.html">
186  time-harmonic linear elasticity elements</a>
187 .
188 for the discretisation of the PDEs (1) and
189 (2), respectively.
190 The displacement boundary condition (3) on the inner surface
191 of the elastic coating is imposed as usual by pinning
192 the relevant degrees of freedom, exactly as in a <a href="../../../time_harmonic_linear_elasticity/elastic_annulus/html/index.html">single-physics
193 solid mechanics problem</a>. Similarly, the
194 Sommerfeld radiation condition (6) on the outer boundary
195 of the fluid domain can be imposed by any of the methods
196 available for the solution of the single-physics
197 Helmholtz equation, such as
198 <a href="../../../helmholtz/scattering/html/index.html#ABCs">
199  approximate/absorbing boundary conditions (ABCs)</a>
200 or a <a href="../../../helmholtz/scattering/html/index.html#DtN">
201 Dirichlet-to-Neumann mapping.</a>
202 
203 The boundary conditions (4) and
204 (5) at the fluid-solid interface are
205 traction boundary conditions for the solid, and Neumann boundary
206 conditions for the Helmholtz equation, respectively. In a single-physics problem
207 we would impose such boundary conditions by attaching suitable \c FaceElements
208 to the appropriate boundaries of the "bulk" elements, as shown in the
209 sketch below: \c TimeHarmonicLinearElasticityTractionElements could be used to
210 impose a (given) traction, \f$ {\bf t}_0\f$, onto the solid;
211 \c HelmholtzFluxElements could be used to impose a (given) normal derivative,
212 \f$ f_0\f$, on the displacement potential. Both \f$ {\bf t}_0\f$
213 and \f$ f_0 \f$ would usually be specified in a user-defined namespace
214 and accessed via function pointers as indicated in the right half of
215 the sketch.
216 
217 \image html single_physics.gif "Sketch illustrating the imposition of flux and traction boundary conditions in single-physics problems. The continuous problems are shown on the left; the discretised ones on the right. "
218 \image latex single_physics.eps "Sketch illustrating the imposition of flux and traction boundary conditions in single-physics problems. The continuous problems are shown on the left; the discretised ones on the right. " width=0.6\textwidth
219 
220 
221 In the coupled problem, illustrated in the left half of the
222 next sketch, the traction acting on the solid becomes a function
223 of the displacement potential via the boundary condition
224 (4), while the normal derivative of the
225 displacement potential is given in terms of the solid displacement via
226 equation (5). Note that corresponding points on the
227 FSI boundary \f$ \partial D_{\rm f}\f$ are
228 identified by matching values of the boundary
229 coordinate \f$ \zeta \f$ which is assumed to be consistent between
230 the two domains.
231 
232 The implementation of this interaction in the discretised problem
233 is illustrated in the right half of the sketch: We replace the single-physics
234 \c HelmholtzFluxElements by \c HelmholtzFluxFromNormalDisplacementBCElements,
235 and the \c TimeHarmonicLinearElasticityTractionElements by
236 \c TimeHarmonicLinElastLoadedByHelmholtzPressureBCElements.
237 (Yes, we like to be verbose...).
238 Both of these \c FaceElements are derived from the \c ElementWithExternalElement
239 base class and can therefore store a pointer to an "external"
240 element that provides the information required to impose the
241 appropriate boundary condition. Thus, the \c
242 HelmholtzFluxFromNormalDisplacementBCElements store pointers to
243 the "adjacent" time-harmonic linear elasticity elements (from which
244 they obtain the boundary displacement required for the imposition of
245 (5)),
246 while the \c TimeHarmonicLinElastLoadedByHelmholtzPressureBCElements
247 store pointers to the "adjacent" Helmholtz elements that provide the
248 value of the displacement potential required for the evaluation of
249 (4).

255 The identification of the "adjacent" bulk elements can be performed using the

611 \subsection comm Comments
612 
613 - This tutorial emerged from an actual research project
614  in which we investigated how efficiently the acoustic power radiated
615  from an oscillating cylinder is reduced when the cylinder
616  is coated with an elastic layer, exactly as in the model problem
617  considered here. The paper then went on to investigate the
618  effect of gaps in the coating and discovered some (rather nice)
619  quasi-resonances -- values of the FSI parameter \f$ Q \f$ at which
620  the radiated acoustic power increases significantly. Read all
621  about it in this paper:
622  \n\n
623  - Heil, M., Kharrat, T., Cotterill, P.A. & Abrahams, I.D. (2012)
624  Quasi-resonances in sound-insulating coatings.
625  <EM>Journal of Sound and Vibration</EM>
626  <b>331</B> 4774-4784. DOI:
627  <A HREF="http://dx.doi.org/10.1016/j.jsv.2012.05.029">

635 \subsection ex Exercises
636 - Equation (8) for the time-averaged radiated power
637  shows that \f$ \overline{\cal P}\f$ depends
638  on the derivatives of the displacement potential \f$ \phi \f$. This
639  implies that the value for \f$ \overline{\cal P}\f$ computed from the
640  finite-element solution for \f$ \phi \f$ is not
641  as accurate as the displacement potential itself. Computing
642  \f$ \overline{\cal P}\f$ to a certain tolerance (e.g. to
643  "graphical accuracy" as in the plot shown above) therefore tends to
644  require meshes that are much finer than would be required
645  if we were only interested in \f$ \phi \f$ itself.
646  \n\n
647  Investigate the accuracy of the computational predictions for \f$
648  \overline{\cal P}\f$ by:
649  \n\n
650  - increasing the spatial resolution e.g. by using
651  the command line flag \c --el_multiplier (which controls the number
652  of elements in the mesh) and suppressing any automatic (un)refinement
653  by setting the maximum number of adaptations to zero
654  using the \c --max_adapt command line flag.
655  \n\n
656  - reducing the outer radius of the computational domain, using
657  the command line flag \c --outer_radius, say.
658  \n\n
659  - varying the element type, from the bi-linear
660  \c RefineableQHelmholtzElement<2,2>
661  to the bi-cubic \c RefineableQHelmholtzElement<2,4>, say.
662  .
663  \n\n
664  Which of these approaches gives you the "most accuracy" for a given number of
665  degrees of freedom?
666 .
