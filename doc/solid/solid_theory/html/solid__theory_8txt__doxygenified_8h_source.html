42 \subsection solids_lagrange Solid mechanics problems -- Lagrangian coordinates
43 All problems discussed so far were formulated in an Eulerian frame,
44 i.e. we assumed all unknowns to be functions of the spatially-fixed,
45 Eulerian coordinates, \f$x_i \ (i=1,...,3)\f$, and of time,
46 \f$ t \f$. Many problems
47 in solid mechanics are formulated more conveniently in terms of
48 body-attached, Lagrangian coordinates. In this section we will
49 briefly review the essential concepts of nonlinear continuum mechanics
50 and present the principle of virtual displacements which forms the
51 basis for all large-displacement solid mechanics computations in \c oomph-lib.
52 
53 Throughout this section we will use the summation convention
54 that repeated indices are to be summed over the range
55 of the three spatial coordinates and all free indices range
56 from 1 to 3. We will retain the summation signs
57 for all other sums, such as sums over the nodes etc.

62 \subsection geometry The geometry
63 The figure below introduces the essential geometrical concepts:
64 We employ a set of Lagrangian coordinates, \f$ \xi^i\f$, to
65 parametrise the (Eulerian) position vector to material points in the
66  body's undeformed position:
67 \f[
68 {\bf r} = {\bf r}(\xi^i).
69 \f]
70 The specific choice of the Lagrangian coordinates is
71 irrelevant for the subsequent development. For analytical studies, it is
72 advantageous to choose a body-fitted Lagrangian coordinate
73 system (as shown in the sketch) because this allows boundary
74 conditions to be applied on iso-levels of the coordinates;
75 in computational studies, it is usually preferable to use a
76 coordinate system in which the governing equations are as compact
77 as possible. A Cartesian coordinate system is best suited for this
78 purpose.
79 
80 We denote the tangent vectors to the coordinate lines \f$ \xi^i = const.\f$
81 in the undeformed configuration by
82 \f[
83 {\bf g}_i = \frac{\partial {\bf r}}{\partial \xi^i},
84 \f]
85 and define the components of the covariant metric tensor via
86 the inner products
87 \f[
88 g_{ij} = {\bf g}_i \cdot {\bf g}_j.
89 \f]
90 This tensor defines the "metric" because the (square of the) infinitesimal
91 length, \f$ ds \f$, of a line element with coordinate increments
92 \f$ d\xi^i\f$ is given by
93 \f[
94 (ds)^2 = g_{ij} \ d\xi^i d\xi^j.
95 \f]
96 The volume of the infinitesimal parallelepiped formed by
97 the coordinate increments \f$ d\xi^i\f$ is given by
98 \f[
99 dv = \sqrt{g} \ d\xi^1 d\xi^2 d\xi^3,
100 \f]
101 where
102 \f[
103 g = \det g_{ij}.
104 \f]
105 \image html Lagrangian_coord.gif "2D sketch of the Lagrangian coordinates. The Lagrangian coordinates parametrise the position vector to material points in the body. As the body deforms, the Lagrangian coordinates remain attached to the same material points and the coordinate lines become distorted. The change in the length of infinitesimal material line elements provides an objective measure of the body's deformation. "
106 \image latex Lagrangian_coord.eps "2D sketch of the Lagrangian coordinates. The Lagrangian coordinates parametrise the position vector to material points in the body. As the body deforms, the Lagrangian coordinates remain attached to the same material points and the coordinate lines become distorted. The change in the length of infinitesimal material line elements provides an objective measure of the body's deformation. " width=0.75\textwidth
107 
108 As the body deforms, the Lagrangian coordinates remain "attached" to the
109 same material points. The body's deformation can therefore be
110 described by the vector field that specifies the position vectors
111 to material particles in the deformed configuration,
112 \f[
113 {\bf R} = {\bf R}(\xi^i).
114 \f]
115 As in the undeformed coordinate system, we form the
116 tangent vectors to the deformed coordinate lines \f$ \xi^i = const.\f$
117 and denote them by
118 \f[
119 {\bf G}_i = \frac{\partial {\bf R}}{\partial \xi^i}.
120 \f]
121 The inner product of these vectors defines the metric tensor in
122 the deformed configuration
123 \f[
124 G_{ij} = {\bf G}_i \cdot {\bf G}_j
125  = \frac{\partial {\bf R}}{\partial \xi^i} \cdot
126  \frac{\partial {\bf R}}{\partial \xi^j},
127 \f]
128 and we have equivalent relations for the lengths of line elements,
129 \f[
130 (dS)^2 = G_{ij} \ d\xi^i d\xi^j,
131 \f]
132 and the volume of infinitesimal parallelepipeds,
133 \f[
134 dV = \sqrt{G} \ d\xi^1 d\xi^2 d\xi^3,
135 \f]
136 where
137 \f[
138 G = \det G_{ij}.
139 \f]
140 Since the metric tensors \f$ G_{ij }\f$ and \f$ g_{ij }\f$ provide
141 a measure of the length of
142 material line elements in the deformed and undeformed configurations,
143 respectively, their difference
144 \f[
145 \gamma_{ij} = \frac{1}{2} (G_{ij} - g_{ij}),
146 \f]
147 provides an objective measure of the strain and is known as
148 the Green strain tensor.

152 \subsection equilibrium Equilibrium and the Principle of Virtual Displacements
153 
154 Let us now assume that the body is subjected to
155 - an applied surface traction \f$ {\bf T} \f$ --
156  a force per unit deformed surface area, applied on (part of) the
157  body's deformed surface area \f$ A_{tract} \subset \partial V\f$,
158 - a body force \f$ {\bf f} \f$ --
159  a force per unit volume of the undeformed (!) body [This can
160  easily be expressed in terms of a force per unit deformed volume,
161  \f$ {\bf F }\f$, by invoking conservation of mass, which shows that
162  \f$ {\bf f } = \sqrt{G/g} \ {\bf F }\f$.],
163 .
164 and its displacements are prescribed on the remaining part of
165 the boundary, \f$ A_{displ} \subset \partial V \f$ (where
166 \f$ A_{tract} \cap A_{displ}=0 \f$ and \f$A_{tract} \cup A_{displ}
167 = \partial V\f$ ), <EM> i.e. </EM>
168 \f[
169 {\bf R}(\xi^k) = {\bf R}^{(BC)}(\xi^k)
170 \mbox{ \ \ \ \ on \ \ \ $ A_{displ},$ }
171  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1)
172 \f]
173 for given \f$ {\bf R}^{(BC)}(\xi^k) \f$.
174 
175 \image html Solid_boundary_conds.gif "Sketch of the boundary conditions: The surface of the body is either subject to a prescribed traction or its displacement is prescribed. "
176 \image latex Solid_boundary_conds.eps "Sketch of the boundary conditions: The surface of the body is either subject to a prescribed traction or its displacement is prescribed. " width=0.75\textwidth
177 
178 The deformation is governed by the principle of virtual displacements
179 \f[
180 \int \left\{ \sigma^{ij} \ \delta \gamma_{ij} - \left( {\bf f} -
181  \rho \frac{\partial^2 {\bf R}}{\partial t^2} \right) \cdot
182  \delta {\bf R} \right\} \ dv -
183  \oint_{A_{tract}} {\bf T} \cdot \delta {\bf R} \ dA =0,
184  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (2)
185  \f]
186  where \f$ \sigma^{ij} = \sigma^{ji}\f$ is the (symmetric)
187  second Piola-Kirchhoff stress tensor, \f$ \rho \f$ is the density of
188  the undeformed body, and
189  \f$ \delta (.) \f$ represents the variation of \f$ (.)\f$.
190  See, e.g., Green, A.E. \& Zerna, W. "Theoretical Elasticity",
191  Dover (1992); or Wempner, G. "Mechanics of Solids with Applications
192  to Thin Bodies", Kluwer (1982) for more details.
193 
194  Upon choosing the particles' position vector in the deformed
195  configuration, \f${\bf R}(\xi^j)\f$, as the unknown, the variation
196  of the strain tensor becomes
197  \f[
198  \delta \gamma_{ij} = \frac{1}{2} \left(
199  \frac{\partial {\bf R}}{\partial \xi^i} \cdot
200  \delta \ \frac{\partial {\bf R}}{\partial \xi^j} +
201  \frac{\partial {\bf R}}{\partial \xi^j} \cdot
202  \delta \ \frac{\partial {\bf R}}{\partial \xi^i}
203  \right),
204  \f]
205  and we have
206  \f[
207  \delta {\bf R} = {\bf 0} \mbox{ \ \ \ \ on \ \ \ $ A_{displ}.$ }
208  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (3)
209  \f]
210  The 2nd Piola Kirchhoff stress tensor is symmetric, therefore
211  we can write the variation of the strain energy in
212  (2) as
213  \f[
214  \int \sigma^{ij} \ \delta \gamma_{ij} \ dv =
215  \int \sigma^{ij} \ \frac{\partial {\bf R}}{\partial \xi^i} \cdot
216  \delta \ \frac{\partial {\bf R}}{\partial \xi^j} \ dv
217  \f]
218  and obtain the displacement form of the principle of
219  virtual displacements:
220  \f[
221  \int \left\{ \sigma^{ij} \ \frac{\partial {\bf R}}{\partial \xi^i} \cdot
222  \delta \ \frac{\partial {\bf R}}{\partial \xi^j}
223  - \left( {\bf f} -
224  \rho \frac{\partial^2 {\bf R}}{\partial t^2} \right) \cdot
225  \delta {\bf R} \right\} \ dv
226  - \oint_{A_{tract}} {\bf T} \cdot \delta {\bf R} \ dA =0.
227  \f]
228 This must be augmented by a constitutive equation which
229 determines the stress as a function of the body's deformation,
230 (and possibly the history of its deformation). Here we will only
231 consider elastic behaviour, where the stress is only a function of the
232 strain.

236 \subsection elastic_constitutive Constitutive Equations for Purely Elastic Behaviour
237 For purely
238 elastic behaviour, the stress is only a function of the
239 instantaneous, local strain and the constitutive equation
240 has the form
241 \f[
242 \sigma^{ij} = \sigma^{ij} (\gamma_{kl}).
243 \f]
244 
245 The functional form of the constitutive equation is different
246 for compressible/incompressible/near-incompressible behaviour:
247 -# \b Compressible \b Behaviour: \n If the material is compressible,
248  the stress can be computed directly from the deformed and undeformed
249  metric tensors,
250  \f[
251  \sigma^{ij} = \sigma^{ij}(\gamma_{kl}) =
252  \sigma^{ij}\bigg( \frac{1}{2} (G_{kl} - g_{kl})\bigg).
253  \f]
254 -# \b Incompressible \b Behaviour: \n If the material is incompressible,
255  its deformation is constrained by the condition that
256  \f[
257  \det G_{ij} - \det g_{ij}= 0,
258  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (4)
259  \f]
260  which ensures that the volume of infinitesimal material
261  elements remains constant during the deformation. This
262  condition is typically enforced by a Lagrange multiplier which
263  plays the role of a pressure. In such cases, the
264  stress tensor has the form
265  \f[
266  \sigma^{ij} = -p G^{ij} +
267  \overline{\sigma}^{ij}\big(\gamma_{kl}\big),
268  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (5)
269  \f]
270  where only the deviatoric part of the stress tensor,
271  \f$ \overline{\sigma}^{ij}, \f$ depends directly on the
272  strain. The pressure \f$ p \f$ must be determined
273  independently by enforcing the incompressibility constraint
274  (4).
275  Given the deformed and undeformed metric tensors,
276  the computation of the stress tensor \f$ \sigma^{ij} \f$
277  for an incompressible
278  material therefore requires the computation of the following quantities:
279  - The deviatoric stress \f$ \overline{\sigma}^{ij} \f$
280  - The contravariant deformed metric tensor \f$ G^{ij} \f$
281  - The determinant of the deformed
282  metric tensor \f$ \det G_{ij} \f$ which
283  is required in equation (4)
284  whose solution determines the pressure.
285  .
286 -# \b Nearly \b Incompressible \b Behaviour: \n If the material is nearly
287  incompressible, it is advantageous to split the stress into
288  its deviatoric and hydrostatic parts by writing the
289  constitutive law in the form
290  \f[
291  \sigma^{ij} = -p G^{ij} +
292  \overline{\sigma}^{ij}\big(\gamma_{kl}\big),
293  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (6)
294  \f]
295  where the deviatoric part of the stress tensor,
296  \f$ \overline{\sigma}^{ij}, \f$ depends on the
297  strain. This form of the constitutive
298  law is identical to that of the incompressible
299  case and it involves a pressure \f$ p \f$ which must be
300  determined from an additional equation. In the
301  incompressible case, this equation was given by the incompressibility
302  constraint (4). Here, we must augment
303  the constitutive law for the deviatoric stress by
304  an additional equation for the pressure. Generally this takes the
305  form
306  \f[
307  p = - \kappa \ d,
308  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (7)
309  \f]
310  where \f$ \kappa \f$ is the "bulk modulus", a material property
311  that must be specified by the constitutive law.
312  \f$ d \f$ is the (generalised) dilatation, i.e. the relative change
313  in the volume of an infinitesimal material element (or some
314  suitable generalised quantity that is related to it). As the
315  material approaches incompressibility, \f$ \kappa \to \infty\f$, so that
316  infinitely large pressures would be required to achieve
317  any change in volume. To facilitate the implementation of
318  (7) as the equation for the pressure, we re-write
319  it in the form
320  \f[
321  p \ \frac{1}{\kappa} + d\big(g_{ij},G_{ij}\big) = 0,
322  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (8)
323  \f]
324  which only involves quantities that remain finite
325  as we approach true incompressibility.
326  \n
327  Given the deformed and undeformed metric tensors,
328  the computation of the stress tensor \f$ \sigma^{ij} \f$
329  for a nearly incompressible
330  material therefore requires the computation of the following quantities:
331  - The deviatoric stress \f$ \overline{\sigma}^{ij} \f$
332  - The contravariant deformed metric tensor \f$ G^{ij} \f$
333  - The generalised dilatation \f$ d \f$
334  - The inverse of the bulk modulus \f$ \kappa \f$
335  .
336 
337 The abstract base class \c ConstitutiveLaw provides
338 interfaces for the computation of the stress in all three forms.

340 \subsubsection strain-energy Strain-energy functions
341 
342 A hyperelastic material is one for which the stress can be derived
343 from a potential function \f$ W(\gamma_{ij})\f$,
344 known as the strain-energy function, and
345 \f[
346  \sigma^{ij} = \frac{\partial W}{\partial
347  \gamma_{ij}}.
348 \f]
349  A strain-energy function exists if the elastic deformations are
350 reversible and isothermal, or reversible and isentropic. If the
351 material is homogeneous and isotropic then the strain-energy function
352 depends only on the three strain invariants:
353 \f[
354  I_{1} = g^{ij} G_{ij},\quad I_{2} = G^{ij} g_{ij} I_{3},\quad
355  I_{3} = G/g
356 \f]
357 and can be written \f$W(I_{1},I_{2},I_{3})\f$. It may be shown, see
358 Green \& Zerna, that
359 \f[ \sigma^{ij} = \Phi g^{ij} + \Psi B^{ij} + p G^{ij},\f]
360 where
361 \f[ \Phi = 2\, \frac{\partial W}{\partial
362 I_{1}},\quad
363  \Psi = 2\, \frac{\partial W}{\partial I_{2}},\quad
364  p = 2 I_{3} \frac{\partial W}{\partial I_{3}} \quad\mbox{and}\quad
365 B^{ij} = I_{1} g^{ij} - g^{ir}g^{js}G_{rs}
366 \f]
367 
368  The abstract base class \c StrainEnergyFunction provides the interfaces \f$
369  W(\gamma_{ij}) \f$ and \f$ W(I_{1},I_{2},I_{3}) \f$ and should be
370  used as the base class for all strain-energy functions.
371  A class \c
372  StrainEnergyFunctionConstitutiveLaw that inherits from \c
373  ConstitutiveLaw uses a specified strain-energy function
374  to compute the appropriate stresses.

378 \subsection non-dim_solid Non-dimensionalisation
379 The principle of virtual displacements (2)
380 is written in dimensional form.
381 We generally prefer to work with non-dimensional quantities and
382 will now discuss the non-dimensionalisation used in the
383 actual implementation of the equations in \c oomph-lib. For this purpose we
384 first re-write equation (2) as
385 \f[
386 \int_{v} \left\{ \sigma^{*ij} \ \delta \gamma_{ij} - \left( {\bf f}^* -
387  \rho \frac{\partial^2 {\bf R}^*}{\partial t^{*2}} \right) \cdot
388  \delta {\bf R}^* \right\} \ dv^*
389  - \oint_{A_{tract}} {\bf T}^* \cdot \delta {\bf R}^* \ dA^* =0,
390  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (9)
391  \f]
392 where we have used asterisks to label those
393 dimensional quantities that will have non-dimensional equivalents.
394 (Some quantities, such as the strain, are already
395 dimensionless, while others, such as the density will not have
396 any non-dimensional counterparts. We do not introduce modifiers
397 for these).
398 
399 We now non-dimensionalise all lengths with a problem-specific
400 length-scale, \f$ {\cal L}\f$, given e.g. the length of the solid
401 body, so that
402 \f[
403 \xi^{*i} = {\cal L} \ \xi^{i}, \ \ \ {\bf R}^* = {\cal L} \ {\bf R},
404 \ \ \ dA^* = {\cal L}^2 \ dA, \mbox{ \ \ and \ \ }
405 dv^* = {\cal L}^3 \ dv.\ \ \
406 \f]
407 We use a characteristic stiffness, \f$ {\cal S}\f$, (e.g. the
408 material's Young's modulus \f$ E \f$) to non-dimensionalise
409 the stress and the loads as
410 \f[
411 \sigma^{*ij} = {\cal S} \ \sigma^{ij}, \ \ \ {\bf T}^* = {\cal S}\
412 {\bf T}, \mbox{ \ \ and \ \ } {\bf f}^* = {\cal S} / {\cal L} \
413 {\bf f},
414 \f]
415 and we non-dimensionalise time with a problem-specific
416 timescale \f$ {\cal T}\f$ (e.g. the period of some external forcing),
417 so that
418 \f[
419 t^* = {\cal T} t.
420 \f]
421 This transforms (9) into
422 \f[
423 \int_{v} \left\{ \sigma^{ij} \ \delta \gamma_{ij} - \left( {\bf f} -
424  \Lambda^2 \frac{\partial^2 {\bf R}}{\partial t^{2}} \right) \cdot
425  \delta {\bf R} \right\} \ dv
426  -\oint_{A_{tract}} {\bf T} \cdot \delta {\bf R} \ dA =0,
427  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (10)
428  \f]
429  where
430 \f[
431 \Lambda = \frac{{\cal L}}{\cal T} \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{\cal S}}
432 \f]
433 is the ratio of system's "intrinsic" timescale,
434 \f$T_{intrinsic} = {\cal L} \sqrt{\rho /{\cal S}},\f$ to the
435 time, \f${\cal T}\f$, used in the non-dimensionalisation of the
436 equations. If a given problem has no externally imposed timescale
437 (e.g. in the free vibrations of an elastic body) \f$T_{intrinsic}\f$
438 (or some suitable problem-dependent multiple thereof) provides a natural
439 timescale for the non-dimensionalisation. Therefore we use
440 \f$ \Lambda=1 \f$ as the default value in all solid mechanics
441 equations. If preferred, computations can, of course, be
442 performed with dimensional units, provided all quantities
443 are expressed in consistent units (e.g. from the SI system).
444 In this case \f$ \Lambda^2 \f$ represents the dimensional
445 density of the material.
446 
447 We adopt a similar approach for non-dimensionalisation of the
448 constitutive equations. Typically, the constitutive parameters
449 (e.g. Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio for a Hookean
450 constitutive equation) are passed to the \c ConstitutiveLaw
451 as arguments to its constructor. Where possible, we select one of these
452 parameters as the reference stress \f$ {\cal S} \f$ and give it
453 a default value of 1.0. Hence, if a Hookean constitutive
454 law is instantiated with just one argument (the Poisson ratio \f$
455 \nu \f$), the stress is assumed to have been scaled on Young's
456 modulus. If two arguments are provided, the second argument
457 should be interpreted as the ratio of
458 the material's Young's modulus to the reference stress
459 \f$ {\cal S} \f$ used in the non-dimensionalisation of the equations.

463 \subsection two_d_solid 2D problems: Plane strain.
464 Many solid mechanics problems can be regarded as two-dimensional
465 in the sense that the quantities of interest only depend on two
466 spatial coordinates. In such problems it is important to consider what
467 constraints the system is subjected to in the third direction --- clearly
468 all real solid bodies are three-dimensional!
469 In plane strain problems, the displacements of material points are assumed
470 to be parallel to the 2D plane spanned by the coordinates \f$x_1
471 \f$ and \f$ x_2\f$, so that any displacements
472 normal to this plane are suppressed. In this case, significant
473 transverse stresses can develop.
474 Conversely, in plane stress problems, it is assumed that no stresses
475 develop in the transverse direction; in this case we must
476 allow material particles to be displaced transversely.
477 Since we have formulated our problem in terms of positions
478 (i.e. in terms of the displacement of material points), our formulation
479 naturally produces a <B>plane strain</B> problem if we reduce
480 the equations to two dimensions:
481 We assume that the transverse displacement vanishes,
482 while the remaining (in-plane) displacements are only
483 functions of the in-plane coordinates, \f$ x_1\f$ and \f$ x_2\f$.
484 
485 It is important to remember that the 2D version of all equations must
486 produce plane-strain behaviour when new, strain-energy-based
487 constitutive equation classes are formulated;
488 when implementing such strain-energy functions, recall that any
489 invariants of metric tensors etc. are the invariants of the full
490 3D quantities which are not necessarily the same as those
491 of the corresponding 2D quantities.

497 \subsection isotropic_growth Isotropic growth.
498 Many biological tissues undergo growth processes. For instance, the cells
499 that make up a solid tumour divide regularly and as a result the
500 total mass of the tumour increases. If the growth
501 occurs non-uniformly so that certain parts of the tumour grow faster
502 than others, regions that grow more slowly restrain the expansion
503 of their neighbours. This process can induce significant growth stresses.
504 The scenario is similar (but not identical) to that of thermal
505 growth in which a non-uniform temperature distribution in
506 a body creates thermal stresses. (The important difference between
507 these two cases is that in the latter process mass is conserved --
508 thermal expansion only leads to an increase in the volume occupied
509 by the material, whereas biological growth via cell division
510 increases the mass of the tumour).
511

513 theoretical framework. The general idea is sketched in the

628 We start our analysis with the stress-free (and
629 "ungrown") reference configuration "0" at the top of the diagram,
630 and initially follow the deformation shown in the left half
631 of the sketch. In a first step, each infinitesimal material
632 element in the body is subjected to the same isotropic growth
633 which changes its mass from \f$\mbox{d}M\f$ to
634 \f$ \Gamma \ \mbox{d}M \f$. Assuming that the growth process does not
635 change the density of the material, \f$ \Gamma\f$ also specifies
636 the volumetric growth of the material. All material
637 elements grow by the same amount, therefore the individual elements
638 can be (re-)assembled to form a continuous body (state
639 U2). In this state, the body is
640 stress-free and, compared to the reference configuration "0",
641 all lengths have increased by a factor of \f$\Gamma^{1/d}\f$
642 where \f$ d \f$ is the body's spatial dimension (i.e. \f$d=2\f$ in
643 the sketch above). The covariant basis vectors in this
644 uniformly-grown, stress-free configuration are therefore given by
645 \f[
646 \widetilde{\bf g}_i = \Gamma^{1/d} \ {\bf g}_i,
647 \f]
648 the metric tensor is given by
649 \f[
650 \widetilde{g_{ij}} = \Gamma^{2/d} \ g_{ij},
651 \f]
652 and the volume of an infinitesimal material element
653 has increased from
654 \f[
655 dv = \sqrt{g} \ d\xi^1 ... \ d\xi^D
656 \f]
657 to
658 \f[
659 \widetilde{dv} = \Gamma \sqrt{g} \ d\xi^1 ... \ d\xi^D
660 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (11)
661 \f]
662 We now subject the stress-free, uniformly-grown body to
663 external loads and determine its deformation, using the principle
664 of virtual displacements. Since the uniformly grown state "U2"
665 is stress-free, we may regard it as the reference state for
666 the subsequent deformation. The strain tensor that describes
667 the deformation from the stress-free (and uniformly-grown) state "U2"
668 to the final equilibrium configuration "UE" must therefore
669 be defined as
670 \f[
671 \gamma_{ij} = \frac{1}{2} \left(G_{ij} - \widetilde{g_{ij}}\right)
672  = \frac{1}{2} \left(G_{ij} - \Gamma^{2/D} \ g_{ij} \right)
673 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (12)
674 \f]
675 Equations (11) and (12) allow
676 us to express the principle of virtual displacements
677 in terms of
678 - the metric of the undeformed (and un-grown) reference
679  state "0",
680 - the volumetric growth \f$ \Gamma \f$, and
681 - quantities associated with the final deformed configuration
682  "UE":
683 .
684 \f[
685 \int_{v} \left\{ \sigma^{ij} \ \delta \gamma_{ij} - \left( {\bf f} -
686  \Lambda^2 \frac{\partial^2 {\bf R}}{\partial t^{2}} \right) \cdot
687  \delta {\bf R} \right\} \ \Gamma \ dv
688  -\oint_{A_{tract}} {\bf T} \cdot \delta {\bf R} \ dA =0,
689  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (13)
690 \f]
691 Note that this equation does not contain any references to quantities
692 in the intermediate states "U1" and "U2".
693 
694 We will now consider the case of spatially non-uniform growth,
695 illustrated in the right half of the sketch. If the isotropic
696 growth is spatially non-uniform, \f$ \Gamma = \Gamma(\xi^i)\f$,
697 the growth will try to expand all infinitesimal material
698 elements isotropically -- but each one by a different amount as
699 illustrated by the hypothetical state N1 in which each material
700 element has expanded to its stress-free state in which its
701 metric tensor is given by
702 \f[
703 \widetilde{g_{ij}} = \Gamma(\xi^k) \ g_{ij}.
704 \f]
705 Material elements will only be stress-free if the strain
706 \f[
707 \gamma_{ij} = \frac{1}{2} \left(G_{ij} - \widetilde{g_{ij}}\right)
708  = \frac{1}{2} \left(G_{ij} - \Gamma^{2/D}(\xi^k) \ g_{ij} \right)
709 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (14)
710 \f]
711 relative to their isotropically grown shape in state N1 is zero.
712 In general, the displacements induced by such an isotropic
713 expansion will be
714 incompatible and it would be impossible to (re-)assemble the
715 individually grown material elements to a continuous body
716 unless the material elements undergo some deformation.
717 The elements' deformation relative to their
718 stress-free shape in N1 will generate internal "growth-stresses"
719 (stage N2). When subjected to external loads and body forces
720 the body will undergo further deformations until the
721 the stress (generated by the particles' total deformation relative to
722 their stress-free state N1) balances the applied loads.
723 
724 It is important to realise that, as in the case of spatially
725 uniform growth, the strain defined by (12) is
726 an intrinsic quantity that provides a measure of each particles'
727 \e local deformation relative to its stress-free shape in N1.
728 The intermediate (and in the current case hypothetical), isotropically
729 grown state N1 does not appear in the analysis -- it only serves
730 to define the stress-free shape for each infinitesimal
731 material element. Equation (13) therefore
732 remains valid.

737 \subsection CartesianLagrangian Specialisation to a Cartesian basis and finite element discretisation
738 If the problem does not have any
739 symmetries (e.g. axisymmetry) whose exploitation would reduce
740 the spatial dimension of the problem, the most compact form
741 of the equations is obtained by resolving all vectors into
742 a fixed Cartesian basis so that the undeformed position vector
743 is given by
744 \f[
745 {\bf r}(\xi^j) = r_i(\xi^j) \ {\bf e}_i,
746  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (15)
747 \f]
748 where the \f$ {\bf e}_i \f$ are the basis vectors in the direction of
749 the Cartesian Eulerian coordinate axes.
750 
751 Similarly, we write
752 \f[
753 {\bf f} = f_i \ {\bf e}_i,
754 \f]
755 \f[
756 {\bf T} = T_i \ {\bf e}_i,
757 \f]
758 and
759 \f[
760 {\bf R}^{(BC)} = R^{(BC)}_i \ {\bf e}_i.
761 \f]
762 
763 We use the Eulerian coordinates in the undeformed
764 configuration as the Lagrangian coordinates so that
765 \f[
766 r_i(\xi^j) = \xi^i. \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (16)
767 \f]
768 With this choice, the tangent vectors to the undeformed coordinate lines
769 are the Cartesian basis vectors
770 \f[
771 {\bf g}_i = {\bf e}_i,
772 \f]
773 and the undeformed metric tensor is the Kronecker delta
774 (the unit matrix)
775 \f[
776 g_{ij} = \delta_{ij}.
777 \f]
778 We now expand the (unknown) deformed position vector in the same basis,
779 \f[
780 {\bf R}(\xi^j) = R_i(\xi^j) \ {\bf e}_i,
781 \f]
782 and derive a finite element approximation for this vector field
783 from the principle of virtual displacements. For this purpose we decompose
784 the undeformed body into a number of finite elements, using the
785 standard mesh generation process described previously.
786 This establishes the Eulerian position \f$ X_{ij}^{(0)} \
787 (j=1,...,N) \f$ of the \f$ N \f$
788 nodes in the body's undeformed configuration.
789 Since the Eulerian coordinates of material points in the undeformed
790 configuration coincide with their Lagrangian coordinates (see
791 (16)), a finite-element representation of the
792 Lagrangian coordinates is established by writing
793 \f[
794 \xi^i = \sum_{j=1}^{N} \Xi_{ij} \ \psi_j,
795 \f]
796 where \f$ \Xi_{ij} = X_{ij}^{(0)} \f$ is the \f$i\f$-th
797 Lagrangian coordinate of global node \f$j\f$, and the \f$\psi_j\f$
798 are the global finite-element shape functions. In practice, the
799 \f$\psi_j\f$ are, of course, represented by local shape functions,
800 \f$\psi_j(s_k)\f$, so that the Lagrangian coordinate at local
801 coordinate \f$ s_k \f$ in element \f$ e \f$ is given by
802 \f[
803 \xi^i(s_k) = \sum_{j=1}^{n} \Xi_{i{\cal J}(j,e)} \ \psi_j(s_k)
804 \f]
805 where we use the same notation as in the
806 <A HREF="../../../intro/html/index.html">Introduction to the
807 Finite Element Method</A> document.
808 
809 We employ the same basis functions to represent the components of
810 the unknown vector field \f$ {\bf R}(\xi^j)\f$, by writing
811 \f[
812 R_i(\xi^k) = \sum_{j=1}^{n} X_{ij} \ \psi_j(\xi^k),
813 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (17)
814 \f]
815 and treat the (Eulerian) nodal positions \f$ X_{ij} \f$ as
816 the unknowns. With this discretisation, the
817 variations in \f$ {\bf R}(\xi^j) \f$ correspond to variations
818 in the nodal positions \f$ X_{ij} \f$ so that
819 \f[
820 \delta {\bf R} = \sum_{j=1}^{N} \delta X_{ij} \ \psi_j \ {\bf e}_i
821 \f]
822 \f[
823 \delta \frac{\partial {\bf R}}{\partial \xi^k} =
824 \sum_{j=1}^{N} \delta X_{ij} \
825 \frac{\partial \psi_j}{\partial \xi^k} \ {\bf e}_i,
826 \f]
827 \f[
828 \mbox{ etc. }
829 \f]
830 The principle of virtual displacement (13)
831 therefore becomes
832  \f[\sum_{m=1}^{N}
833  \left\{
834  \int \left[ \sigma^{ij} \
835  \left(\sum_{l=1}^N X_{kl} \frac{\partial \psi_l}{\partial \xi^i} \right)
836  \frac{\partial \psi_m}{\partial \xi^j} \
837  - \left( f_k -
838  \Lambda^2 \left(\sum_{l=1}^N
839  \frac{\partial^2 X_{kl}}{\partial t^2} \psi_l \right) \right)
840  \psi_m \right] \ \Gamma \ dv
841  - \oint_{A_{tract}} T_k \ \psi_m \ dA \right\}
842  \delta X_{km} =0. \ \ \ \ (18)
843  \f]
844 [Note that summation convention enforces the summation over the index
845 \f$ k \f$.]
846 The displacement boundary condition (1)
847 determines the positions of all nodes that are located on the
848 boundary \f$A_{displ}\f$,
849 \f[
850  X_{ij} = R^{(BC)}_i(\Xi_{lj}) \mbox{ \ \ \ if node $j$ is
851  located on $A_{displ},$}
852 \f]
853 and equation (3)
854 requires their variations to vanish,
855 \f[
856  \delta X_{ij} = 0 \mbox{ \ \ \ if node $j$ is located on $A_{displ}.$}
857 \f]
858 The variations of all other nodal positions are arbitrary (and independent
859 of each other), therefore the terms in the curly brackets in
860 (18) must vanish individually. This provides one
861 (discrete) equation for each unknown \f$ X_{km} \f$ ,
862  \f[ f_{km} =
863  \int \left[ \sigma^{ij} \
864  \left(\sum_{k=1}^N X_{kl} \frac{\partial \psi_l}{\partial \xi^i} \right)
865  \frac{\partial \psi_m}{\partial \xi^j} \
866  - \left( f_k -
867  \Lambda^2 \left(\sum_{k=1}^N
868  \frac{\partial^2 X_{kl}}{\partial t^2} \psi_l \right) \right)
869  \psi_m \right] \ \Gamma \ dv \
870  - \oint_{A_{tract}} T_k \ \psi_m \ dA
871  =0. \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (19)
872  \f]
873 These equations can again be assembled in an element-by-element
874 fashion.

881 \section solid_implementation Implementation
882 We will now discuss how the discrete equations (19) are
883 implemented in \c oomph-lib. To facilitate the analysis
884 of multi-physics problems, we introduce generalisations of the \c Node,
885 \c FiniteElement and \c Mesh classes which provide
886 separate storage (and access functions) for all solid mechanics
887 data. The resulting \c SolidFiniteElements can
888 be used as stand-alone elements for the simulation of
889 pure solid mechanics problems. More importantly, however, the design
890 makes it easy to employ multiple inheritance to create
891 more complex elements that solve the equations of solid mechanics
892 together with any other field equations. For instance, if we combine
893 a \c FiniteElement that solves the unsteady heat equation
894 with a \c SolidFiniteElement that describes the elastic
895 deformations, we obtain an element that can be used to simulate
896 unsteady heat conduction in an elastic body that is subject to
897 large-amplitude deformations, say. This is illustrated in one of
898 \c oomph-lib's
899 <A HREF="../../../multi_physics/thermo/html/index.html">multi-physics
900 example codes.</A>

904 \subsection solid_node The SolidNode class
905 
906 The \c SolidNode class is derived from the \c Node class and
907 implements the additional functionality required for solid
908 mechanics problems. The key new feature is that each \c Node
909 must store its (fixed) Lagrangian coordinate \f$ \Xi_{ij}\f$,
910 while its Eulerian position \f$ X_{ij}\f$ must be regarded
911 as an unknown. This requires the following changes to
912 member functions of the \c Node class:
913 
914 - The function \c Node::x(...) returns the
915  Eulerian coordinates of the \c Node. Internally, this function
916  accesses the nodal coordinates via pointers to double
917  precision numbers. In solid mechanics problems we must be able to
918  regard the nodal positions as unknowns. In \c SolidNodes
919  the nodal positions are therefore stored as values of a
920  (member) \c Data object created during construction of the \c
921  SolidNode. (As usual, the values can be either unknown or pinned, and
922  can have time histories). The function
923  \code
924  SolidNode::position_eqn_number(...)
925  \endcode
926  gives access to the global equation number for each
927  nodal coordinate and, following our usual convention, the function
928  returns the static integer \c Data::Is_pinned (which is set to -1) if a
929  coordinate is pinned.
930 - We introduce a new member function
931  \code
932  SolidNode::xi(...)
933  \endcode
934  which returns the (fixed) Lagrangian coordinates of the node.
935 - The function has the usual extensions to generalised
936  coordinates
937  \code
938  SolidNode::xi_gen(...)
939  \endcode
940  which is required for Hermite elements and any other elements
941  that use generalised nodal coordinates to interpolate the
942  element's geometry.
943 - Similar to the \c Data member function \c Data::pin(...) which can
944  be used to pin specific nodal values, we provide the function
945  \code
946  SolidNode:pin_position(...)
947  \endcode
948  which allows pinning of selected nodal coordinates.
949 - Dynamic problems require the evaluation of time-derivatives
950  of the nodal positions, such as \f$ \partial^2 X_{ij}/\partial t^2\f$,
951  see (19). These time-derivatives are evaluated by the
952  \c TimeStepper of the positional \c Data. By default,
953  we use the same \c TimeStepper for the nodal \c Data and for the nodal
954  positions. In multi-physics problems this may not be
955  appropriate, however. Consider, for instance, solving an unsteady
956  heat equation in a dynamically deforming, elastic body. In this problem
957  the 2nd time-derivatives of the nodal position might be evaluated
958  by a \c Newmark scheme, acting on the history values of the nodal
959  positions, whereas the time-derivatives of the temperature
960  might be determined by a \c BDF scheme, operating on the
961  history values of the nodal \c Data.
962  In such cases, the default assignment for the two timesteppers
963  can be overwritten with the access functions
964  \code
965  Node::position_time_stepper_pt()
966  \endcode
967  and
968  \code
969  Node::time_stepper_pt()
970  \endcode
971  where the latter is inherited from \c Data::time_stepper_pt().
972 - Our implementation is based on the displacement form of the
973  principle of virtual displacements in which the position vector
974  \f$ {\bf R}(\xi^i) \f$ in the deformed configuration is regarded
975  as the unknown vector field. Equation (17)
976  defines the representation of this vector
977  field within each finite element in terms of the nodal coordinates.
978  Some constitutive equations require the representation
979  of additional (non-positional) variables.
980  For instance, for incompressible (or nearly incompressible) materials,
981  the stress \f$ \sigma^{ij} \f$ contains a contribution from the
982  (scalar) pressure field \f$ p \f$; see the discussion of the
983  constitutive equations above. If we choose a continuous
984  representation for the pressure in which its value is
985  interpolated between nodal values (as in Taylor-Hood-type
986  elements), the nodal pressure values are stored
987  in the "normal" nodal \c Data. Similarly, the elements'
988  internal \c Data is used to store any discontinuous solid
989  pressures.
990 - Finally, \c SolidNodes overload the function
991  \c Data::assign_eqn_numbers()
992  with
993  \code
994  SolidNode::assign_eqn_numbers()
995  \endcode
996  which creates global equation numbers for all (non-pinned)
997  positional values, and then deals with the "normal" nodal \c Data by calling
998  \c Data::assign_eqn_numbers().

1003 \subsection solid_fe The SolidFiniteElement class
1004 
1005 The class \c SolidFiniteElement is derived from \c FiniteElement and
1006 implements the additional functionality required for solid
1007 mechanics problems. Again, most of the additional (or revised)
1008 functionality is related to the presence of the two coordinate
1009 systems which requires the following changes to \c FiniteElement
1010 member functions:
1011 - The nodes of \c SolidFiniteElements are \c SolidNodes, therefore
1012  we overload the function \c FiniteElements::construct_node(...)
1013  to ensure that a \c SolidNode with the appropriate
1014  amount of storage is built. As in the case of \c FiniteElements,
1015  the required spatial dimension of the elements' constituent
1016  nodes, their number of nodal values etc. are specified via
1017  the \c FiniteElement's (pure) virtual member functions
1018  \c FiniteElement::required_ndim(...),
1019  \c FiniteElement::required_nvalue(...), etc, which must be
1020  implemented for all specific elements that are derived from
1021  the \c SolidFiniteElement base class. As discussed above, the
1022  constructor of the \c SolidNodes requires additional parameters, such
1023  as the number of Lagrangian coordinates. These must be specified
1024  by implementing \c SolidFiniteElement::required_nlagrangian(...)
1025  and similar other functions. As in the
1026  case of \c FiniteElements, many of these functions are already
1027  implemented as virtual (rather than pure virtual) functions which provide
1028  sensible default values. Such functions must be overloaded in
1029  specific derived elements if the default assignments are not
1030  appropriate.
1031 - The interpolation of the Eulerian coordinates, implemented
1032  in \c FiniteElement::interpolated_x(...), can remain unchanged
1033  because \c Node::x(...) always returns the Eulerian nodal positions.
1034  \c SolidFiniteElements provide additional functions, such as
1035  \code
1036  SolidFiniteElement::interpolated_xi(...)
1037  \endcode
1038  which determines the interpolated Lagrangian coordinates at
1039  a specified local coordinate within the element, or
1040  \code
1041  SolidFiniteElement::raw_lagrangian_position(...)
1042  \endcode
1043  and
1044  \code
1045  SolidFiniteElement::lagrangian_position(...)
1046  \endcode
1047  which provides a wrapper for the nodal values of the
1048  Lagrangian coordinates. (The "raw" version of the function returns
1049  the Lagrangian coordinates stored at the \c SolidNode; the second
1050  version automatically computes the suitably constrained Lagrangian
1051  coordinates if the \c SolidNode is hanging.)
1052 - The displacement form of the principle of virtual displacements
1053  (19) contains derivatives of the shape functions
1054  with respect to the Lagrangian (rather than the Eulerian)
1055  coordinates. Their computation is implemented in
1056  \code
1057  SolidFiniteElement::dshape_lagrangian(...)
1058  \endcode
1059 - We have now created storage and access functions for the
1060  \c Data that represents the nodal positions. We must ensure that
1061  these \c Data items are included in the various equation numbering
1062  schemes. For this purpose we provide the function
1063  \c SolidFiniteElement::assign_solid_local_eqn_numbers()
1064  which sets up the local equation numbering scheme for all
1065  solid \c Data associated with an element. This function is
1066  called when the SolidFiniteElement's local equation numbers
1067  are generated.
1068 - We're done! \c SolidFiniteElements now form a suitable basis for
1069  all elements whose deformation is determined
1070  by the equations of solid mechanics (or some variant thereof).
1071  To implement a specific
1072  solid mechanics element, we must represent its geometry,
1073  its state of stress, etc., in terms of the \c
1074  SolidFiniteElement's positional and non-positional
1075  \c Data. This requires the specification of the shape functions
1076  and the functions that compute the element's Jacobian matrix
1077  and its residual vector -- the latter implementing the
1078  element's contribution to the global residual vector defined by the
1079  discretised principle of virtual displacements, (19).
1080  As for "normal" \c
1081  FiniteElements it is sensible to construct specific \c
1082  SolidFiniteElements in a hierarchy which separates between
1083  the implementation of the governing equations and the
1084  representation of the element geometry. For instance,
1085  the \c SolidQElement family represents the generalisation of the
1086  \c QElement family to \c SolidFiniteElements, while \c
1087  PVDEquations implement the principle of virtual
1088  displacements (19). The two are combined
1089  by multiple inheritance to form the \c QPVDElement class.
1090 - The computation of the
1091  element's Jacobian matrix requires the evaluation of
1092  the derivatives of the discrete residuals (19) with respect
1093  to the unknown nodal positions, and with respect to any
1094  additional unknown solid mechanics variables (e.g. pressures). The
1095  derivatives with respect to the nodal positions result in
1096  fairly complex algebraic expressions and it is sometimes
1097  more efficient to evaluate these entries by finite
1098  differencing. By default, the derivatives are evaluated analytically
1099  using carefully optimised assembly loops, but
1100  a finite-difference-base evaluation can be activated instead;
1101  see the detailed description of the \c PVDEquations and the
1102  \c PVDEquationsWithPressure classes.

1196 \section Solid_IC Timestepping and the generation of initial conditions for solid mechanics problems
1197 In time-dependent problems, the boundary value problem defined by
1198 the variational principle (10) must be
1199 augmented by suitable initial conditions which specify the
1200 state of the system at time \f$ t=t_0. \f$ The initial
1201 conditions specify the initial shape of the solid body,
1202 \f[
1203 {\bf R}(\xi^i,t=t_0) = {\bf R}^{(IC)}(\xi^i),
1204 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (20)
1205 \f]
1206 and its initial velocity,
1207 \f[
1208 \left. \frac{\partial {\bf R}(\xi^i,t)}{\partial t} \right|_{t=t_0} =
1209 {\bf V}^{(IC)}(\xi^i),
1210 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (21)
1211 \f]
1212 where \f$ {\bf R}^{(IC)}(\xi^i) \f$ and \f$ {\bf V}^{(IC)}(\xi^i) \f$
1213 are given. The accelerations \f$ \partial^2 {\bf R}/\partial t^2
1214 \f$ at \f$t=t_0\f$ follow from the solution of
1215 (10) and can therefore not be enforced,
1216 unless we wish to initialise the time-stepping procedure with a known
1217 exact solution \f$ {\bf R}^{(exact)}(\xi^i,t) \f$. (Only!) in this case
1218 are we allowed to assign an initial value for the acceleration via
1219 \f[
1220 \left. \frac{\partial^2 {\bf R}(\xi^i,t)}{\partial t^2} \right|_{t=t_0} =
1221 {\bf A}^{(IC)}(\xi^i) \equiv
1222 \left. \frac{\partial^2 {\bf R}^{(exact)}(\xi^i,t)}{\partial t^2}
1223 \right|_{t=t_0}.
1224 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (22)
1225 \f]
1226 We will assume that time-stepping is performed with the \c Newmark
1227 method which is our default timestepper for hyperbolic
1228 problems. In this case the time-derivatives of the nodal positions in
1229 (10) are replaced by an approximation
1230 which involves the current and three "history values" of the nodal
1231 positions. To start the time-integration, we must assign
1232 suitable values to these quantities to ensure that the initial
1233 state of the system is represented correctly.
1234 
1235 To assign the initial values for the nodal positions, we
1236 (temporarily) remove all boundary conditions for the nodal
1237 positions and determine their initial values by solving equation
1238 (20) in its weak form,
1239 \f[
1240 f_{il}^{(0)} = \int \left( \sum_{j=1}^N X_{ij}^{(0)} \psi_j(\xi^k)
1241 - R^{(IC)}_i(\xi^k)
1242 \right) \psi_l(\xi^k) \ dv = 0
1243 \mbox{ \ \ \ \ for \ \ \ $l=1,...,N; \ \ i=1,..,3$}
1244 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (23)
1245 \f]
1246 where \f$ R^{(IC)}_i \f$ is the \f$i\f$-th component of
1247 \f${\bf R}^{(IC)}.\f$ Equation (23) provides
1248 \f$ 3 \times N\f$ equations for the \f$ 3 \times N \f$ components
1249 of the initial nodal positions, \f$ X_{ij}^{(0)} \f$ (where
1250 \f$i=1,...3; \ j=1,...,N)\f$. To determine the initial nodal
1251 velocities, we repeat the same procedure with the prescribed
1252 velocities and solve
1253 \f[
1254 f_{il}^{(1)} = \int \left( \sum_{j=1}^N X_{ij}^{(1)} \psi_j(\xi^k)
1255 - V^{(IC)}_i(\xi^k)
1256 \right) \psi_l(\xi^k) \ dv = 0
1257 \mbox{ \ \ \ \ for \ \ \ $l=1,...,N; \ \ i=1,..,3$}
1258 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (24)
1259 \f]
1260 for the initial nodal velocities, \f$ X_{ij}^{(1)} \f$ (where
1261 \f$i=1,...3; \ j=1,...,N)\f$. Finally, assuming that we have
1262 an exact solution for the accelerations, we solve
1263 \f[
1264 f_{il}^{(2)} = \int \left( \sum_{j=1}^N X_{ij}^{(2)} \psi_j(\xi^k)
1265 - A^{(IC)}_i(\xi^k)
1266 \right) \psi_l(\xi^k) \ dv = 0
1267 \mbox{ \ \ \ \ for \ \ \ $l=1,...,N; \ \ i=1,..,3$}
1268 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (25)
1269 \f]
1270 for the initial nodal accelerations, \f$ X_{ij}^{(2)} \f$ (where
1271 \f$i=1,...3; \ j=1,...,N)\f$. Having determined the
1272 nodal positions and their first and second time-derivatives
1273 at \f$ t=t_0 \f$, we can use the functions
1274 \c Newmark::assign_initial_data_values_stage1(...) and
1275 \c Newmark::assign_initial_data_values_stage2(...) to compute the
1276 positional history values which ensure that the Newmark
1277 approximations for the
1278 initial velocity and acceleration are correct.
1279 This procedure is fully implemented in the function
1280 \code
1281 SolidMesh::Solid_IC_problem.set_newmark_initial_condition_directly(...)
1282 \endcode
1283 whose arguments are:
1284 - The pointer to the problem being solved. This is needed because
1285  the solution of equations (23) -
1286  (25) requires a temporary change to the
1287  boundary conditions and to the equation numbering scheme. Once
1288  the history values have been assigned, the original
1289  boundary conditions are restored and the equation numbers
1290  are re-generated by executing \c Problem::assign_eqn_numbers().
1291 - A pointer to the \c SolidMesh on which the initial conditions
1292  are assigned.
1293 - A pointer to the \c TimeStepper (which has to be a member of the \c Newmark
1294  family).
1295 - A pointer to the "Elastic initial condition" object (discussed below).
1296 - The initial timestep.

1387 to determine the initial nodal positions and velocities.
1388 In that case we solve the equations (19) for the
1389 remaining "history value". Since the equations (19)
1390 are linear in the accelerations, this is a linear problem
1391 whose Jacobian matrix is proportional to the mass matrix
1392 \f[

1395 \f]

1397 from the given initial positions and velocities while ensuring
1398 consistency with the governing equation at \f$ t = t_0\f$
1399 is implemented in

1401 which takes the same arguments as the function that assigns
1402 the acceleration directly, but also requires a function pointer
1403 to a "multiplier" \f$ {\cal M} \f$. If there
1404 is no growth, i.e. if \f$\Lambda=1\f$ in (19),
1405 the multiplier is given by the timescale ratio
1406 \f$\Lambda^2\f$; if the body is subjected to uniform isotropic
1407 growth, \f$\Gamma \ne 1\f$, the multiplier is equal to
1408 \f$\Gamma \Lambda^2\f$.
1409 If the wrong multiplier is specified (or if it is omitted, in
1410 which case the default value of 1.0 is used) the residuals (19)
1411 will be nonzero (or at least larger than the tolerance specified in \c
1412 SolidICProblem). In this case a warning is issued and the
1413 code execution terminates. This behaviour can be suppressed
1414 by increasing the tolerance suitably, but you do this at your own risk!

1421 such as solid pressures. We do not believe that
1422 it is possible to implement the assignment of initial conditions

1429 which involve unknowns that do not represent nodal positions. If the function
