3 \mainpage Demo problem: Compression of 2D circular disk
4 
5 In this example we study the compression of a 2D circular disk, loaded
6 by an external pressure. We also demonstrate:
7 - how to "upgrade" a \c Mesh to a \c SolidMesh
8 - why it is necessary to use "undeformed MacroElements" to ensure that
9  the numerical results converge to the correct solution under
10  mesh refinement if the domain has curvilinear boundaries.
11 - how to switch between different constitutive equations
12 - how to incorporate isotropic growth into the model
13 .
14 We validate the numerical results by comparing them
15 against the analytical solution of the equations of linear
16 elasticity which are valid for small deflections.

22 \section problem The problem
23 The figure below shows a sketch of the basic problem: A 2D circular disk
24 of radius \f$ a \f$ is loaded by a uniform pressure \f$ p_0^*\f$.
25 We wish to compute the disk's deformation for a variety of
26 constitutive equations.
27 
28 \image html disk_compression.gif "Sketch of the problem. "
29 \image latex disk_compression.eps "Sketch of the problem. " width=0.75\textwidth
30 
31 The next sketch shows a variant of the problem: We assume that
32 the material undergoes isotropic growth (e.g. via a
33 biological growth process or thermal expansion, say) with a constant growth
34 factor \f$ \Gamma \f$. We refer to the the document
35 <A HREF="../../solid_theory/html/index.html#isotropic_growth">
36 "Solid mechanics: Theory and implementation"</A> for a detailed
37 discussion of the theory of isotropic growth. Briefly, the growth
38 factor defines the relative increase in the volume of an infinitesimal material
39 element, relative to its volume in the stress-free reference
40 configuration. If the growth factor is spatially uniform, isotropic
41 growth leads to a uniform expansion of the material. For a circular disk,
42 uniform growth increases the disk's
43 radius from \f$ a_0 \f$ to \f$ a = a_0 \sqrt{\Gamma} \f$ without
44 inducing any internal stresses. This uniformly expanded
45 disk may then be regarded as the stress-free reference configuration
46 upon which the external pressure acts.

68 The figure below illustrates the disk's load-displacement characteristics
69 by plotting the disk's non-dimensional radius as function of the
70 non-dimensional pressure, \f$ p_0 = p_0^*/{\cal S} \f$ , where
71 \f$ {\cal S} \f$ is the characteristic stiffness of the material, for
72 a variety of constitutive equations.

75 \image html trace.gif "Load displacement characteristics for various constitutive equations. "
76 \image latex trace.eps "Load displacement characteristics for various constitutive equations. " width=0.75\textwidth \subsection hooke Generalised Hooke's law
77 
78 The blue, dash-dotted line corresponds to \c oomph-lib's
79 generalisation of Hooke's law (with Young's modulus \f$ E \f$ and
80 Poisson ratio \f$ \nu \f$ ) in which the dimensionless second
81 Piola Kirchhoff stress tensor (non-dimensionalised with the
82 material's Young's modulus \f$ E \f$, so that \f$ {\cal S} = E \f$ )
83 is given by
84 \f[
85 \sigma^{ij} = \frac{1}{2(1+\nu)}
86 \left(
87 G^{ik} G^{jl} + G^{il} G^{jk} +
88 \frac{2\nu}{1-2\nu} G^{ij} G^{kl}
89 \right) \gamma_{kl}.
90 \f]
91 Here \f$ \gamma_{ij}= 1/2 (G_{ij}-g_{ij})\f$ is Green's strain
92 tensor, formed from the difference between the deformed and
93 undeformed metric tensors, \f$ G_{ij} \f$ and \f$ g_{ij} \f$,
94 respectively. The three different markers identify the
95 results obtained with the two forms of the
96 principle of virtual displacement, employing the displacement
97 formulation (squares), and a pressure/displacement formulation
98 with a continuous (delta) and a discontinuous (nabla) pressure interpolation.
99 
100 For zero pressure the disk's non-dimensional radius is equal to the
101 uniformly grown radius \f$ \sqrt{\Gamma} = 1.0488. \f$ For
102 small pressures the load-displacement curve follows
103 the linear approximation
104 \f[
105 r = \sqrt{\Gamma} \big( 1-p_0 (1+\nu)(1-2\nu) \big).
106 \f]
107 We note that the generalised Hooke's law leads to strain
108 softening behaviour under compression (the pressure required
109 to reduce the disk's radius to a given value increases more
110 rapidly than predicted by the linear approximation) whereas
111 under expansion (for negative external pressures) the behaviour is
112 strain softening.

114 \subsection mr Generalised Mooney-Rivlin law
115 
116 The red, dashed line illustrates the behaviour when Fung \& Tong's
117 generalisation of the Mooney-Rivlin law (with Young's modulus, \f$ E \f$,
118 Poisson ratio \f$ \nu \f$ and Mooney-Rivlin parameter \f$ C_1\f$)
119 is used as the constitutive equation. For this constitutive law,
120 the non-dimensional strain energy function \f$ W = W^*/{\cal S}\f$,
121 where the characteristic stress is given by Young's modulus, i.e. \f$ {\cal
122 S} =E \f$, is given by
123 \f[
124 W = \frac{1}{2} (I_1-3) + (G-C_1)(I_2-3) + (C_1-2G)(I_3-1)
125 + (I_3-1)^2 \ \frac{G(1-\nu)}{2(1-2\nu)} \ ,
126 \f]
127 where
128 \f[
129 G = \frac{E}{2(1+\nu)}
130 \f]
131 is the shear modulus, and \f$ I_1, I_2 \f$ and \f$ I_3 \f$ are the
132 three invariants of Green's strain tensor. See
133 <A HREF="../../solid_theory/html/index.html#strain-energy">
134 "Solid mechanics: Theory and implementation"</A> for a detailed
135 discussion of strain energy functions. The figure shows that
136 for small deflections, the disk's behaviour is again well approximated
137 by linear elasticity. However, in the large-displacement regime
138 the Mooney-Rivlin is strain hardening under extension and softening
139 under compression when compared to the linear elastic behaviour.

372 \subsection macro The use of MacroElements in solid mechanics problems
373 <A HREF="../../../poisson/fish_poisson2/html/index.html">Recall</A>
374 how \c oomph-lib employs \c MacroElements to represent the
375 exact domain shapes in adaptive computations involving problems
376 with curvilinear boundaries. When an element is refined, the
377 (Eulerian) position of any newly-created nodes is based on the
378 element's \c MacroElement counterpart, rather than being determined
379 by finite-element interpolation from the "father element". This ensures
380 that <b>(i)</b> newly-created nodes on curvilinear domain boundaries are placed
381 exactly onto those boundaries and <b>(ii)</b> that newly-created nodes in the
382 interior are placed at positions that match smoothly onto the boundary.
383 
384 This strategy is adapted slightly for solid mechanics problems:
385 -# The Eulerian position of newly-created \c SolidNodes is
386  determined by finite element interpolation from the
387  "father element", \b unless the newly-created \c SolidNode is located
388  on a domain boundary and its position is pinned by displacement
389  boundary conditions.
390  \n\n
391 -# The same procedure is employed to determine the Lagrangian
392  coordinates of newly-created \c SolidNodes.
393 .
394 These modifications ensure that, as before, newly-created nodes
395 on curvilinear domain boundaries are placed exactly onto those
396 boundaries if their positions are pinned by displacement boundary
397 conditions. (If the nodal positions are not pinned, the node's
398 Eulerian position will be determined as part of the
399 solution.) The use of finite-element interpolation from the "father element"
400 in the interior of the domain for both Lagrangian and Eulerian
401 coordinates ensures that the creation of new nodes does not induce
402 any stresses into a previously computed solution.
