3\mainpage Demo problem: Compression of 2D circular disk
4
5In this example we study the compression of a 2D circular disk, loaded
6by an external pressure. We also demonstrate:
7- how to "upgrade" a \c Mesh to a \c SolidMesh
8- why it is necessary to use "undeformed MacroElements" to ensure that
9 the numerical results converge to the correct solution under
10 mesh refinement if the domain has curvilinear boundaries.
11- how to switch between different constitutive equations
12- how to incorporate isotropic growth into the model
13.
14We validate the numerical results by comparing them
15against the analytical solution of the equations of linear
16elasticity which are valid for small deflections.

22\section problem The problem
23The figure below shows a sketch of the basic problem: A 2D circular disk
24of radius \f$ a \f$ is loaded by a uniform pressure \f$ p_0^*\f$.
25We wish to compute the disk's deformation for a variety of
26constitutive equations.
27
28\image html disk_compression.gif "Sketch of the problem. "
29\image latex disk_compression.eps "Sketch of the problem. " width=0.75\textwidth
30
31The next sketch shows a variant of the problem: We assume that
32the material undergoes isotropic growth (e.g. via a
33biological growth process or thermal expansion, say) with a constant growth
34factor \f$ \Gamma \f$. We refer to the the document
35<A HREF="../../solid_theory/html/index.html#isotropic_growth">
36"Solid mechanics: Theory and implementation"</A> for a detailed
37discussion of the theory of isotropic growth. Briefly, the growth
38factor defines the relative increase in the volume of an infinitesimal material
39element, relative to its volume in the stress-free reference
40configuration. If the growth factor is spatially uniform, isotropic
41growth leads to a uniform expansion of the material. For a circular disk,
42uniform growth increases the disk's
43radius from \f$ a_0 \f$ to \f$ a = a_0 \sqrt{\Gamma} \f$ without
44inducing any internal stresses. This uniformly expanded
45disk may then be regarded as the stress-free reference configuration
46upon which the external pressure acts.

78\subsection hooke Generalised Hooke's law
79
80The blue, dash-dotted line corresponds to \c oomph-lib's
81generalisation of Hooke's law (with Young's modulus \f$ E \f$ and
82Poisson ratio \f$ \nu \f$ ) in which the dimensionless second
83Piola Kirchhoff stress tensor (non-dimensionalised with the
84material's Young's modulus \f$ E \f$, so that \f$ {\cal S} = E \f$ )
85is given by
86\f[
87\sigma^{ij} = \frac{1}{2(1+\nu)}
88\left(
89G^{ik} G^{jl} + G^{il} G^{jk} +
90\frac{2\nu}{1-2\nu} G^{ij} G^{kl}
91\right) \gamma_{kl}.
92\f]
93Here \f$ \gamma_{ij}= 1/2 (G_{ij}-g_{ij})\f$ is Green's strain
94tensor, formed from the difference between the deformed and
95undeformed metric tensors, \f$ G_{ij} \f$ and \f$ g_{ij} \f$,
96respectively. The three different markers identify the
97results obtained with the two forms of the
98principle of virtual displacement, employing the displacement
99formulation (squares), and a pressure/displacement formulation
100with a continuous (delta) and a discontinuous (nabla) pressure interpolation.
101
102For zero pressure the disk's non-dimensional radius is equal to the
103uniformly grown radius \f$ \sqrt{\Gamma} = 1.0488. \f$ For
104small pressures the load-displacement curve follows
105the linear approximation
106\f[
107r = \sqrt{\Gamma} \big( 1-p_0 (1+\nu)(1-2\nu) \big).
108\f]
109We note that the generalised Hooke's law leads to strain
110softening behaviour under compression (the pressure required
111to reduce the disk's radius to a given value increases more
112rapidly than predicted by the linear approximation) whereas
113under expansion (for negative external pressures) the behaviour is
114strain softening.
115
116\subsection mr Generalised Mooney-Rivlin law
117
118The red, dashed line illustrates the behaviour when Fung \& Tong's
119generalisation of the Mooney-Rivlin law (with Young's modulus, \f$ E \f$,
120Poisson ratio \f$ \nu \f$ and Mooney-Rivlin parameter \f$ C_1\f$)
121is used as the constitutive equation. For this constitutive law,
122the non-dimensional strain energy function \f$ W = W^*/{\cal S}\f$,
123where the characteristic stress is given by Young's modulus, i.e. \f$ {\cal
124S} =E \f$, is given by
125\f[
126W = \frac{1}{2} (I_1-3) + (G-C_1)(I_2-3) + (C_1-2G)(I_3-1)
127+ (I_3-1)^2 \ \frac{G(1-\nu)}{2(1-2\nu)} \ ,
128\f]
129where
130\f[
131G = \frac{E}{2(1+\nu)}
132\f]
133is the shear modulus, and \f$ I_1, I_2 \f$ and \f$ I_3 \f$ are the
134three invariants of Green's strain tensor. See
135<A HREF="../../solid_theory/html/index.html#strain-energy">
136"Solid mechanics: Theory and implementation"</A> for a detailed
137discussion of strain energy functions. The figure shows that
138for small deflections, the disk's behaviour is again well approximated
139by linear elasticity. However, in the large-displacement regime
140the Mooney-Rivlin is strain hardening under extension and softening
141under compression when compared to the linear elastic behaviour.

232the domain, applying appropriate symmetry conditions along the
233x and y axes. The computational domain may be discretised
234with the \c RefineableQuarterCircleSectorMesh that we
235already used in many previous examples. To use the mesh in
236this solid mechanics problem we must first "upgrade"
237it to a \c SolidMesh. This is easily done by multiple inheritance:

240\skipline start_mesh
241\until {

243The constructor calls the constructor of the underlying \c
244RefineableQuarterCircleSectorMesh and sets the Lagrangian
245coordinates of the nodes to their current Eulerian positions,
246making the initial configuration stress-free.

374\subsection macro The use of MacroElements in solid mechanics problems
375<A HREF="../../../poisson/fish_poisson2/html/index.html">Recall</A>
376how \c oomph-lib employs \c MacroElements to represent the
377exact domain shapes in adaptive computations involving problems
378with curvilinear boundaries. When an element is refined, the
379(Eulerian) position of any newly-created nodes is based on the
380element's \c MacroElement counterpart, rather than being determined
381by finite-element interpolation from the "father element". This ensures
382that <b>(i)</b> newly-created nodes on curvilinear domain boundaries are placed
383exactly onto those boundaries and <b>(ii)</b> that newly-created nodes in the
384interior are placed at positions that match smoothly onto the boundary.
385
386This strategy is adapted slightly for solid mechanics problems:
387-# The Eulerian position of newly-created \c SolidNodes is
388 determined by finite element interpolation from the
389 "father element", \b unless the newly-created \c SolidNode is located
390 on a domain boundary and its position is pinned by displacement
391 boundary conditions.
392 \n\n
393-# The same procedure is employed to determine the Lagrangian
394 coordinates of newly-created \c SolidNodes.
395.
396These modifications ensure that, as before, newly-created nodes
397on curvilinear domain boundaries are placed exactly onto those
398boundaries if their positions are pinned by displacement boundary
399conditions. (If the nodal positions are not pinned, the node's
400Eulerian position will be determined as part of the
401solution.) The use of finite-element interpolation from the "father element"
402in the interior of the domain for both Lagrangian and Eulerian
403coordinates ensures that the creation of new nodes does not induce
404any stresses into a previously computed solution.
405
