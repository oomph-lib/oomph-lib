3 \mainpage Demo problem: Compression of 2D circular disk
4 
5 In this example we study the compression of a 2D circular disk, loaded
6 by an external pressure. We also demonstrate:
7 - how to "upgrade" a \c Mesh to a \c SolidMesh
8 - why it is necessary to use "undeformed MacroElements" to ensure that
9  the numerical results converge to the correct solution under
10  mesh refinement if the domain has curvilinear boundaries.
11 - how to switch between different constitutive equations
12 - how to incorporate isotropic growth into the model
13 .
14 We validate the numerical results by comparing them
15 against the analytical solution of the equations of linear
16 elasticity which are valid for small deflections.

23 The figure below shows a sketch of the basic problem: A 2D circular disk

25 We wish to compute the disk's deformation for a variety of
26 constitutive equations.
27 
28 \image html disk_compression.gif "Sketch of the problem. "
29 \image latex disk_compression.eps "Sketch of the problem. " width=0.75\textwidth
30 
31 The next sketch shows a variant of the problem: We assume that
32 the material undergoes isotropic growth (e.g. via a
33 biological growth process or thermal expansion, say) with a constant growth
34 factor \f$ \Gamma \f$. We refer to the the document
35 <A HREF="../../solid_theory/html/index.html#isotropic_growth">
36 "Solid mechanics: Theory and implementation"</A> for a detailed
37 discussion of the theory of isotropic growth. Briefly, the growth
38 factor defines the relative increase in the volume of an infinitesimal material
39 element, relative to its volume in the stress-free reference
40 configuration. If the growth factor is spatially uniform, isotropic
41 growth leads to a uniform expansion of the material. For a circular disk,
42 uniform growth increases the disk's
43 radius from \f$ a_0 \f$ to \f$ a = a_0 \sqrt{\Gamma} \f$ without
44 inducing any internal stresses. This uniformly expanded
45 disk may then be regarded as the stress-free reference configuration
46 upon which the external pressure acts.

78 \subsection hooke Generalised Hooke's law
79 
80 The blue, dash-dotted line corresponds to \c oomph-lib's
81 generalisation of Hooke's law (with Young's modulus \f$ E \f$ and
82 Poisson ratio \f$ \nu \f$ ) in which the dimensionless second
83 Piola Kirchhoff stress tensor (non-dimensionalised with the
84 material's Young's modulus \f$ E \f$, so that \f$ {\cal S} = E \f$ )
85 is given by
86 \f[
87 \sigma^{ij} = \frac{1}{2(1+\nu)}
88 \left(
89 G^{ik} G^{jl} + G^{il} G^{jk} +
90 \frac{2\nu}{1-2\nu} G^{ij} G^{kl}
91 \right) \gamma_{kl}.
92 \f]
93 Here \f$ \gamma_{ij}= 1/2 (G_{ij}-g_{ij})\f$ is Green's strain
94 tensor, formed from the difference between the deformed and
95 undeformed metric tensors, \f$ G_{ij} \f$ and \f$ g_{ij} \f$,
96 respectively. The three different markers identify the
97 results obtained with the two forms of the
98 principle of virtual displacement, employing the displacement
99 formulation (squares), and a pressure/displacement formulation
100 with a continuous (delta) and a discontinuous (nabla) pressure interpolation.
101 
102 For zero pressure the disk's non-dimensional radius is equal to the
103 uniformly grown radius \f$ \sqrt{\Gamma} = 1.0488. \f$ For
104 small pressures the load-displacement curve follows
105 the linear approximation
106 \f[
107 r = \sqrt{\Gamma} \big( 1-p_0 (1+\nu)(1-2\nu) \big).
108 \f]
109 We note that the generalised Hooke's law leads to strain
110 softening behaviour under compression (the pressure required
111 to reduce the disk's radius to a given value increases more
112 rapidly than predicted by the linear approximation) whereas
113 under expansion (for negative external pressures) the behaviour is
114 strain softening.

116 \subsection mr Generalised Mooney-Rivlin law
117 
118 The red, dashed line illustrates the behaviour when Fung \& Tong's
119 generalisation of the Mooney-Rivlin law (with Young's modulus, \f$ E \f$,
120 Poisson ratio \f$ \nu \f$ and Mooney-Rivlin parameter \f$ C_1\f$)
121 is used as the constitutive equation. For this constitutive law,
122 the non-dimensional strain energy function \f$ W = W^*/{\cal S}\f$,
123 where the characteristic stress is given by Young's modulus, i.e. \f$ {\cal
124 S} =E \f$, is given by
125 \f[
126 W = \frac{1}{2} (I_1-3) + (G-C_1)(I_2-3) + (C_1-2G)(I_3-1)
127 + (I_3-1)^2 \ \frac{G(1-\nu)}{2(1-2\nu)} \ ,
128 \f]
129 where
130 \f[
131 G = \frac{E}{2(1+\nu)}
132 \f]
133 is the shear modulus, and \f$ I_1, I_2 \f$ and \f$ I_3 \f$ are the
134 three invariants of Green's strain tensor. See
135 <A HREF="../../solid_theory/html/index.html#strain-energy">
136 "Solid mechanics: Theory and implementation"</A> for a detailed
137 discussion of strain energy functions. The figure shows that
138 for small deflections, the disk's behaviour is again well approximated
139 by linear elasticity. However, in the large-displacement regime
140 the Mooney-Rivlin is strain hardening under extension and softening
141 under compression when compared to the linear elastic behaviour.

374 \subsection macro The use of MacroElements in solid mechanics problems
375 <A HREF="../../../poisson/fish_poisson2/html/index.html">Recall</A>
376 how \c oomph-lib employs \c MacroElements to represent the
377 exact domain shapes in adaptive computations involving problems
378 with curvilinear boundaries. When an element is refined, the
379 (Eulerian) position of any newly-created nodes is based on the
380 element's \c MacroElement counterpart, rather than being determined
381 by finite-element interpolation from the "father element". This ensures
382 that <b>(i)</b> newly-created nodes on curvilinear domain boundaries are placed
383 exactly onto those boundaries and <b>(ii)</b> that newly-created nodes in the
384 interior are placed at positions that match smoothly onto the boundary.
385 
386 This strategy is adapted slightly for solid mechanics problems:
387 -# The Eulerian position of newly-created \c SolidNodes is
388  determined by finite element interpolation from the
389  "father element", \b unless the newly-created \c SolidNode is located
390  on a domain boundary and its position is pinned by displacement
391  boundary conditions.
392  \n\n
393 -# The same procedure is employed to determine the Lagrangian
394  coordinates of newly-created \c SolidNodes.
395 .
396 These modifications ensure that, as before, newly-created nodes
397 on curvilinear domain boundaries are placed exactly onto those
398 boundaries if their positions are pinned by displacement boundary
399 conditions. (If the nodal positions are not pinned, the node's
400 Eulerian position will be determined as part of the
401 solution.) The use of finite-element interpolation from the "father element"
402 in the interior of the domain for both Lagrangian and Eulerian
403 coordinates ensures that the creation of new nodes does not induce
404 any stresses into a previously computed solution.
