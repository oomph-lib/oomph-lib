3\mainpage Demo problem: Compressible and incompressible behaviour
4
5The purpose of this tutorial is to discuss the use of compressible
6and incompressible constitutive equations for solid mechanics
7problems. As discussed in the
8<a href="../../solid_theory/html/index.html#elastic_constitutive">
9Solid Mechanics Theory Tutorial</a>, problems in which the solid
10is truly incompressible require a solution based on the
11pressure/displacement formulation of the principle of
12virtual displacements (PVD). Mathematically, this is because
13the pressure acts as the Lagrange multiplier that enforces
14incompressibility. The pressure/displacement form of the
15PVD may be discretised with finite elements that employ
16continuous (e.g. Taylor-Hood-type) or discontinuous
17(e.g. Crouzeix-Raviart-type) elements.
18
19 Some constitutive equations allow for compressible and
20incompressible behaviour, depending on their parameters.
21Problems involving such constitutive equations may be solved
22with large number of formulations. As an example, consider \c
23oomph-lib's generalised Hookean constitutive equation
24(see the <a href="#disclaimer">disclaimer</a> below).
25
26
27<b> I. The displacement form </b>
28
29\c oomph-lib's generalised Hookean constitutive law assumes that
30the 2nd Piola Kirchhoff stress \f$ \sigma^{ij} \f$ (non-dimensionalised on
31Young's modulus \f$ E \f$) is related to Green's strain tensor
32\f$ \gamma_{ij} = 1/2 \ (G_{ij} - g_{ij}) \f$ via
33\f[
34\sigma^{ij} = \frac{1}{2(1+\nu)}
35\left(
36G^{ik} G^{jl} + G^{il} G^{jk} +
37\frac{2\nu}{1-2\nu} G^{ij} G^{kl}
38\right) \gamma_{kl}.
39\hspace{3cm} (1)
40\f]
41Here \f$ g_{ij} \f$ and \f$ G_{ij} \f$ are the metric tensors
42associated with the undeformed and deformed configurations.
43This constitutive law reduces to the
44the classical version of Hooke's law for small strains when
45\f$ G_{ij} \to g_{ij}. \f$ In the above form, the constitutive
46law can be used directly in the displacement-based form of the PVD,
47unless \f$ \nu = 1/2 \f$ -- the case that corresponds to
48incompressible behaviour in the small-displacement regime.
49(While the above formulation only breaks down completely
50when \f$ \nu = 1/2, \f$ numerical solutions based on the
51above form of the constitutive equation become badly-behaved
52as \f$ \nu \f$ approaches that value.)
53
54
55<b> II. The pressure-displacement form for compressible and
56incompressible behaviour</b>
57
58To avoid the problems that arise as \f$ \nu \to 1/2, \f$ the constitutive
59equation may be rewritten in the form
60\f[
61\sigma^{ij} = \overline{\sigma}^{ij} - p \ G^{ij} \ ,
62\f]
63where the deviatoric stress is given by
64\f[
65\overline{\sigma}^{ij} =
66\frac{1}{2(1+\nu)}
67\bigg(
68G^{ik} G^{jl} + G^{il} G^{jk} \bigg) \gamma_{kl}.
69\f]
70To remain consistent with (1), the pressure
71\f$ p \f$ must then be determined via the equation
72\f[
73p \ \frac{1}{\kappa} + d = 0, \hspace{4cm} (2)
74\f]
75where
76\f[
77d = G^{ij} \gamma_{ij}
78\f]
79is the generalised dilatation (which reduces to the actual dilatation
80in the small-strain limit). The inverse bulk modulus is defined as
81\f[
82\frac{1}{\kappa} = \frac{(1-2\nu)(1+\nu)}{\nu}
83\f]
84and tends to zero as \f$ \nu \to 0. \f$ The alternative
85form of the constitutive equation can therefore be used for
86any value of \f$ \nu \f$. If \f$ \nu = 1/2, \f$ the
87constraint (2) enforces \f$ d=0, \f$ i.e
88incompressible behaviour (at least in the
89small displacement regime -- for large deflections the constraint
90 (2) simply ensures that the generalised dilatation
91vanishes; this may not be a physically meaningful constraint).
92
93<b> III. Truly incompressible behaviour</b>
94
95Finally, we may retain the decomposition of the stress into its
96deviatoric and non-deviatoric parts but determine the pressure
97from the actual incompressibility constraint, i.e. replace
98(2) by
99\f[
100\det G_{ij} - \det g_{ij} = 0. \hspace{5cm} (3)
101\f]
102In this case, the deviatoric part of the stress is determined
103by the material's constitutive parameters, i.e. its Young's modulus
104and its Poisson ratio while the pressure (acting as the Lagrange
105multiplier for the constraint (3))
106enforces true incompressibility.
107

113<center>
114<b>Disclaimer</b>
115</center>
116We wish to stress that not all the combinations discussed above
117are necessarily physically meaningful. For instance, as already
118acknowledged, setting \f$ \nu = 1/2 \f$ in formulation II does not
119ensure true incompressibility (in the sense of (3))
120if the solid undergoes large deflections. Similarly, setting
121\f$ \nu \f$ to a value that differs from \f$ 1/2 \f$ while
122enforcing true incompressibility via formulation III would be
123extremely odd. Furthermore, it is not clear (to us) if our
124generalisation of Hooke's law (obtained by replacing the undeformed
125metric tensor \f$ g_{ij} \f$ by its deformed counterpart \f$ G_{ij}
126\f$ yields a constitutive equation that is particularly useful
127for any specific material. However, the same is true for any other
128constitutive equation -- not all of them are useful for all materials!
129The important point is that all constitutive equations that allow
130for compressible and incompressible behaviour contain combinations
131of constitutive parameters that blow up as incompressibility
132is approached. Formulation II shows how to express such
133constitutive laws in a way that can be used for any value of
134the constitutive parameters.
135</td>
136</tr>
137</TABLE>
138
139
140With this disclaimer in mind, we will now demonstrate the use
141of the various combinations outlined above in a simple test problem
142for which an exact (linearised) solution is available.

147\section problem The problem
148Here is a sketch of the problem: Three faces of a square elastic body are
149surrounded by "slippery" rigid walls that allow the body to slide freely
150along them. The body is subject to a vertical, gravitational
151body force, acting in the negative \f$ y \f$ -direction.
152
153\image html sketch.gif "Sketch of the problem. "
154\image latex sketch.eps "Sketch of the problem. " width=0.75\textwidth
155
156We choose the height of the square as the reference length for
157the non-dimensionalisation by setting
158\f$ {\cal L} = L, \f$ and use the characteristic stiffness associated with the
159body's constitutive equation to scale the stresses and the body
160forces. For instance, for linear elastic behaviour, we choose
161the reference stress to be the solid's Young's modulus, thus setting
162\f$ {\cal S} = E. \f$ The non-dimensional body force
163is then given by \f$ {\bf f} = - g \ {\bf e}_y, \f$ where
164\f[
165g = \frac{\rho g^* L}{E}
166\f]
167indicates the magnitude of the gravitational load relative to the
168body's stiffness;
169see the <a href="../../solid_theory/html/index.html#non-dim_solid">
170Solid Mechanics Theory Tutorial</a> for full details on the
171non-dimensionalisation of the governing equations.

179\section exact An exact solution
180
181Assuming weak loading, i.e. \f$ g \ll 1 \f$, the body will undergo
182small deflections and its deformation will be governed by the equations
183of linear elasticity. Given that the walls bounding the solid
184are slippery, it is easy to show that a displacement field has
185the form \f$ {\bf u} = v(y)\ {\bf e}_y \f$. Inserting this
186ansatz into the Navier-Lame equations shows that the non-dimensional
187displacement in the vertical direction is given by
188\f[
189v(y) = g \ \frac{(1+\nu)(1-2\nu)}{(1-\nu)}
190\left( \frac{1}{2} y^2 - y \right).
191\f]
192 The stresses are given by
193\f[
194\sigma_{xx} = \frac{g \ \nu}{(1-\nu)} (y-1) \ , \ \ \ \
195\sigma_{xy} = 0 \ ,
196\mbox{ \ \ \ and \ \ \ }
197\sigma_{yy} = g \ (y-1) \ .
198\f]
199This shows that, as the solid approaches incompressibility,
200\f$ \nu \to 1/2, \f$ the displacements are suppressed and
201the stress distribution becomes hydrostatic with \f$ \sigma_{xx} \to
202\sigma_{yy}. \f$

395\section comm_ex Comments and Exercises
396
397As usual, \c oomph-lib provides self-tests that assess if the
398enforcement incompressibility (or the lack thereof) is consistent:
399- The compiler will not allow the user to enforce incompressibility
400 on elements that are based on the displacement form of the
401 principle of virtual displacements. This is because
402 the displacement-based elements do not have a member function
403 \c incompressible().
404 \n\n
405- Certain constitutive laws, such as the Mooney-Rivlin law used in
406 the present example require an incompressible formulation. If \c oomph-lib
407 is compiled with the \c PARANOID flag, an error is thrown if
408 such a constitutive law is used by an element for which
409 incompressibility has not been requested.
410 \n\n
411 <center>
412 <b>Recall that the default setting is not to enforce
413 incompressibility!</b>
414 </center>
415 \n
416 If the library is
417 compiled without the \c PARANOID flag no warning will be issued
418 but the results will be "wrong" at least in the sense that
419 the material does not behave like an incompressible Mooney-Rivlin
420 solid. In fact, it is likely that the Newton solver will
421 diverge. Anyway, as we keep saying, without the \c PARANOID flag,
422 you're on your own!
423 \n\n
424.
425You should experiment with different combinations of constitutive laws and
426element types to familiarise yourself with these issues.
