3 \mainpage Demo problem: Bending of a 3D non-symmetric cantilever beam made of incompressible material
4 
5 In this tutorial we demonstrate the solution of a 3D solid mechanics
6 problem: the large-amplitude bending deformation of a non-symmetric
7 cantilever beam made of incompressible Mooney-Rivlin material.
8 
9 Here is an animation of the beam's deformation. In its undeformed
10 configuration, the beam is straight and its cross-section is given
11 by a quarter circle. The beam is loaded by an increasing gravitational
12 body force, acting in the negative \f$ y\f$-direction, while its left
13 end (at \f$ z=0 \f$) is held fixed. Because of its non-symmetric
14 cross-section, the beam's downward bending deformation is
15 accompanied by a sideways deflection.
16

19 
20 Note how the automatic mesh adaptation refines the mesh in the region
21 of strongest bending.

170 \subsection incompr How to enforce incompressibility
171 We stress that the imposition of incompressibility must be requested
172 explicitly via the element's member function \c incompressible().
173 Mathematically, incompressibility is enforced via a Lagrange
174 multiplier which manifests itself physically as the pressure.
175 Incompressibility can therefore only be enforced for
176 elements that employ the pressure-displacement formulation
177 of the principle of virtual displacements. This is why we
178 the \c Problem constructor checked if the element is
179 derived from the \c PVDEquationsWithPressure class before
180 setting the element's \c incompressible flag to \c true.
181 
182 As usual, \c oomph-lib provides self-tests that assess if the
183 enforcement incompressibility (or the lack thereof) is consistent:
184 - The compiler will not allow the user to enforce incompressibility
185  on elements that are based on the displacement form of the
186  principle of virtual displacements.
187  \n\n
188 - Certain constitutive laws, such as the Mooney-Rivlin law used in
189  the present example require an incompressible formulation. If \c oomph-lib
190  is compiled with the \c PARANOID flag, an error is thrown if
191  such a constitutive law is used by an element for which
192  incompressibility has not been requested.
193  \n\n
194  <center>
195  <b>Recall that the default setting is not to enforce
196  incompressibility!</b>
197  </center>
198  \n
199  If the library is
200  compiled without the \c PARANOID flag no warning will be issued
201  but the results will be "wrong" at least in the sense that
202  the material does not behave like an incompressible Mooney-Rivlin
203  solid. In fact, it is likely that the Newton solver will
204  diverge. Anyway, as we keep saying, without the \c PARANOID flag,
205  you're on your own!
206  \n\n
207 - Some constitutive laws can be used for compressible and
208  incompressible behaviour. In this case it is important to
209  set the \c incompressible() flag to the correct value.
210  This issue is discussed in more detail in
211  <a href="../../compressed_square/html/index.html">another tutorial.</a>
212 .
213 You should experiment with different combinations of constitutive laws and
214 element types to familiarise yourself with these.
