3 \mainpage Demo problem: Inflation of a blood vessel
4 
5 
6 The purpose of this tutorial is to demonstrate the simulation of
7 cardiovascular solid mechanics problems with \c oomph-lib. We showed in
8 <a href="../../../meshes/mesh_from_vmtk/html/index.html">another tutorial</a>
9 how to use <a href="http://www.vmtk.org">VMTK</a>
10 together with \c oomph-lib's conversion code
11 <CENTER>
12 <a href="../../../../demo_drivers/meshing/mesh_from_vmtk/create_fluid_and_solid_surface_mesh_from_fluid_xda_mesh.cc">demo_drivers/meshing/mesh_from_vmtk/create_fluid_and_solid_surface_mesh_from_fluid_xda_mesh.cc</a>
13 </CENTER>
14 to generate unstructured fluid and solid meshes for the simulation
15 of physiological fluid-structure interaction problems based on data from
16 medical images. Here we show how to study the deformation of the
17 vessel wall (which was assumed to have a constant thickness when
18 the solid mesh was generated from the imaging data) when subjected to a
19 pressure loading. The problem serves as a "warm-up problem" for the
20 <a href="../../../interaction/vmtk_fsi/html/index.html">
21 corresponding fluid-structure interaction problem </a> in which the elastic
22 vessel conveys (and is loaded by) a viscous fluid.
23 
24 We stress that the tutorial focuses on the implementation
25 aspects, not the actual physics. Since the driver code discussed
26 here is also used in the library's self-tests we
27 deliberately use a very coarse mesh and apply very simplistic
28 boundary conditions. The results shown below are therefore unlikely
29 to bear much resemblance to the actual deformation of a blood
30 vessel \e in \e vivo. The section \ref comm_ex at the end of this
31 tutorial provides several suggestions on how to make the simulation
32 more realistic.

55 is loaded by an internal pressure while its "ends" are held in
56 a fixed position. As the magnitude of the pressure load is

72 \section driver The driver code
73 
74 The driver code is practically identical to the one discussed in
75 <a href="../../unstructured_three_d_solid/html/index.html">
76 another tutorial.</a> In fact, the two codes only differ
77 in the specification of the names of the tetgen files that describe the
78 unstructured fluid mesh, and the enumeration of the boundaries.
79 We also disabled the gravitational body force.
80 Therefore, we will not discuss the driver code in detail.

87 \subsection finer Generating finer meshes
88 As acknowledged at the beginning of the tutorial, the solid mesh
89 is far too coarse for the simulation results to be accurate.
90 As an exercise, follow the instructions in \c oomph-lib's
91 <a href="../../../meshes/mesh_from_vmtk/html/index.html">VMTK tutorial</a>
92 to generate a finer mesh. We are hoping to make the image files
93 used in this tutorial available soon. Please
94 <a href="../../../contact/html/index.html">contact us</a>
95 if you can't wait. Remember that you will have to update the
96 enumeration of the domain boundaries if you change the mesh.

101 \subsection bc Making the boundary conditions more "realistic"
102 In the example shown above we applied boundary conditions that held
103 the "ends" of the bifurcation in a fixed position. These boundary
104 conditions are obviously rather artificial as no such constraints
105 exist in the human body. Unfortunately, it is not entirely clear
106 how to come up with boundary conditions that are genuinely "more
107 realistic". One
108 way to ensure that the (inevitably artificial) boundary conditions
109 have a weak(er) effect on the deformation of the "region of interest"
110 would be to attach "flow extensions" to the in- and outflow
111 cross-sections, using the technique described in \c oomph-lib's
112 <a href="../../../meshes/mesh_from_vmtk/html/index.html#add_extensions">
113 VMTK tutorial.</a>
114 Alternatively, we could constrain the displacement of a much smaller
115 number of material points -- rigid body motions can be suppressed by
116 pinning the position of just three nodes. We encourage you to
117 explore these options yourself.
