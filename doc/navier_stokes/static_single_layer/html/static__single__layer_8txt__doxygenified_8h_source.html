3 \mainpage Demo problem: The static free surface bounding a layer of viscous fluid.
4 
5 \section overview Overview of the problem
6 
7  We consider an open rectangular container of unit width
8  containing a still viscous fluid of
9  prescribed volume \f$ \mathcal{V} \f$
10  that meets the wall of the container at a contact
11  angle \f$ \theta_{c} \f$. The external fluid is assumed to be
12  inviscid and massless and acts only through an external pressure,
13  \f$ P_{\mbox{ext}} \f$, at
14  the bounding (free) surface of the viscous fluid. In the absence of
15  any body forces or external forcing, a
16  static solution is obtained in which the velocity
17  field is zero, the fluid pressure is a constant,
18  \f$ P_{\mbox{fluid}} \f$,
19  and free surface is
20  of constant curvature (an arc of a circle in
21  two-dimensions), set by the
22  contact angle and the geometry of the domain. From simple geometry, the
23  mean curvature of the interface in the present problem
24  is \f$ \kappa = 1/r = 2\cos\theta_{c} \f$, see the figure below.
25  The pressure difference
26  across the interface, \f$ \Delta P = P_{\mbox{ext}} -
27  P_{\mbox{fluid}} \f$,
28  then follows from the dynamic boundary condition.
29 
30 \image html sketch.gif "Sketch of the problem: the static interface curvature is set by the geometry "
31 \image latex sketch.eps "Sketch of the problem: the static interface curvature is set by the geometry " width=0.75\textwidth
32 
33  The governing equations are the Navier--Stokes equations together
34  with the free surface boundary conditions at the upper surface, see
35  <A HREF="../../single_layer_free_surface/html/index.html">
36 another tutorial </A> for further details. As in that tutorial,
37  we also compute the deformation of the internal fluid mesh by using a
38  pseudo-solid node-update strategy.
39 
40  For simplicity, we assume symmetry of the domain about
41  the vertical centreline of the container, which we choose to be the
42  \f$ x_{2} \f$-axis; hence, the initial
43  computational domain is \f$ x_{1} \in [0,0.5],\ x_{2} \in [0,1] \f$.
44  The fluid boundary conditions are no slip, \f$ u_{i} = 0 \f$, on the container
45  boundaries at \f$ x_{2} =0 \f$ and \f$ x_{1} = 0.5; \f$ and a symmetry
46  condition at \f$ x_{1} = 0 \f$. Under the standard
47  non-dimensionalisation the pressure difference over the interface is
48  \f$ \Delta P = 2\cos\theta_{c}/Ca \f$,
49  where \f$ Ca \f$ is the capillary number,
50  also defined in the other tutorial. In the present (static) example, the
51  capillary number is a simple multiplicative factor that sets the
52  pressure scale.
53 
54  There remain the two additional constraints that must be enforced:
55 -# The fluid volume must be \f$ \mathcal{V} \f$.
56 -# The contact angle where the fluid meets the container wall at
57 \f$ x_{1} = 0.5 \f$ must be \f$ \theta_{c}. \f$
58 .
59 
60 \section vol_const Enforcing the volume constraint
61 
62  For capillary-static problems, the fluid velocity field is zero and
63  the continuity equation \f$ \mbox{\boldmath$\nabla\cdot
64  u$} = \mbox{\boldmath$0$} \f$ is trivially satisfied. Thus,
65  conservation of mass is not guaranteed without an additional
66  constraint. Mathematically speaking, the system is underdetermined
67  and the equation associated with a
68  pressure degree of freedom (either a fluid pressure or the external
69  pressure) can be replaced by the additional volume constraint,
70  specified by the equation:
71 
72  \f[ \frac{1}{2} \oint \mbox{\boldmath$x\cdot n$} \mbox{d} s =
73  \frac{1}{2} \int \hspace{-0.25cm} \int
74 \mbox{\boldmath$\nabla\cdot x$} \mbox{d} A =
75 \int \hspace{-0.25cm} \int
76  \mbox{d} A = \mathcal{V}. \f]
77 
78  MATTHIAS, see also the droplet/bubble problems.
79 
80  Perhaps the most natural implementation in the present problem
81  is to fix a reference pressure value
82  within the fluid and then to treat the external pressure as a degree
83  of freedom with the
84  volume constraint as the associated equation. Alternatively, the
85  external pressure can be fixed at a reference pressure and an
86  internal fluid pressure degree of freedom is then associated with the
87  volume constraint. We shall demonstrate both methods in the driver code below.
88 
89  Computation of the above equation associated with the volume constraint is
90  performed by specialised \c LineVolumeConstraintBoundingElements that
91  must be attached to all boundaries that enclose the fluid.
92 
93 \section contact_angle Enforcing the contact angle constraint
94 
95  Here the ``natural'' contact-angle boundary condition arises from the
96  weak form of the dynamic boundary condition after integration using
97  the surface divergence theorem. This leads to a term of the
98  form
99 
100  \f[
101  \frac{1}{Ca}
102  \left[m_{i}\psi\right]_{\mbox{\boldmath$x$}_l}^{\mbox{\boldmath$x$}_{r}}
103  \f]
104 
105  in the momentum equations associated with the boundaries of the free
106  surface, where \f$ \mbox{\boldmath$m$} \f$ is a unit vector
107  tangential to the free surface at those boundaries.
108 
109  At the (left-hand) symmetry boundary, \f$ \mbox{\boldmath$x$}_{l} \f$,
110  we neglect this additional term in the momentum equation
111  associated with the tangential direction,
112  which ensures that the interface is (weakly) normal to the
113  symmetry boundary.
114  At the (right-hand) container boundary,
115  \f$ \mbox{\boldmath$x$}_{r} \f$,
116  the contact angle could be
117  enforced by adding a boundary term with prescribed \f$
118  \mbox{\boldmath$m$} \f$. In general,
119  \f[ \mbox{\boldmath$m$} = \cos\theta_{c} \mbox{\boldmath$n$}_{w} +
120  \sin\theta_{c} \mbox{\boldmath$t$}_{w}, \f]
121  where \f$ \mbox{\boldmath$n$}_{w} \f$ is the normal vector to the
122  bounding wall directed out of the fluid and \f$
123  \mbox{\boldmath$t$}_{w} \f$ is the tangent vector to the wall. In the
124  present example, \f$ \mbox{\boldmath$n$}_{w} = (1,0) \f$ and \f$
125  \mbox{\boldmath$t$}_{w} = (0,1) \f$, which gives
126  \f$ \mbox{\boldmath$m$} =
127  (\cos\theta_{c}, \sin\theta_{c}) \f$.
128 
129  Unfortunately, the no-slip boundary condition at the wall
130  means that the momentum equation is not assembled at the interface
131  boundaries. If we wanted to use this
132  method of (weak) enforcement of the contact angle condition, the
133  velocity degrees of freedom at the node on the right-hand end of
134  the free surface must be unpinned, but the no-slip condition will then
135  not be imposed. In fact, this problem is deeper than it might appear
136  and is a manifestation of the failure of the continuum hypothesis to treat
137  dynamic (moving) contact lines. The correct (mathematical) treatment
138  of that problem is still unresolved, but in this static problem,
139  there is an alternative treatment.
140 
141  Here, we can make use of the fact that the kinematic
142  condition is also trivially satisfied because the velocity field is
143  zero. Thus, we replace the kinematic condition associated with the
144  right-most node on the interface by the (strong) condition that
145  \f[ \cos\theta_{c} = \mbox{\boldmath$n\cdot n$}_{w}, \f]
146  where \f$ \mbox{\boldmath$n$} \f$ is the outer unit normal to the free
147  surface and \f$ \mbox{\boldmath$n$}_{w} \f$ is the unit normal to the
148  bounding wall directed out of the fluid, as before.
149 
150  The imposition of the contact angle condition is implemented within
151  \c FluidInterfaceBoundingElements that are \c FaceElements of the \c
152  FluidInterfaceElements; in other words, they are elements that are two
153  spatial dimensions lower than the bulk elements. In two-dimensional
154  problems, the \c FluidInterfaceBoundingElement's are \c PointElements.

175 \section driver The driver code
176 
177  We shall solve the problem twice, once using the external pressure as
178  the degree of freedom associated with the volume constraint and once
179  using an internal fluid pressure degree of freedom instead. The
180  choice of which degree of freedom to use is determined by the boolean
181  flag passed to the problem constructor.
182  The driver consists of a simple loop to cover both possibilities,
183  construction of the problem and then a call to run the parameter
184  study with different output directories in each case.
185 
186  \skipline start_of_main
187  \until {
188  \skipline Solve the pseudosolid
189  \until end_of_main
190 
191  The elements are \c Hijacked \c PseudoSolidNodeUpdate \c
192  QCrouzeixRaviart type elements, where the \c Hijacked actually required only in
193  the case when an internal pressure degree of freedom is to be used to
194  enforce the volume constraint, see below.

197 \section problem The problem class
198 
199 The problem class consists of a constructor that takes the
200 aforementioned boolean flag to determine which pressure degree of
201 freedom to use for the volume constraint; a destructor to clean up all
202 memory allocated during the problem construction; a function that performs a
203 parameter study, decreasing the contact angle from \f$ \theta_{c} =
204 90^{\circ} \f$ in steps of \f$ 5^{\circ} \f$; and a function that
205 documents the solution.
206 
207 \dontinclude static_single_layer.cc
208 \skipline start_of_pseudo_elastic_class
209 \until doc_solution(
210 
211  There are also three helper functions that are used to create the
212 \c FaceElements used to enforce the additional boundary conditions and
213 constraints. The function \c create_free_surface_elements() adds
214 \c ElasticLineFluidInterfaceElements to the upper boundary (boundary
215 2) of the mesh, exactly as in <A HREF="../../single_layer_free_surface/html/index.html"> another
216 tutorial </A>. The function
217 \c create_volume_constraint_elements() constructs the elements required to
218 enforce the volume constraint. Finally, the function

220 enforces the contact angle condition at the solid wall of the
221 container.
222 
223 \until create_contact_angle

225  Finally, the class has member data corresponding to
226  the physical variables of the
227  problem and provides permanent storage for pointers to the
228  data that stores the external pressure and the data for the pressure
229  degree of freedom that will be "traded" for the volume constraint.
230  In additional, an output stream and pointers
231  to the different meshes that will be assembled in the problem are
232  also included.

236 \section constructor The problem constructor
237 
238  The constructor first initialises the member data and sets the outer
239  unit normal to the wall to be \f$ (1,0) \f$. The initial contact
240  angle is set to be \f$ 90^{\circ} \f$, so that the interface will
241  be horizontal.
242 
243 \skipline start_of_constructor
244 \until Wall_normal[1]
245 
246  Next, a \f$ 4 \times 4 \f$ element rectangular mesh is constructed on
247  the undeformed domain.
248 
249 \until ElasticRectangularQuadMesh<ELEMENT>(
250 
251  A \c Data object containing a single value is constructed to store
252  the external pressure and the initial value is set.
253 
254 \until set_value(0,Pext);
255 
256 
257  \anchor hijack
258  We next setup the reference pressure and traded pressure. If we are
259  using the external pressure as the reference pressure, then we pin it
260  and set an internal pressure degree of freedom as the "traded"
261  pressure. In order to use an internal pressure degree of freedom as
262  the "traded" pressure, we must ensure that the original associated
263  (continuity) equation is not assembled, but that the degree of freedom is
264  still treated a fluid pressure variable when assembling the other
265  residuals within the element. In addition,
266  we will also need direct access to the data and its global equation
267  number. The appropriate machinery is provided by the \c Hijacked wrapper class
268  which allows variables within an underlying element to be
269  "hijacked". The bulk elements are of \c QCrouzeixRaviart type,
270  which means that the pressure variables are internal data and we
271  hijack the first of these in the first element (although any would
272  do). The function \c hijack_internal_value(..) instructs the bulk
273  element to null out any contributions to the residuals and Jacobian
274  matrix associated
275  with that global equation number and returns a "custom" \c Data
276  object that consists of a single value corresponding to the
277  "hijacked" value and its global equation number. The "custom" \c Data object
278  can then be used as external data in other elements that
279  assemble the new residuals. It is important that any hijacked degrees
280  of freedom must have residual contributions provided by another
281  element otherwise the system Jacobian matrix will be singular. For
282  more details about "hijacking" see <a href="../../../hijacking/html/index.html"> this document. </a>
283 
284 \until }
285 
286  If we are using an internal pressure as the reference pressure then
287  the external pressure is a degree of freedom and must be added as
288  global data of the problem. The "traded" pressure is the external
289  pressure and an internal fluid pressure is fixed to have
290  the value zero.
291 
292 \until }
293 
294  We then build the constitutive law that determines the fluid mesh
295  motion and assign it to the bulk elements.
296 
297 \until }
298 
299  We next set the fluid boundary conditions by pinning both velocity
300  components on the base and side of the container (boundaries 0 and 1)
301  and the horizontal velocity only on the symmetry line (boundary
302  3). The upper free surface (boundary 2) remains free.
303 
304 \until end_of_fluid_boundary
305 
306  The boundary conditions for the pseudo-solid mesh have
307  a certain amount of ambiguity. We choose the least restrictive
308  boundary conditions and enforce that the nodes cannot move away from
309  the container boundaries, but can slide tangentially. Thus the
310  vertical displacement is pinned on boundary 0 and the horizontal
311  displacement is pinned on boundaries 1 and 3.
312 
313 \until end_of_solid_boundary
314 
315  In order to reduce the number of degrees of freedom we further
316  constrain the nodes to move only in the vertical direction by pinning
317  all horizontal displacements.
318 
319 \until end_of_constraint
320 
321  Finally, we call the helper functions to construct the additional
322  elements, add all sub meshes to the problem, build the global mesh
323  and assign equation numbers.
324 
325 \until end_of_constructor

336 \subsection vol Volume constraint elements
337 
338  The volume constraint condition is enforced by two different types of
339  element. A single \c GeneralisedElement that stores the prescribed
340  volume and the \c Data that is traded for the volume constraint. We
341  first create this \c VolumeConstraintElement and add it to the
342  \c Mesh addressed by the \c Volume_constraint_mesh_pt.
343 
344  \skipline start_of_create_volume
345  \until (vol_constraint_element);
346 
347  We next build and attach \c
348  ElasticLineVolumeConstraintBoundingElements to all the boundaries of
349  the domain. These elements compute the contribution to the boundary
350  integral with integrand \f$ \mbox{\boldmath$x\cdot n$} \f$,
351  described above. The sign
352  convention is chosen so that these element will return a positive
353  volume. The "master" \c VolumeConstraintElement must be passed to
354  each \c FaceElement so that the \c Data traded for the volume
355  constraint is consistent between all elements.
356 
357  \until end_of_create_volume

359 \subsection contact Contact angle elements
360 
361  The single contact angle (point) element is constructed from the free
362  surface element via the function \c make_bounding_element(..) which
363  takes an integer specifying which face to use. In this problem,
364  the right-hand-side (face 1) of the final free surface
365  element is the contact point. The contact angle, capillary number and
366  wall normal must be passed to the element which is then added to the
367  appropriate \c Mesh. In general the wall normal can very with
368  position, so it is passed as a function pointer.

402 \section comments Comments and Exercises

404 \subsection com Comments
405 
406 - The driver code also contains an alternative formulation in which \c
407  SpineElements are used to determine the deformation of the fluid
408  mesh. The formulation is somewhat more cumbersome because the permitted
409  deformation must be specified for each different problem and is
410  specifically tied to a given domain geometry. The overall approach
411  is exactly the same and large sections of the code are identical.
412  The advantage of using \c SpineElements is that fewer degrees of freedom are
413  required to update the position of the fluid mesh.
414 
415 - The weak imposition of the contact angle condition is enabled by
416 passing an optional boolean flag when setting the contact angle
417 \code
418  el_pt->set_contact_angle(&Angle,false);
419 \endcode
420  If a contact angle is not set then the \c
421  FluidInterfaceBoundingElement will construct and add the appropriate boundary
422  term to the momentum equation which may be required if another
423  equation is used to prescribe the contact angle indirectly.
424 
425 - An equivalent problem using
426  <a href="../../../axisym_navier_stokes/axi_static_cap/html/index.html">
427 an axisymmetric formulation </a>
428 of the governing equations is also included within the
429 library. The only physical difference is that the
430 pressure drop in the axisymmetric problem is twice that of the two-dimensional
431 problem because the mean curvature of the sphere is twice that of a
432 circle of equivalent radius.

437 -# Confirm that the computed pressure differences agree with the
