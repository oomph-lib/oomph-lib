3 \mainpage Demo problem: Flow of a fluid film down an inclined plane
4 
5  The two-dimensional flow of a free surface down an inclined plane is
6  a simple exact solution of the Navier--Stokes equations. We describe
7  two different ways of solving the problem using either spines or a
8  pseudo-elastic method to define the bulk mesh motion. Reassuringly,
9  the results are the same irrespective of the method chosen.

14 \section form Problem formulation
15 
16 \image html sketch.gif "A film of incompressible viscous fluid of a given thickness flows down a plane inclined at a prescribed angle to the gravitational field. "
17 \image latex sketch.eps "A film of incompressible viscous fluid of a given thickness flows down a plane inclined at a prescribed angle to the gravitational field. " width=0.75\textwidth
18 
19  Formulating the problem in coordinates tangential (\f$x^{*}\f$) and
20  normal (\f$y^{*}\f$) to the plane and assuming that the flow is steady
21  and only in the tangential direction, but independent of the tangential
22  coordinate, reduces the momentum equations to
23 
24 <CENTER>
25 \f[ \frac{\partial p^*}{\partial x^*} = \rho \,g \sin\alpha +
26 \mu\,\frac{\partial^{2} u^*}{\partial y^{*2}}, \f]
27 \f[ \frac{\partial p^*}{\partial y^*} = -\rho \,g \cos\alpha, \f]
28 </CENTER>
29 where \f$u^*\f$ is the velocity component tangential to the plane and
30 \f$p^*\f$ is the fluid pressure. Note that
31 the continuity equation is automatically satisfied.
32 
33 We non-dimensionalise using the only length-scale in the
34 problem \f$H\f$, choosing the viscous scale for the pressure and
35 choosing a reference velocity scale \f$U\f$:
36 \f[ x^{*} = H x,\qquad y^{*} = H y,\qquad u^* = U u,\qquad p^* = \mu
37 U/H,\f]
38 and the governing equations become
39 <CENTER>
40 \f[ \frac{\partial p}{\partial x} = \frac{\rho \,g H^{2}}{\mu U} \sin\alpha +
41  \frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial y^{2}} = \frac{Re}{Fr} \sin \alpha +
42  \frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial y^{2}}, \f]
43 \f[ \frac{\partial p}{\partial y} = -\frac{\rho \,g H^{2}}{\mu U}
44  \cos\alpha = - \frac{Re}{Fr} \cos\alpha. \f]
45 </CENTER>
46 The dimensionless grouping \f$ \rho g H^{2} / (\mu U) \f$ represents
47 the ratio of gravitational forces to viscous forces and we choose to
48 identify it as a Reynolds number \f$ Re = \rho U H / \mu\f$ divided by
49 a Froude number \f$ Fr = U^{2} / (g H)\f$.
50 
51 We proceed by assuming that the flow is driven entirely by the
52 gravitational body force and that there is no additional tangential
53 pressure gradient. Then, integrating the tangential momentum balance
54 twice and using the boundary conditions of no-slip at the plane (\f$y=0\f$)and
55 that the free surface (\f$y=1\f$) is tangentially stress-free gives
56 \f[ u = \frac{1}{2}\frac{Re}{Fr}\sin\alpha \left(2 y - y^{2}\right). \f]
57 Integrating the normal momentum balance and setting the reference
58 external pressure to be zero at the free surface gives
59 \f[ p = \frac{Re}{Fr}\cos\alpha (1 - y).\f]
60 
61 Finally, we specify a "natural" velocity scale by setting \f$Re/Fr =
62 2\f$, corresponding to the velocity of the free-surface for a vertical
63 film (\f$\alpha = \pi/2\f$).
64 
65 We shall assess the stability of the flat-film solution by applying a
66 small, short-duration perturbation to the wall velocity
67 and evolving the system in time. If the interface is stable,
68 the perturbation should decay, if not it should grow. A linear
69 stability analysis for this problem was performed by Benjamin (1957)
70 and Yih (1963), who both found that for long waves in the absence of
71 surface tension, the interface was unstable when
72 \f[ Re > \frac{5}{4} \sin\alpha. \f]
73 (If you read the papers you will see that
74 the Reynolds number was defined such that the
75 average fluid downslope velocity was one; to convert to our Reynolds
76 number, we must multiply by 3/2.)
77 
78 The figure below shows the time evolution of the interface on a slope of
79 \f$ \pi/4 \f$ for
80 Reynolds numbers of zero (red line) and \f$ 4 \sin\alpha \f$ (green
81 line). The perturbation wavenumber is \f$ K = 0.1 \f$ and the interface rapidly develops waves that grow as they are
82 convected downslope for the higher Reynolds number, but decay when \f$
83 Re = 0 \f$.

97 \subsection bound A note on the boundary conditions
98 
99  Resolving the above analytic solution in a finite computational
100 domain requires some thought about boundary conditions. We are only
101 ever free to set one pressure value and setting the external pressure
102 to zero fixes the pressure within the fluid. The boundary conditions
103 at the plane are those of no-slip and at the free-surface
104 the usual dynamic and kinematic conditions apply.
105 Nonetheless, we have a number of
106 possibilities for the boundary conditions at the "artificial" upstream
107 and downstream computational boundaries.
108  - Prescribe periodic boundary conditions.
109  - Prescribe the velocity profile as a Dirichlet condition at both
110  ends.
111  - Prescribe the appropriate hydrostatic pressure gradient and zero
112  normal velocity.
113 .
114 
115 We have chosen the last option, in which case the hydrostatic
116  pressure gradient must be consistent with the external pressure. In
117  other words, the pressure must be zero at the free surface
118  (\f$y=0\f$). Changing the external pressure would correspond to
119  changing the film thickness, so the external pressure is directly
120  responsible for enforcing a specific volume constraint, unless
121  \f$\alpha = \pi/2\f$. When \f$\alpha = \pi/2\f$ there is no variation in
122  hydrostatic pressure through the film and its thickness is
123  not specified by the external pressure.
124 
125 We must also worry about the boundary conditions on the free surface
126 itself and we choose to impose a contact angle condition of \f$ \pi/2 \f$
127 at the upstream end, which ensures that the film remains flat.
128 At the downstream end, we add a line tension term that arises
129 from use of the surface divergence theorem to integrate the
130 contribution of the dynamic boundary condition. This term can be used to
131 enforce contact angle conditions in a weak formulation, but here we
132 simply add the term using the angle calculated from the current
133 position of the free surface.

211  remaining boundaries will be the inlet (3) and outlet (1). Below we shall

213  \c ElasticMesh suitable for free-surface problems.

355 \section exercises Exercises
356 -# Confirm that the steady solution agrees with the exact solution.
357 -# Investigate what happens when the angle is varied. What happens
358 when the angle is set to zero? What happens when the angle is set to
359 \f$\pi/2\f$?
360 -# What happens if the hydrostatic pressure boundary conditions are
361 not applied?
362 -# How does the stability of the system to the perturbation change
363 with angle, \f$ Ca \f$ and \f$ K \f$? Are the results in agreement
364 with the theoretical predictions?
365 -# Are the results independent of the length of the domain?
366 -# Compare the spine-based and pseudo-elastic-based formulations? What
367 is the same and what is different? Which method do you prefer?
