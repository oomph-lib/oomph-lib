3\mainpage Demo problem: Flow of a fluid film down an inclined plane
4
5 The two-dimensional flow of a free surface down an inclined plane is
6 a simple exact solution of the Navier--Stokes equations. We describe
7 two different ways of solving the problem using either spines or a
8 pseudo-elastic method to define the bulk mesh motion. Reassuringly,
9 the results are the same irrespective of the method chosen.

14\section form Problem formulation
15
16\image html sketch.gif "A film of incompressible viscous fluid of a given thickness flows down a plane inclined at a prescribed angle to the gravitational field. "
17\image latex sketch.eps "A film of incompressible viscous fluid of a given thickness flows down a plane inclined at a prescribed angle to the gravitational field. " width=0.75\textwidth
18
19 Formulating the problem in coordinates tangential (\f$x^{*}\f$) and
20 normal (\f$y^{*}\f$) to the plane and assuming that the flow is steady
21 and only in the tangential direction, but independent of the tangential
22 coordinate, reduces the momentum equations to
23
24<CENTER>
25\f[ \frac{\partial p^*}{\partial x^*} = \rho \,g \sin\alpha +
26\mu\,\frac{\partial^{2} u^*}{\partial y^{*2}}, \f]
27\f[ \frac{\partial p^*}{\partial y^*} = -\rho \,g \cos\alpha, \f]
28</CENTER>
29where \f$u^*\f$ is the velocity component tangential to the plane and
30\f$p^*\f$ is the fluid pressure. Note that
31the continuity equation is automatically satisfied.
32
33We non-dimensionalise using the only length-scale in the
34problem \f$H\f$, choosing the viscous scale for the pressure and
35choosing a reference velocity scale \f$U\f$:
36\f[ x^{*} = H x,\qquad y^{*} = H y,\qquad u^* = U u,\qquad p^* = \mu
37U/H,\f]
38and the governing equations become
39<CENTER>
40\f[ \frac{\partial p}{\partial x} = \frac{\rho \,g H^{2}}{\mu U} \sin\alpha +
41 \frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial y^{2}} = \frac{Re}{Fr} \sin \alpha +
42 \frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial y^{2}}, \f]
43\f[ \frac{\partial p}{\partial y} = -\frac{\rho \,g H^{2}}{\mu U}
44 \cos\alpha = - \frac{Re}{Fr} \cos\alpha. \f]
45</CENTER>
46The dimensionless grouping \f$ \rho g H^{2} / (\mu U) \f$ represents
47the ratio of gravitational forces to viscous forces and we choose to
48identify it as a Reynolds number \f$ Re = \rho U H / \mu\f$ divided by
49a Froude number \f$ Fr = U^{2} / (g H)\f$.
50
51We proceed by assuming that the flow is driven entirely by the
52gravitational body force and that there is no additional tangential
53pressure gradient. Then, integrating the tangential momentum balance
54twice and using the boundary conditions of no-slip at the plane (\f$y=0\f$)and
55that the free surface (\f$y=1\f$) is tangentially stress-free gives
56\f[ u = \frac{1}{2}\frac{Re}{Fr}\sin\alpha \left(2 y - y^{2}\right). \f]
57Integrating the normal momentum balance and setting the reference
58external pressure to be zero at the free surface gives
59\f[ p = \frac{Re}{Fr}\cos\alpha (1 - y).\f]
60
61Finally, we specify a "natural" velocity scale by setting \f$Re/Fr =
622\f$, corresponding to the velocity of the free-surface for a vertical
63film (\f$\alpha = \pi/2\f$).
64
65We shall assess the stability of the flat-film solution by applying a
66small, short-duration perturbation to the wall velocity
67and evolving the system in time. If the interface is stable,
68the perturbation should decay, if not it should grow. A linear
69stability analysis for this problem was performed by Benjamin (1957)
70and Yih (1963), who both found that for long waves in the absence of
71surface tension, the interface was unstable when
72\f[ Re > \frac{5}{4} \sin\alpha. \f]
73(If you read the papers you will see that
74the Reynolds number was defined such that the
75average fluid downslope velocity was one; to convert to our Reynolds
76number, we must multiply by 3/2.)
77
78The figure below shows the time evolution of the interface on a slope of
79\f$ \pi/4 \f$ for
80Reynolds numbers of zero (red line) and \f$ 4 \sin\alpha \f$ (green
81line). The perturbation wavenumber is \f$ K = 0.1 \f$ and the interface rapidly develops waves that grow as they are
82convected downslope for the higher Reynolds number, but decay when \f$
83Re = 0 \f$.
84
85\image html animated_surface.gif "Time evolution (or static snapshot at t = 7.5) of the interface shape for Re = 0 (Red) and 4 sin(alpha) (Green). "
86\image latex animated_surface.eps "Time evolution (or static snapshot at t = 7.5) of the interface shape for Re = 0 (Red) and 4 sin(alpha) (Green). " width=0.75\textwidth
87
88The decay rate of the interfacial perturbation at \f$ Re = 0 \f$ is
89slow, but can be seen in the next figure, which shows the height of
90the interface at the downstream end of the domain plotted against
91time. The domain is chosen so that it will contain three waves and the
92decay or growth of successive crests and troughs can be seen.

97\subsection bound A note on the boundary conditions
98
99 Resolving the above analytic solution in a finite computational
100domain requires some thought about boundary conditions. We are only
101ever free to set one pressure value and setting the external pressure
102to zero fixes the pressure within the fluid. The boundary conditions
103at the plane are those of no-slip and at the free-surface
104the usual dynamic and kinematic conditions apply.
105Nonetheless, we have a number of
106possibilities for the boundary conditions at the "artificial" upstream
107and downstream computational boundaries.
108 - Prescribe periodic boundary conditions.
109 - Prescribe the velocity profile as a Dirichlet condition at both
110 ends.
111 - Prescribe the appropriate hydrostatic pressure gradient and zero
112 normal velocity.
113.
114
115We have chosen the last option, in which case the hydrostatic
116 pressure gradient must be consistent with the external pressure. In
117 other words, the pressure must be zero at the free surface
118 (\f$y=0\f$). Changing the external pressure would correspond to
119 changing the film thickness, so the external pressure is directly
120 responsible for enforcing a specific volume constraint, unless
121 \f$\alpha = \pi/2\f$. When \f$\alpha = \pi/2\f$ there is no variation in
122 hydrostatic pressure through the film and its thickness is
123 not specified by the external pressure.
124
125We must also worry about the boundary conditions on the free surface
126itself and we choose to impose a contact angle condition of \f$ \pi/2 \f$
127at the upstream end, which ensures that the film remains flat.
128At the downstream end, we add a line tension term that arises
129from use of the surface divergence theorem to integrate the
130contribution of the dynamic boundary condition. This term can be used to
131enforce contact angle conditions in a weak formulation, but here we
132simply add the term using the angle calculated from the current
133position of the free surface.

138\section global Global parameters and functions
139 The global parameters are the Reynolds number,
140 the dimensionless grouping \f$Re/Fr\f$, the angle of inclination of
141 the slope \f$\alpha\f$, the direction of the gravity vector \f$G\f$
142 and the capillary number \f$Ca\f$, which only influences the dynamics.
143

145\skipline Global_Physical_Variables

147
148 The hydrostatic pressure field is specified as an applied
149 traction. At the outlet (inlet), the outer unit normal is in the
150 positive (negative)
151 \f$x\f$ direction and so the required traction is given by \f$-p\f$
152 (\f$p\f$),
153 \f[ \mbox{\boldmath$t$}|_{\mbox{outlet}} = (- (Re/Fr)\cos\alpha (1 -
154 y), 0), \qquad
155 \mbox{\boldmath$t$}|_{\mbox{inlet}} = ((Re/Fr)\cos\alpha (1 -
156 y), 0).\f]
157 These tractions are specified by the two different functions
158 \skipline hydrostatic
159 \until end of traction
160 Note that \c G [ \c 1 ] is the component of the gravitational body force in
161 the vertical direction, so \f$ G[1] = - \cos\alpha\f$.
162
163 We must also specify the direction of the normals (directed out of the
164 fluid) to the notional walls that form the inlet and outlet
165 and a contact angle of \f$ \pi /2 \f$ that will be
166 used as a boundary condition on the free surface at the upstream end
167 of the domain. In this case the normal to the inlet is in the

207\section mesh The mesh classes
208
209 The base mesh class is the \c SimpleRectangularQuadMesh: boundary 0
210 will be the wall; boundary 2 will be the free surface; and the
211 remaining boundaries will be the inlet (3) and outlet (1). Below we shall
212 demonstrate how to convert an existing mesh into a \c SpineMesh and
213 \c ElasticMesh suitable for free-surface problems.
214

217SimpleRectangularQuadMesh and adds vertical spines to the Nodes
218within the mesh in the constructor.
219Note that the resulting mesh is essentially the same
220as the \c SingleLayerSpineMesh, but has a somewhat simpler interface.
221

244\section problem The problem classes
245\subsection generic_prob The generic problem
246 For ease of exposition, all generic functionality is included
247 in the \c InclinedPlaneProblem class, which is templated by the bulk
248 \c ELEMENT and the \c INTERFACE_ELEMENT. The class includes storage
249 for the different sub-meshes: Bulk, the Traction elements associated
250 with the inlet and outlet, the (free) Surface elements and the point
251 elements associated with the ends of the interface. In addition, a
252 string \c Output_prefix is used to distinguish
253 between the output files from different formulations.
254 \dontinclude inclined_plane.cc
255 \skipline Generic problem class
256 \until Output_prefix
257
258 The time-dependent perturbation is introduced in the function \c
259 actions_before_implicit_timestep(), which sets the vertical
260 velocity on the wall (boundary 0)
261 \f[ v = \epsilon \sin(K x) t \mbox{e}^{-t} \f]
262 \skipline actions_before_implicit_timestep()
263\until end_of_actions_before_implicit
264
265 The function \c make_traction_elements() creates
266 \c NavierStokesTractionElement s adjacent to the mesh boundaries 3
267 (the inlet) and 1 (the inlet). These elements are added to the \c
268 Mesh \c Traction_mesh_pt, which is itself constructed
269 in the function and pointers to the
270 appropriate traction functions are assigned.
271 \skipline traction boundary elements
272 \until end of make_traction_elements
273
274 The function \c make_free_surface_elements() creates the appropriate
275\c INTERFACE_ELEMENTs adjacent to the free surface (boundary 2), sets
276the capillary number and also creates free-surface boundary elements
277at the left- and right-hand ends of the interface. If these "point"
278elements are not included then the surface tension is not
279applied correctly at the edges of the domain. The contact angle is set
280to be the value \c Inlet_Angle at the left-hand edge of the domain.
281\skipline Make the free
282\until end of make_free_surface_elements

355\section exercises Exercises
356-# Confirm that the steady solution agrees with the exact solution.
357-# Investigate what happens when the angle is varied. What happens
358when the angle is set to zero? What happens when the angle is set to
359\f$\pi/2\f$?
360-# What happens if the hydrostatic pressure boundary conditions are
361not applied?
362-# How does the stability of the system to the perturbation change
363with angle, \f$ Ca \f$ and \f$ K \f$? Are the results in agreement
364with the theoretical predictions?
365-# Are the results independent of the length of the domain?
366-# Compare the spine-based and pseudo-elastic-based formulations? What
367is the same and what is different? Which method do you prefer?
