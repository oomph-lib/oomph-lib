3 \mainpage The Bretherton problem: An air finger propagates into a 2D channel
4 
5 This tutorial discusses the solution of the classical
6 Bretherton problem in which an air-finger is driven steadily into a 2D
7 fluid-filled, rigid-walled channel.
8 
9 
10 The driver code listed below was originally developed as a
11 test-bed for the paper
12 
13 <CENTER>
14 Hazel, A.L. & Heil, M. (2008) The influence of gravity on the steady
15 propagation of a semi-infinite bubble into a flexible channel.
16 Physics of Fluids 20, 092109
17 <a href="http://www.maths.manchester.ac.uk/~mheil/MATTHIAS/PDF/HazelHeilPoF2008.pdf">(pdf reprint)</a>
18 </CENTER>
19 
20 in which we studied the elastic-walled equivalent, paying particular
21 attention to the effect of transverse gravity which plays quite an important
22 role in this problem.
23 
24 Compared to other tutorials, there is no detailed discussion of the
25 driver code itself because the implementation is somewhat messy.
26 However, given that the driver code is (of course!) very well documented
27 you should be able to figure out what's going on once you've read
28 through the discussion of the problem formulation and our brief
29 comments on the \ref implement.

31 if you need more information.

34 \section problem The problem
35 
36 
37 The sketch below shows the problem setup: An (inviscid) air
38 finger propagates at a steady speed \f$ U \f$ into a 2D rigid-walled,
39 fluid-filled channel of half-width \f$ H_0^* \f$.
40 In the process it displaces some of the viscous fluid (of
41 density \f$ \rho \f$, viscosity
42 \f$ \mu \f$ and surface tension \f$ \sigma^* \f$)
43 and deposits a film of thickness \f$ h_0^* \f$ on the channel walls.
44 [Note that, for the sake of simplicity, we ignore the effect of
45 transverse gravity in this discussion; the driver code listed
46 below allows the specification of a gravitational body force which will break
47 the up-down symmetry of the solution.]
48 
49 
50 \image html bretherton_sketch.gif "Sketch of the problem setup "
51 \image latex bretherton_sketch.eps "Sketch of the problem setup " width=0.75\textwidth
52 
53 
54 We non-dimensionalise the velocities on \f$ U \f$, the lengths
55 on \f$ H_0^* \f$ and the pressure on the viscous scale, \f$ \mu U /
56 H_0^* \f$ and solve the problem in a frame moving with the
57 velocity of the finger. The flow is then governed by the
58 non-dimensional Navier-Stokes equations
59 \f[
60 Re \ u_j\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j} = -\frac{\partial p}{\partial x_i} + \
61 \frac{\partial }{\partial x_j }\left( \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j} + \frac{\partial u_j}{\partial x_i} \right)
62 \f]
63 and the continuity equation
64 \f[
65 \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_i}=0,
66 \f]
67 where the Reynolds number is defined as \f$ Re=U H_0 \rho/\mu \f$.
68 On the free fluid surface, whose outer unit normal we
69 denote by \f$ {\bf n} \f$, the fluid normal velocity vanishes,
70 \f[
71 {\bf u} \cdot {\bf n} = 0 \mbox{\ \ \ on the air-liquid interface}.
72 \f]
73 We measure the fluid pressure \f$ p \f$ relative to the bubble
74 pressure
75 \f$ p_b \f$. Then
76 the dynamic boundary condition implies that
77 \f[
78 -p n_i + \left( \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j} +
79 \frac{\partial u_j}{\partial x_i} \right) n_j + \frac{1}{Ca} \
80 \kappa_f n_i = 0
81 \mbox{\ \ \ on the air-liquid interface,}
82 \f]
83 where \f$ \kappa_f = \kappa_f^* H_0 \f$ is the non-dimensional interface
84 curvature and the capillary number \f$ Ca = U\mu/\sigma^* \f$ is
85 a non-dimensional measure
86 of the bubble velocity.
87 The no-slip condition on the channel walls requires that \f$ {\bf
88 u}=(-1,0) \f$ at \f$ x_2=\pm 1 \f$. Far behind the bubble tip, the fluid
89 remains stationary on the walls. In the moving frame of reference, this
90 corresponds to the plug flow profile \f$ {\bf u}=(-1,0) \f$ as \f$
91 x_1\to -\infty \f$.
92 The residual film thickness \f$ h_0 \f$ has to be determined as part of the
93 solution and it determines the volume flux through the system.
94 The Poiseuille velocity profile far ahead of the bubble tip,
95 \f[
96 {\bf u}=(-1+\frac{3}{2}(1-h_0)(1-x_2^2),0) \mbox{\ \ \ as $ x_1\to \infty$,}
97 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1)
98 \f]
99 is then determined by overall continuity.

126 \section implement Implementation
127 
128 The discretisation of the problem is reasonably straightforward,
129 the most (human-)time-consuming part being the creation of the spine mesh,
130 discussed in <a href="../../../navier_stokes/spine_channel/html/index.html">
131 another tutorial</a>. The real complication arises from the fact
132 that the application of the "inflow condition" (1) at
133 the right end of the domain -- superficially a Dirichlet condition for the
134 velocity -- depends on the non-dimensional film thickness
135 \f$ h_0 \f$ at the left end of the domain. This introduces a
136 non-local interaction between these degrees of freedom.
137 We handle this by providing a templated wrapper class
138 \c BrethertonElement (templated by the type of the "wrapped" fluid
139 element) which allows the specification of the film thickness as
140 external \c Data (i.e. \c Data whose values affect the element's residuals but
141 are not determined by the element; see
142 the discussion of \c oomph-lib's various types of elemental \c Data
143 in <a href="../../../the_data_structure/html/index.html">the "bottom up"
144 discussion of the library's data structure</a>). Read the driver
145 code listed below to see how it works!
