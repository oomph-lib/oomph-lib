3 \mainpage Demo problem: Fluid Mechanics on unstructured meshes
4 
5 This tutorial provides another quick demonstration of how to use
6 unstructured meshes for the solution of fluid flow problems.
7 (The <A HREF="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xfig">xfig </a>/
8 <A HREF="http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~quake/triangle.html">\c Triangle
9 </A> <a href="../../../meshes/mesh_from_xfig/html/index.html">
10 tutorial</a> already contains 2D and 3D unstructured fluid examples.)
11 
12 The specific problem considered here serves as a "warm-up problem" for the
13 <a href="../../../interaction/unstructured_fsi/html/index.html">corresponding
14 fluid-structure interaction problem</a> in which the part of the
15 domain boundary is replaced by an elastic object.

104 \section mesh_code Creating the mesh
105 
106 In anticipation of the mesh's use in a fluid-structure interaction
107 problem, we create the mesh by multiple inheritance from \c oomph-lib's
108 \c TriangleMesh and the \c SolidMesh base class. This will allow
109 us to use pseudo-solid node-update techniques to update the position
110 of the fluid nodes in response to changes in the domain boundary.

235 \section bound Setting the boundary conditions
236 
237 
238 We pin the pseudo-solid nodes along
239 all domain boundaries, apply a no-slip condition for the fluid velocity
240 along the solid boundary (boundary 0), pin the velocity at the inflow
241 (boundary 1, where we will impose a Poiseuille flow profile), and impose
242 parallel outflow at the downstream end (boundary 2).
243 Given that the manual identification of
244 mesh boundaries in unstructured meshes that are generated by
245 third-party mesh generators is a relatively error-prone process, we
246 document the boundary conditions in three separate files to allow
247 an external sanity check; see
248 <a href="../../../solid/unstructured_solid/html/index.html#comm_ex">
249 the comments in the corresponding solid mechanics tutorial.</a>
250 The \ref comm_ex section of the present tutorial also has a sub-section
251 that illustrates what can go wrong.
252 
253 \until solid_bc_file.close();
254 
255 We complete the build of the elements by specifying
256 the Reynolds number and the constitutive equation for the pseudo-solid
257 equations.
258 
259 \until }
260 
261 Finally, we impose a Poiseuille profile at the inflow boundary (boundary
262 1).
263 
264 \until end_of_set_boundary_conditions

287 \subsection reynolds What is the Reynolds number?
288 
289 The problem considered here is a "toy"-problem, devised
290 to illustrate the use of unstructured meshes in fluids problems.
291 The specific non-dimensionalisation and parameter values are therefore
292 of secondary importance. However, since \c oomph-lib's implementation
293 of the Navier-Stokes equations is based on their non-dimensional form
294 it is important to clarify the meaning of the Reynolds number
295 in the present problem.
296 
297 <a href="../../driven_cavity/html/index.html#equation">Recall</a>
298 that the Reynolds number is defined as
299 \f[
300 Re = \frac{\rho {\cal U} {\cal L}}{\mu}
301 \f]
302 where \f$ \rho \f$ and \f$ \mu \f$ are the fluid density and viscosity,
303 respectively. \f$ {\cal L} \f$ is the reference length chosen for the
304 non-dimensionalisation of the coordinates. In the present problem,
305 where the domain boundaries were simply drawn in
306 <A HREF="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xfig">xfig </a>, no specific reference
307 length was identified, but inspection of the maximum and minimum
308 y-coordinates of the inflow boundary in the mesh plot
309 shows that the inflow boundary has a (dimensional) length
310 of \f$ (4.0875 - 0.1125) {\cal L} = 3.975 {\cal L}. \f$
311 In the non-dimensional version of the Navier-Stokes equations, all
312 velocities are non-dimensionalised with a reference velocity
313 \f$ {\cal U} \f$. When we applied the inflow boundary conditions,
314 we chose the (dimensionless) inflow profile such that its
315 integral over the inflow boundary yields a value of 1. The velocity
316 scale \f$ {\cal U} \f$ may therefore be interpreted as
317 a (dimensional) average inflow velocity through the channel, i.e. the
318 volume flux divided by the reference length scale.

353 \subsection pseudo_elast Pseudo-elasticity
354 
355 As mentioned above, the elements' pseudo-elasticity plays no useful
356 role in the present problem, as the domain boundaries remain
357 at fixed positions and no mesh movement is required.
358 
359 - As an exercise, remove all functionality related to the
360  pseudo-elasticity by changing the element type from
361  \code
362  PseudoSolidNodeUpdateElement<TTaylorHoodElement<2>,TPVDElement<2,3> >
363  \endcode
364  to
365  \code
366  TTaylorHoodElement<2>
367  \endcode
368  Adjust the code as required (e.g. remove mesh's inheritance from the
369  \c SolidMesh base class; remove the application of boundary conditions
370  for the nodal positions; etc) and confirm that the results
371  for the flow field remain unchanged.
372  \n\n
373 - Alternatively, the pseudo-elasticity can can be suppressed by
374  pinning all nodal positions, drastically reducing the number
375  of degrees of freedom in the problem without the need to
376  rewrite the rest of the code. (Note that this is also a useful
377  test for the development of FSI codes.)
378  \n\n
379 - However, if the pseudo-elastic capabilities are retained
380  the fluid mesh can indeed deform as an elastic
381  body; for example, by specifying a (solid mechanics) body force.
382  Simply follow the steps used in
383  <a href="../../../solid/unstructured_solid/html/index.html">
384  the corresponding solid mechanics problem.</a> Define the body
385  force in the namespace \c Global_Physical_Variables
386  and pass a (function-)pointer to it
387  to the elements. N.B.
388  The body force for the (pseudo-)solid equations must be specified explicitly
389  because the Navier-Stokes equations have their own body force
390  pointer! The compiler will complain about ambiguities in the following code:
391  \n
392  \code
393  [...]
394 
395  //Set the body force
396  el_pt->body_force_fct_pt() = Global_Physical_Variables::gravity;
397 
398  [...]
399  \endcode
400  Instead, we must be more specific by writing
401  \n
402  \code
403  [...]
404 
405  //Set the body force
406  el_pt->PVDEquationsBase<2>::body_force_fct_pt() =
407  Global_Physical_Variables::gravity;
408 
409  [...]
410  \endcode
411  indicating that it is the body force defined in the
412  2D solid mechanics equations that is being specified.
413 .
