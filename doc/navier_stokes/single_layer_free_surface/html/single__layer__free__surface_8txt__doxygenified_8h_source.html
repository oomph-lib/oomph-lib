13 \section free_surface_eqns Boundary conditions at a free surface
14 
15 Free surfaces occur at the interface between two fluids. Such
16 interfaces require two boundary conditions to be applied:
17 -# a kinematic condition which relates the motion of the free
18 surface to the fluid velocities at the surface, and
19 -# a dynamic condition which is concerned with the force balance at
20 the free surface.
21 
22 \subsection kinematic_condition_theory The kinematic condition
23 
24 The kinematic condition states that the fluid particles at the surface
25 remain on the surface for all times. If the surface is parametrised
26 by intrinsic coordinates \f$ \zeta_1 \f$ and \f$ \zeta_2 \f$, then the
27 Eulerian position vector which describes the surface at a given time
28 \f$ t \f$ can be written as \f$ \mathbf{R}^* =
29 \mathbf{R}^*(\zeta_1,\zeta_2,t) \f$. The kinematic condition is then
30 given by
31 \f[
32 \left(u_i^*-\frac{\partial R_i^*}{\partial t^*}\right) n_i = 0,
33 \f]
34 where \f$ \mathbf{u}^* \f$ is the (dimensional) velocity of the fluid
35 and \f$ \mathbf{n} \f$ is the outer unit normal to the free
36 surface. Using the same problem-specific reference quantities for the
37 velocity, \f$ {\cal U} \f$, length, \f$ {\cal L} \f$, and time,
38 \f${\cal T} \f$, that were used to <A HREF="../../driven_cavity/html/index.html#equation">non-dimensionalise the Navier--Stokes equations</A>, we scale the dimensional quantities such that
39 \f[
40 u_i^* = {\cal U} \, u_i, \qquad
41 R_i^* = {\cal L} \, R_i, \qquad
42 t^* = {\cal T} \, t.
43 \f]
44 The non-dimensional form of the kinematic boundary condition is then
45 given by
46 \f[
47 \left(u_i - St\, \frac{\partial R_i}{\partial t}\right) n_i = 0,
48 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1)
49 \f]
50 where the Strouhal number is
51 \f[
52 St = \frac{{\cal L}}{{\cal U}{\cal T}}.
53 \f]
54 
55 \subsection dynamic_condition_theory The dynamic condition
56 
57 \image html free_surface_sketch.gif "Sketch of the interface between two fluids. "
58 \image latex free_surface_sketch.eps "Sketch of the interface between two fluids. " width=0.6\textwidth
59 
60 The dynamic boundary condition requires the stress to be continuous
61 across a flat interface between two fluids. Referring to the sketch
62 above, we define the lower fluid to be fluid 1 and the upper fluid to
63 be fluid 2. The traction exerted by fluid 1 onto fluid 2, \f$
64 \mathbf{t}^{[1]*} \f$, is equal and opposite to that exerted by fluid
65 2 onto fluid 1, \f$ \mathbf{t}^{[2]*} \f$, and therefore \f$
66 \mathbf{t}^{[1]*} = - \mathbf{t}^{[2]*} \f$. The traction in fluid \f$
67 \beta \f$ (\f$ \beta = 1,2 \f$) is given by
68 \f[
69 t_i^{[\beta]*} = \tau_{ij}^{[\beta]*} \, n_j^{[\beta]},
70 \f]
71 where \f$ \mathbf{\tau}^{[\beta]*} \f$ is the stress tensor in fluid \f$
72 \beta \f$ and \f$ \mathbf{n}^{[\beta]} \f$ is the outer unit normal to
73 fluid \f$ \beta \f$. Since \f$ \mathbf{n}^{[2]} \f$ must equal \f$
74 -\mathbf{n}^{[1]} \f$, we have
75 \f[
76 \tau_{ij}^{[1]*} \, n_j^{[1]} = \tau_{ij}^{[2]*} \, n_j^{[1]},
77 \f]
78 where we have arbitrarily chosen to use \f$ \mathbf{n}^{[1]} \f$ as
79 the unit normal.
80 
81 On curved surfaces, surface tension creates a pressure jump \f$ \Delta p^*
82 = \sigma \kappa^* \f$ across the interface, where \f$ \sigma \f$ is the
83 surface tension and \f$ \kappa^* \f$ is equal to twice the mean curvature of
84 the surface. Therefore the dynamic boundary condition is given by
85 \f[
86 \tau_{ij}^{[2]*} \, n_j^{[1]} =
87 \tau_{ij}^{[1]*} \, n_j^{[1]} + \sigma \, \kappa^* \, n_i^{[1]},
88 \f]
89 where \f$ \kappa > 0 \f$ if the centre of curvature lies inside fluid
90 1. Using the same problem-specific reference quantities as in the
91 <A HREF="#kinematic_condition_theory">section above</A>, the
92 dimensional quantities are scaled such that
93 \f[
94 \tau_{ij}^* = \frac{\mu_{ref} \, {\cal U}}{{\cal L}} \, \tau_{ij}, \qquad
95 \kappa^* = \frac{1}{{\cal L}} \, \kappa.
96 \f]
97 The non-dimensional form of the dynamic boundary condition is then
98 given by
99 \f[
100 \tau_{ij}^{[2]} \, n_j^{[1]} =
101 \tau_{ij}^{[1]} \, n_j^{[1]} + \frac{1}{Ca} \, \kappa \, n_i^{[1]},
102 \f]
103 where the Capillary number is
104 \f[
105 Ca = \frac{\mu_{ref} \, {\cal U}}{\sigma}.
106 \f]
107 
108 In certain cases, such as the example problem below, we wish to model
109 the fluid above the interface as totally inviscid. In this case, the
110 stress tensor in fluid 2 reduces to \f$ \tau_{ij}^{[2]} = -
111 \delta_{ij} p_{ext} \f$, where \f$ p_{ext} \f$ is the
112 (non-dimensional) constant pressure above the free surface. The
113 dynamic boundary condition therefore becomes
114 \f[
115 \tau_{ij} \, n_j = - \left( \frac{1}{Ca} \, \kappa + p_{ext} \right) n_i,
116 \f]
117 where we have dropped the explicit references to fluid 1 since it is
118 understood that the stress tensor and unit normals refer to those of
119 the (one and only) viscous fluid in the problem.
120 
121 We shall now discuss how the free surface boundary conditions are
122 implemented in \c oomph-lib.

127 \section implementation Implementation

129 \subsection kinematic_condition_implementation The kinematic condition and the pseudo-solid node-update procedure
130 
131 In addition to solving for the fluid velocity and pressure (as in all
132 Navier--Stokes examples), we have additional degrees of freedom in our
133 problem due to the fact that the position of the free surface \f$
134 \mathbf{R} \f$ is unknown. The Navier--Stokes elements in
135 \c oomph-lib are based on the Arbitrary Lagrangian Eulerian (ALE) form
136 of the Navier-Stokes equations, and so can be used to solve problems
137 in moving domains. This allows us to discretise our domain using a
138 boundary fitted mesh, which will need to deform in response to
139 the motion of the free surface. This is achieved by treating the
140 interior of the mesh as a fictitious elastic solid, and solving a
141 solid mechanics problem for the (unknown) nodal positions. This
142 technique, which will subsequently be referred to as a `pseudo-solid
143 node-update strategy', employs wrapper elements to
144 existing fluid and solid equation classes. The specific element used
145 in this example is a \c PseudoSolidNodeUpdateElement<QCrouzeixRaviartElement<2>, \c QPVDElement<2,3> \c >
146 element, which takes two template arguments. The first is the standard
147 element type used to solve the fluid problem, and the second is the
148 element type which solves the equations that are used to control the
149 mesh deformation.
150 
151 The deformation of the free surface boundary is imposed by introducing
152 a field of Lagrange multipliers at the free surface, following the
153 method outlined in Cairncross et al., `A finite element method for
154 free surface flows of incompressible fluids in three dimensions. Part
155 I. Boundary fitted mesh motion' (2000). These new unknowns are stored
156 as nodal values, and so the vector of values at each node is resized
157 accordingly. Since this introduces further degrees of freedom into the
158 problem, we require an additional equation: the kinematic boundary
159 condition (1).
160 
161 We discretise this equation by attaching \c FaceElements to the
162 boundaries of the "bulk" elements that are adjacent to the free
163 surface. The specific \c FaceElement used in this example is an \c
164 ElasticLineFluidInterfaceElement<ELEMENT>, which takes the bulk
165 element type as a template argument. This allows the user of the driver
166 code to easily change the bulk element type, since the appropriate
167 \c FaceElement type is automatically used. These \c FaceElements
168 are applied in the same way as all other surface
169 elements (e.g. \c NavierStokesTractionElements, \c UnsteadyHeatFluxElements,
170 etc.), and a general introduction can be found in <A HREF="../../../poisson/two_d_poisson_flux_bc/html/index.html#create_flux">another tutorial</A>.
171 
172 \subsection dynamic_condition_implementation The dynamic condition
173 
174 Within a finite element framework, the dynamic boundary condition is
175 incorporated as contributions to each of the momentum equations at the
176 free surface. We refer to Ruschak, `A method for incorporating free
177 boundaries with surface tension in finite element fluid-flow
178 simulators' (1980), for details on the formulation, which can also be
179 found in our <a href="../../surface_theory/html/index.html#boundary_conditions"> free
180 surface theory </a> document.
181 Since both Taylor--Hood and Crouzeix--Raviart elements
182 are implemented such that the normal stresses between elements are
183 balanced, applying the dynamic boundary condition in cases in
184 which we are solving the Navier--Stokes equations on both sides of the
185 interface is as straightforward as adding the appropriate surface
186 tension contributions to the relevant momentum equations at the
187 interface. In cases such as the example below, where we have an
188 inviscid fluid above
189 the free surface, we need to add the appropriate external pressure
190 contributions (if any) as well. Both of these contributions are
191 automatically added to the appropriate momentum equations using
192 the same \c FaceElements which are used to discretise the kinematic
193 boundary condition (see <A HREF="#kinematic_condition_implementation">above</A>).
194 
195 The Capillary number defaults to 1.0 and other values may be set using
196 the function:
197 
198 \code
199 double* FluidInterfaceElement::ca_pt().
200 \endcode
201 
202 The Strouhal number defaults to 1.0 and other values may be set using
203 the function:
204 
205 \code
206 double* FluidInterfaceElement::st_pt().
207 \endcode
208 
209 The external pressure defaults to zero and other values may be set
210 using the function:
211 
212 \code
213 void FluidInterfaceElement::set_external_pressure_data(Data* p_ext_data_pt),
214 \endcode
215 
216 where \c p_ext_data_pt is (a pointer to) the \c Data in which the
217 value of the external pressure is stored. We note that the external
218 pressure is represented by \c Data because it may be an unknown in
219 certain problems, although it is simply a constant parameter in the
220 example below. It can be accessed using the function:
221 
222 \code
223 double FluidInterfaceElement::pext().
224 \endcode
225 
226 The way in which the dynamic condition is incorporated within our
227 finite element structure is discussed in more detail in the
228 <A HREF="#application_of_dbc">comments</A> at the end of this tutorial.

334 \section validation Validation
335 
336 The free surface boundary conditions for the Cartesian Navier--Stokes
337 equations have been validated against an analytical test case, and we
338 present the results in the figure below. For sufficiently small
339 amplitudes, \f$ \epsilon \ll 1 \f$, we can linearise the governing
340 equations by proposing that we can write the fluid velocities and
341 pressure, \f$ u(x,y,t) \f$, \f$ v(x,y,t) \f$ and \f$ p(x,y,t) \f$, as
342 well as the `height' of the interface, \f$ h(x,t) \f$, in the form \f$
343 x = \bar{x} + \epsilon \hat{x} \f$, where the barred quantities
344 correspond to the `base' state, chosen here to be the trivial solution \f$
345 \bar{h} = \bar{u} = \bar{v} = \bar{p} = 0 \f$. Once the linearised
346 forms of the governing equations and boundary conditions have been
347 determined we propose a separable solution of the form
348 \f[
349 \hat{h}(x,t) = H e^{\lambda t + ikx},
350 \f]
351 \f[
352 \hat{u}(x,y,t) = U(y) e^{\lambda t + ikx},
353 \f]
354 \f[
355 \hat{v}(x,y,t) = V(y) e^{\lambda t + ikx},
356 \f]
357 and
358 \f[
359 \hat{p}(x,y,t) = P(y) e^{\lambda t + ikx}.
360 \f]
361 Substituting the above ansatz into the governing equations results in
362 a system of coupled ordinary differential equations for \f$ U(y) \f$,
363 \f$ V(y) \f$ and \f$ P(y) \f$ which we solve to find their general
364 solutions up to a set of unknown constants \f$ A \f$, \f$ B \f$, \f$ C
365 \f$ and \f$ D \f$. By substituting these general forms into the set of
366 (linearised and separated) boundary conditions we obtain a linear
367 system of five equations in the five unknowns \f$ A \f$, \f$ B \f$,
368 \f$ C \f$, \f$ D \f$ and \f$ H \f$, from which we can assemble a
369 homogeneous linear system of the form
370 \f[
371  \mathbf{M} \left[
372  \begin{array}{c}
373  A \\ B \\ C \\ D \\ H
374  \end{array}
375  \right] = \left[
376  \begin{array}{c}
377  0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0
378  \end{array}
379  \right],
380 \f]
381 where \f$ \mathbf{M} \f$ is a \f$ 5 \times 5 \f$ matrix whose entries
382 are the coefficients of the unknowns in our five conditions. This
383 system only has a non-trivial solution if \f$ \left| \mathbf{M}
384 \right| = 0 \f$, and solving this equation gives us \f$ \lambda \f$ as a
385 function of \f$ k \f$. This is a dispersion relation and describes how wave
386 propagation varies as a function of its wavenumber. More specifically,
387 the real part of \f$ \lambda \f$ is the growth rate of the wave and the
388 imaginary part is its frequency. This analytical result can now be
389 compared to numerical results computed for given values of the
390 wavenumber \f$ k \f$. We choose an initial deflection amplitude of \f$
391 \epsilon = 0.01 \f$ and determine the growth rate and frequency of the
392 oscillation from a time-trace of the left-hand edge of the interface.

458 \section problem_class The problem class
459 
460 Since we are solving the unsteady Navier--Stokes equations, the
461 \c Problem class is very similar to that used in the <A HREF="../../rayleigh_channel/html/index.html">Rayleigh
462 channel example</A>. We specify the type of the element and the type
463 of the timestepper (assumed to be a member of the \c BDF family) as
464 template parameters, before passing the number of elements and domain
465 length in both coordinate directions to the problem constructor. We
466 define an empty destructor, functions to set the initial and boundary
467 conditions and a post-processing function \c doc_solution(...),
468 which will be used by the timestepping function \c unsteady_run(...).
469 
470 \dontinclude elastic_single_layer.cc
471 \skipline start_of_problem_class
472 \until unsteady_run
473 
474 The nodal positions are unknowns in the problem and hence are updated
475 automatically, so there is no need to update the mesh before
476 performing a Newton solve. However, since the main use of the
477 methodology demonstrated here is in
478 free-boundary problems where the solution of the solid problem merely
479 serves to update the nodal positions in response to the motion of the
480 free surface, we reset the nodes' Lagrangian coordinates to their
481 Eulerian positions before every solve, by calling \c
482 SolidMesh::set_lagrangian_nodal_coordinates(). This makes the deformed
483 configuration stress-free and tends to stabilise the computation,
484 allowing larger domain deformations to be computed.
485 
486 \skipline private
487 \until deform_free_surface
488 
489 The problem class stores pointers to the specific bulk mesh and the
490 surface mesh, which will contain the interface elements, as well as
491 a pointer to a constitutive law for the pseudo-solid mesh. The width
492 of the domain is also stored since it is used by the function \c
493 deform_free_surface(...) when setting up the initial mesh
494 deformation. Finally we store an output stream in which we record the
495 height of the interface at the domain edge.
496 
497 \skipline Pointer to the (specific) "bulk" mesh
498 \until End of problem class

529 \until }

537 On the solid bottom boundary (\f$ y = 0 \f$) we pin both velocity

702 \section comments Comments

704 \subsection application_of_dbc The application of the dynamic boundary condition within the FEM
705 
706 As discussed in an <A HREF="../../rayleigh_traction_channel/html/index.html#traction_theory">earlier tutorial</A>,
707 the finite element solution of the Navier--Stokes equations is based
708 on their weak form, which is obtained by weighting the
709 stress-divergence form of the momentum equations with the global test
710 functions \f$ \psi_l \f$, and integrating by parts to obtain the
711 discrete residuals
712 \f[
713 f_{il} =
714 \int_D \left[
715 Re\left(St\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial t} +
716 u_j\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j} - \frac{G_i}{Fr}\right) \ \psi_l +
717  \tau_{ij} \ \frac{\partial \psi_l}{\partial x_j} \right] \, dV -
718 \int_{\partial D} \tau_{ij} \ n_j \ \psi_l \ dS = 0.
719  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (10)
720 \f]
721 Weighting the dynamic condition (7) by the same
722 global test functions \f$ \psi_l \f$ and integrating over the domain
723 boundary \f$ \partial D\f$ gives
724 \f[
725 \int_{\partial D} \tau_{ij} n_j \ \psi_l \ dS =
726 - \int_{\partial D} p_{ext} n_i \ \psi_l \ dS
727 - \int_{\partial D} \frac{1}{Ca} \kappa \ \psi_l \ dS.
728  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (11)
729 \f]
730 In a two-dimensional problem, such as the one considered in this
731 tutorial, the domain boundary reduces to a one-dimensional curve, \f$
732 C \f$. A further integration by parts of (11)
733 therefore gives
734 \f[
735 \int_{\partial D} \tau_{ij} n_j \ \psi_l \ dS =
736 - \int_C p_{ext} n_i \ \psi_l \ dS
737 + \int_C \frac{1}{Ca} t_i \ \frac{\partial\psi_l}{\partial S} \ dS
738 - \frac{1}{Ca} \left[ t_i \ \psi_l \right]^{c_2}_{c_1},
739  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (12)
740 \f]
741 where \f$ t_i \f$, \f$ (i=1,2) \f$ is the \f$ i \f$-th component of a
742 unit vector tangent to \f$ C \f$ and pointing in the direction of
743 increasing \f$ S \f$, and \f$ c_1 \f$ and \f$ c_2 \f$ are the two
744 endpoints of \f$ C \f$.
745 
746 In the problem considered in this tutorial, the domain boundary \f$ C
747 \f$ can be written as \f$ C = C_{solid} \ \cup \ C_{surface} \f$, where
748 \f$ C_{solid} \f$ represents the portion of the domain boundary
749 corresponding to a rigid wall and \f$ C_{surface} \f$ represents the
750 portion corresponding to the free surface. The velocity along \f$
751 C_{solid} \f$ is prescribed by Dirichlet boundary conditions, and we <A HREF="../../../intro/html/index.html#galerkin">recall</A>
752 that the global test functions \f$ \psi_l \f$ vanish in this case. On
753 non-Dirichlet boundaries we must either specify the external pressure \f$
754 p_{ext} \f$, or deliberately neglect this term to obtain the `natural'
755 condition \f$ p_{ext} = 0 \f$.
756 
757 \subsection contact_line The contact line
758 
759 In this two-dimensional case, the contact `line' actually reduces to
760 two contact points, \f$ c_1 \f$ and \f$ c_2 \f$, which are located at
761 either side of the portion of the domain boundary corresponding to the
762 free surface \f$ C_{surface} \f$. The two point contributions are
763 added by specifying the the tangent to the surface \f$ t_i \f$ at
764 each of the contact points, which is equivalent to prescribing the
765 contact angle that the free surface makes with the
766 neighbouring domain boundary. We note that in the problem considered
767 here we do not explicitly apply any boundary conditions at either end
768 of the free surface. Neglecting these contributions corresponds to the
769 `natural' condition of prescribing a \f$ 90^o \f$ contact angle, which
770 happens to be the appropriate condition in this case. Contact angles
771 of arbitrary size can be enforced using \c FluidInterfaceBoundingElements,
772 which are discussed in a <A HREF="../../static_single_layer/html/index.html#contact_angle">later tutorial</A>.
773 
774 Specifying the contact angle is not the only condition that can be
775 applied at the edges of an interface. The alternative boundary
776 condition is to pin the contact line so that its position is fixed
777 for all time. Since this is a Dirichlet condition it causes the
778 integral over the contact line to vanish.
