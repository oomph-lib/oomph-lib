3 \mainpage Example problem: Finite-Reynolds-number flow inside an oscillating ellipse
4 
5 In this example we consider our first moving-boundary Navier-Stokes
6 problem: The flow of a viscous fluid contained in an elliptical
7 ring whose walls perform periodic oscillations.
8 
9 \c oomph-lib's Navier-Stokes elements are based on the Arbitrary
10 Lagrangian Eulerian (ALE) form of the Navier-Stokes equations and can
11 therefore be used in moving domain problems. In this example we
12 illustrate their use in \c Domain - based meshes (first discussed in
13 the example demonstrating the solution of the
14 <A HREF="../../../unsteady_heat/two_d_unsteady_heat_ALE/html/index.html">
15 unsteady heat equation in a moving domain</A>) in which \c
16 MacroElements are used to update the nodal positions in response
17 to changes in the domain boundary. In subsequent examples, we
18 will discuss alternative, sparse mesh update techniques that
19 are useful in problems with free boundaries and in fluid-structure
20 interaction problems.

28 <B>Finite-Reynolds-number-flow driven by an oscillating ellipse</B>
29 </CENTER>
30 
31 We consider the unsteady 2D flow of a Newtonian fluid that is contained in an
32 oscillating elliptical ring whose wall shape is parametrised
33 by the Lagrangian coordinate \f$ \xi \f$ as
34 \f[
35 {\bf R}_w(\xi) = \left(
36 \begin{array}{l}
37 a(t) \cos(\xi) \\
38 a^{-1}(t) \sin(\xi)
39 \end{array}
40 \right)
41 \f]
42 where
43 \f[
44 a(t) = A + \widehat{A} \cos\left(\frac{2\pi t}{T}\right).
45 \f]
46 \f$ A \f$ represents the average half-axis of the elliptical
47 ring in the \f$ x_1 \f$-direction, and \f$ \widehat{A} \f$ is the amplitude
48 of its periodic variation. The ring has constant cross-sectional area
49 -- consistent with the incompressibility of the fluid whose motion
50 is governed by the ALE form of the Navier-Stokes equations,
51 \f[
52 Re\left(St\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial t} +
53 \left(u_j-u_j^M\right)\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j}\right) =
54 - \frac{\partial p}{\partial x_i} +
55 \frac{\partial }{\partial x_j} \left(
56 \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j} +
57 \frac{\partial u_j}{\partial x_i} \right),
58  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1)
59 \f]
60 and the continuity equation
61 \f[
62 \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_i} = 0,
63 \f]
64 where \f$ u_j^M \f$ is the mesh velocity. We exploit the symmetry of
65 the problem and solve the equations in the quarter domain
66 \f[
67 D = \left\{(x_1,x_2) \, \bigg| \,
68 x_1\geq0, \, x_2\geq0,\, \left(\frac{x_1}{a(t)}\right)^2 +
69 \big(x_2 \, a(t)\big)^2 \leq 1\right\},
70 \f]
71 shown in this sketch (for \f$ A=1, \ \widehat{A}=1 \f$ and \f$T=1\f$),
72 \image html wall.gif "Sketch of the computational domain. "
73 \image latex wall.eps "Sketch of the computational domain. " width=0.75\textwidth
74 
75 The fluid is subject to no-slip boundary conditions on the curved wall,
76 \f[
77 \left. {\bf u} \right|_{\partial D_{ellipse}} = \frac{\partial {\bf
78 R}_w(\xi, t)}{\partial t}
79 \f]
80 and symmetry conditions on the symmetry boundaries,
81 \f[
82 \left.u_1\right|_{x_2=0}=0, \qquad \left.u_2\right|_{x_1=0}=0.
83 \f]
84 The initial conditions for the velocity are given by
85 \f[
86 {\bf u}(x_1,x_2,t=0) = {\bf u}_{IC}(x_1,x_2),
87 \f]
88 where \f$ {\bf u}_{IC}(x_1,x_2) \f$ is a given, divergence-free
89 velocity field that satisfies the velocity boundary conditions
90 at \f$ t=0 \f$. No initial conditions are required for the pressure.

98 \section solution An exact solution
99 It is easy to show (by inspection) that the unsteady stagnation point flow
100 \f[
101 u_1(x_1,x_2,t) = \frac{1}{a}\frac{da}{dt}\, x_1 =
102 -\frac{2\pi\widehat{A}\sin\left(\frac{2\pi t}{T}\right)}
103  {T\left(A+\widehat{A}\cos\left(\frac{2\pi
104  t}{T}\right)\right)} \, x_1
105 \mbox{\ \ \ \ and \ \ \ \ \ }
106 u_2(x_1,x_2,t) = -\frac{1}{a}\frac{da}{dt} \, x_2 =
107 -\frac{2\pi\widehat{A}\sin\left(\frac{2\pi t}{T}\right)}
108  {T\left(A+\widehat{A}\cos\left(\frac{2\pi
109  t}{T}\right)\right)} \, x_2,
110 \f]
111 is an exact solution of the above problem as it satisfies the
112 Navier-Stokes equations and the velocity boundary conditions.
113 The pressure is given by
114 \f[
115 p =
116 \frac{2\pi\widehat{A}\, Re\, \left(x_1^2\, St\,
117  \cos\left(\frac{2\pi t}{T}\right)A + x_1^2\, St\, \widehat{A} -
118  x_1^2\widehat{A} +
119  x_1^2\widehat{A}\cos^2\left(\frac{2\pi t}{T}\right) -
120  x_2^2\, St\, \cos\left(\frac{2\pi t}{T}\right) -
121  x_2^2\, St\, \widehat{A} -
122  x_2^2\widehat{A} +
123  x_2^2\widehat{A}\cos^2\left(\frac{2\pi t}{T}\right)\right)}
124  {T^2\left(A^2 + 2A\widehat{A}\cos\left(\frac{2\pi t}{T}\right) +
125  \widehat{A}^2\cos^2\left(\frac{2\pi t}{T}\right)\right)}.
126 \f]

155 \section wall The moving wall
156 As usual, we represent the moving wall as a \c GeomObject and define
157 its shape by implementing the pure virtual function
158 \c GeomObject::position(...). The arguments to the constructor
159 specify the mean half-axis of the ellipse, \f$ A \f$, the amplitude
160 of its variations, \f$ \widehat{A} \f$, and the period of the oscillation,
161 \f$ T \f$ . We also pass the pointer to a \c Time object to the
162 constructor and store it in a private data member, to
163 allow the \c position(...) functions to access the current
164 value of the continuous time.

215 \section problem The problem class
216 Most of the problem class is a straightforward combination of the
217 problem classes employed in the simulation of
218 <A HREF="../../driven_cavity/html/index.html"> the adaptive driven cavity
219 </A> and
220 <A HREF="../../rayleigh_channel/html/index.html"> Rayleigh channel
221 </A> problems, in that the problem combines unsteadiness with spatial
222 adaptivity
223 (though in the present problem the adaptivity is only used to
224 uniformly refine the very coarse base mesh; we refer to ***another
225 example*** for the use of full spatial adaptivity in a moving-domain
226 Navier-Stokes problem).

233 
234 The key new feature in the current problem
235 is the presence of the moving domain which requires updates of
236 -# all nodal positions
237 -# the prescribed velocities on the
238  moving wall via the no-slip condition.
239 .
240 before every timestep. Since the nodal positions of the
241 \c QuarterCircleSectorMesh are determined via its \c MacroElement
242  / \c Domain representation (which updates the nodal position in
243 response to changes in the geometry of the \c GeomObjects that define
244 its boundaries), the former task may be accomplished by
245 executing the \c Mesh::node_update() function; the update of
246 the no-slip condition may be performed by calling the function
247 \c FSI_functions::apply_no_slip_on_moving_wall(Node* node_pt),
248 a helper function, defined in the namespace \c FSI_functions,
249 which updates the velocity components \f$ u_1, u_2 [, u_3] \f$
250 according to the no-slip boundary condition
251 \f[
252 {\bf u}_{Node} = \frac{\partial {\bf x}_{Node}}{\partial t}
253 \f]
254 where the time-derivative of the nodal positions is evaluated by
255 the \c Node's positional timestepper. [\b Note: The function
256 \c FSI_functions::apply_no_slip_on_moving_wall(...)
257 assumes that the velocity components are stored in the \c Node's
258 first 2 [3] values. This is consistent with the storage of
259 the velocity component in all existing Navier-Stokes elements.
260 If you develop your own Navier-Stokes elements and use a different
261 storage scheme you use this function at your own risk.]

315 \section IC Assigning the initial conditions
316 This function assigns "history values" for the velocities and the
317 nodal positions from the exact solution. It is implemented in exactly the
318 same way as in the solution of the
319 <A HREF="../../../unsteady_heat/two_d_unsteady_heat_ALE/html/index.html#IC">
320 unsteady heat equation in a moving domain</A>. Note that because
321 the domain is moving, the nodal positions must be updated
322 (according to the position of the domain boundary at the relevant previous
323 timestep), before evaluating the exact solution at the nodal position.

375 \section comments Comments and Exercises
376 -# Compare the results of the numerical simulation in which
377  \f$ {\bf u}_{IC} \f$ is given by the exact solution (an unsteady
378  stagnation point flow) to that obtained from an "impulsive start"
379  where \f$ {\bf u}_{IC} = {\bf 0}. \f$ (This is most easily
380  implemented by replacing the call to \c set_initial_condition()
381  with a call to \c Problem::assign_initial_values_impulsive().
382  \n \n
383  Why do we obtain the same velocity with both initial conditions
384  and why does the pressure take a few timesteps (How many exactly?
385  Compare simulations with \c BDF<4> and \c BDF<2> timesteppers.)
386  to "catch up" with the exact solution? [Hint: The
387  unsteady stagnation point flow is a potential flow, therefore
388  the viscous terms in the Navier-Stokes equations disappear. See
389  also chapter 3.19 in Volume 2 of Gresho & Sani's wonderful book
390  "Incompressible Flow and the Finite Element Method".]
