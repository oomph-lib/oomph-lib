3 \mainpage Demo problem: Steady finite-Reynolds-number flow through an iliac bifurcation
4 
5 The purpose of this tutorial is to demonstrate the simulation of
6 cardiovascular fluid mechanics problems with \c oomph-lib. We showed in
7 <a href="../../../meshes/mesh_from_vmtk/html/index.html">another tutorial</a>
8 how to use <a href="http://www.vmtk.org">VMTK</a>
9 together with \c oomph-lib's conversion code \n\n
10 <CENTER>
11 <a href="../../../../demo_drivers/meshing/mesh_from_vmtk/create_fluid_and_solid_surface_mesh_from_fluid_xda_mesh.cc">demo_drivers/meshing/mesh_from_vmtk/create_fluid_and_solid_surface_mesh_from_fluid_xda_mesh.cc</a>
12 </CENTER>
13 \n\n
14 to generate unstructured fluid and solid meshes for the simulation
15 of physiological fluid-structure interaction problems based on data from
16 medical images. Here we show how to simulate steady finite-Reynolds-number flow
17 through a (rigid) iliac bifurcation. A particular feature of this
18 problem is that, unlike the problem considered in
19 <a href="../../unstructured_three_d_fluid/html/index.html">
20 another tutorial,</a> the in- and outflow boundaries are not
21 aligned with any coordinate planes. Parallel in- and outflow
22 is therefore enforced by a Lagrange multiplier method, implemented
23 using \c oomph-lib's \c FaceElements.
24 
25 
26 The problem studied here also serves as a "warm-up problem" for the
27 <a href="../../../interaction/vmtk_fsi/html/index.html">
28 corresponding fluid-structure interaction problem </a> in which the vessel
29 wall is elastic and deforms in response to the traction
30 that the fluid exerts onto it.
31 
32 We stress that the tutorial focuses on the implementation
33 aspects, not the actual physics. Since the driver code discussed
34 here is also used in the library's self-tests we
35 deliberately use a very coarse mesh and restrict ourselves to
36 steady flows. The results shown below are therefore unlikely
37 to bear much resemblance to the actual flows that arise
38 \e in \e vivo. The section \ref realism at the end of this
39 tutorial provides several suggestions on how to make the simulation
40 more realistic.

71 \section parallel_flow Imposing parallel in- and outflow
72 In most of the Navier-Stokes problems we have considered so far,
73 the geometry of the fluid domain was such that the in- and outflow
74 cross-sections were aligned with the Cartesian coordinate planes.
75 In such geometries the imposition of parallel in- and outflow is
76 straightforward as it only requires pinning of the transverse
77 velocity components. A pressure drop between
78 upstream and downstream ends can be applied by attaching
79 \c NavierStokesTractionElements to the bulk elements that are
80 adjacent to the relevant domain boundaries.
81 
82 In the current problem, the in- and outflow cross-sections have
83 some arbitrary orientation, implying that when the in- or outflow is
84 parallel (or, more accurately, orthogonal to the in- or outflow cross
85 sections), neither of the three velocity components vanishes.
86 The easiest way to enforce parallel outflow in such situations is
87 to employ Lagrange multipliers to enforce the two constraints
88 \f[
89 {\bf u} \cdot {\bf t}_\alpha = 0 \mbox{\ \ \ \ for $\alpha = 1,2$,}
90 \f]
91 where \f$ {\bf t}_\alpha \f$ (for \f$\alpha = 1,2\f$) are the
92 two tangent vectors spanning the in- or outflow cross-sections.
93 Physically, the Lagrange multipliers act as tangential tractions
94 that enforce the parallel flow. The Lagrange multipliers
95 introduce additional degrees of freedom into the problem and their
96 implementation as \c ImposeParallelOutflowElements employs a
97 technique similar to that used to enforce prescribed boundary
98 displacements in solid mechanics problems. (This is discussed in
99 <a href="../../../solid/prescribed_displ_lagr_mult/html/index.html">
100 another tutorial</a>.) The \c ImposeParallelOutflowElements also
101 allow the specification of a pressure that acts on the fluid
102 (in the direction opposite to the outer unit normal on the bulk
103 fluid element).

239 \subsection re What does the Reynolds number mean in this problem?
240 \c oomph-lib's implementation of the Navier-Stokes equations is
241 based on <a href="../../driven_cavity/html/index.html#equation">their
242 non-dimensional form</a> so we typically expect
243 the geometry of the problem to have been non-dimensionalised on
244 a representative lengthscale, \f$ {\cal L} \f$. When dealing with
245 geometries that are obtained from medical images, the vessel coordinates are
246 typically provided as dimensional quantities, e.g. in millimetres.
247 Discarding the unit of the coordinates (i.e. using
248 a non-dimensional coordinate \f$ (x,y,z)=(1,2,3)\f$ to represent
249 the point located at \f$
250 (x^*,y^*,z^*)=(1\mbox{mm},2\mbox{mm},3\mbox{mm}),\f$ say)
251 is therefore equivalent to non-dimensionalising
252 all lengths on a reference length of \f$ {\cal L} = 1\mbox{mm}. \f$
253 <a href="../../driven_cavity/html/index.html#equation">Recall</a>
254 that the Reynolds number is defined as
255 \f[
256 Re = \frac{\rho {\cal U} {\cal L}}{\mu}
257 \f]
258 where \f$ \rho \f$ and \f$ \mu \f$ are the fluid density and viscosity,
259 respectively. The lengthscale \f$ {\cal L} = 1\mbox{mm} \f$ does obviously
260 not provide a measure of the dimension of our blood vessel whose
261 diameter (at the inlet) is about \f$ D \approx 16\mbox{mm}. \f$ The
262 nominal Reynolds number of \f$ Re=10 \f$ used in the computations
263 is therefore equivalent to an actual Reynolds number (formed with
264 the vessel diameter)
265 of
266 \f[
267 Re_D = \frac{\rho {\cal U} D }{\mu} = Re \ \frac{D}{\cal L} = 160,
268 \f]
269 where the velocity scale \f$ {\cal U} \f$ is formed with the
270 (dimensional) applied pressure drop \f$ \Delta P^* \f$
271 between the in- and outflow cross-sections, as in the
272 <a href="../../unstructured_three_d_fluid/html/index.html">problem
273 with axis-aligned in- and outflow cross-sections,</a>
274 \f[
275 {\cal U} = \frac{\Delta P^*\ {\cal L} }{\mu}.
276 \f]

280 \subsection realism How to make the simulation more realistic
281 The simulation presented above is obviously very crude and serves
282 primarily as a proof of concept. However, it is straightforward
283 to address most of the shortcomings and we encourage you to explore
284 the following improvements:
285 - Generate a finer fluid mesh using the instructions
286  in \c oomph-lib's
287  <a href="../../../meshes/mesh_from_vmtk/html/index.html">VMTK
288  tutorial </a> We are hoping to make the image files
289  used in this tutorial available soon. Please
290  <a href="../../../contact/html/index.html">contact us</a>
291  if you can't wait. Remember that you will have to update the
292  enumeration of the domain boundaries if you change the mesh.
293  \n\n
294 - Attach "flow extensions" to the in- and outflow
295  cross-sections, using the technique described in \c oomph-lib's
296  <a href="../../../meshes/mesh_from_vmtk/html/index.html#add_extensions">
297  VMTK tutorial.</a>
298  \n\n
299 - Make the problem time-dependent and apply a period driving pressure
300  drop.
