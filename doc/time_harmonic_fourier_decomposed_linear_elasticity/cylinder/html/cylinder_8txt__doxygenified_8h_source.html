3 \mainpage The azimuthally Fourier-decomposed equations of 3D time-harmonic linear elasticity
4 
5 The aim of this tutorial is to demonstrate the solution of the
6 time-harmonic equations of linear elasticity in cylindrical polar
7 coordinates, using a Fourier decomposition of the solution in the
8 azimuthal direction.
9 These equations are useful to describe forced, time-harmonic,
10 non-axisymmetric oscillations of axisymmetric elastic bodies.

33 \section theory Theory
34 Consider a three-dimensional, axisymmetric body (of density \f$ \rho
35 \f$, Young's modulus \f$ E \f$, and Poisson's ratio \f$ \nu \f$),
36 occupying the region \f$ D \f$
37 whose boundary is \f$ \partial D \f$. Assuming that the body performs
38 time-harmonic oscillations of frequency of \f$ \omega \f$, we use cylindrical
39 coordinates \f$ (r^*,\theta,z^*) \f$. The equations of time-harmonic
40 linear elasticity can then be written as
41 <CENTER>
42 \f[
43 \mbox{\boldmath$ \nabla^*\cdot\tau^*$}+\rho \mbox{\boldmath$
44 F^*$}=-\rho\omega^2\mbox{\boldmath$ u^*$},
45 \f]
46 </CENTER>
47 where \f$\mbox{\boldmath$ \nabla^*$}=(\frac{\partial}{\partial
48 r^*},\frac{1}{r^*}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta},\frac{\partial}{\partial
49 z^*}) \f$, and the stresses, body force
50 and displacements are given by
51 \f$ {\rm Re}\{\mbox{\boldmath$\tau^*$}(r^*,\theta,z^*){\rm e}^{-{\rm i}\omega
52 t^*}\} \f$,
53 \f$ {\rm Re}\{\mbox{\boldmath$F^*$}(r^*,\theta,z^*){\rm e}^{-{\rm i}\omega
54 t^*}\} \f$ and \f$ {\rm Re}\{\mbox{\boldmath$u^*$}(r^*,\theta,z^*){\rm e}^{-{\rm i}\omega
55 t^*}\} \f$ respectively. Note that here and henceforth, the superscript
56 asterisk notation is used to distinguish dimensional quantities
57 from their non-dimensional counterparts where required. (The coordinate
58 \f$ \theta \f$ is by definition dimensionless, and so we do not use
59 an asterisk when referencing this parameter).
60 
61 The body is subject to imposed time-harmonic displacements
62 \f$ \mbox{\boldmath$ u^*$} \f$
63 along \f$ \partial D_d \f$, and subject to an imposed traction
64 \f$ \mbox{\boldmath$ \hat{\tau}^*$} \f$ along \f$ \partial D_n \f$
65 where \f$ \partial D=\partial D_d\cup\partial D_n \f$ so that
66 <CENTER>
67 \f[
68 \mbox{\boldmath$ u^*$}=\mbox{\boldmath$ \hat{u}^*$}\,\,\textrm{on $
69 \partial D_d $ },\quad
70 \mbox{\boldmath$ \tau^*$} \cdot
71 {\bf n}=\mbox{\boldmath$ \hat{\tau}^*$}\,\,\textrm{on $
72 \partial D_n $ }
73 \f]
74 </CENTER>
75 where \f$ {\bf n} \f$ is the outer unit normal on the boundary.
76 
77 The stresses and displacements are related by the constitutive equations
78 <CENTER>
79 \f[
80 \mbox{\boldmath$ \tau^*$}=\frac{E}{1+\nu}\left(
81 \frac{\nu}{1-2\nu}(\mbox{\boldmath$\nabla^*\cdot u^*$})\textbf{I}+
82 \frac{1}{2}(\mbox{\boldmath$ \nabla^* u^*$}+\mbox{\boldmath$ \nabla^* u^*$}^{{\rm T}})\right),
83 \f]
84 </CENTER>
85 where \f$ \mbox{\boldmath$ \nabla^* u^*$}^{{\rm T}}
86 \f$ represents the transpose of \f$ \mbox{\boldmath$ \nabla^* u^*$}
87 \f$. Note that in cylindrical coordinates, the second-order
88 tensor \f$ \mbox{\boldmath$ \nabla^* u^*$} \f$ is given by
89 <CENTER>
90 \f[
91 \mbox{\boldmath$ \nabla^* u^*$}=
92 {
93 \left(\begin{array}{ccc}
94 \frac{\partial u^*_r}{\partial r^*}&
95 \frac{1}{r^*}\frac{\partial
96 u^*_r}{\partial\theta}-\frac{u^*_\theta}{r^*}&
97 \frac{\partial u^*_r}{\partial z^*}\\
98 \frac{\partial u^*_\theta}{\partial r^*}&
99 \frac{1}{r^*}\frac{\partial
100 u^*_\theta}{\partial\theta}+\frac{u^*_r}{r^*}&
101 \frac{\partial u^*_\theta}{\partial z^*}\\
102 \frac{\partial u^*_z}{\partial r^*}&
103 \frac{1}{r^*}\frac{\partial u^*_z}{\partial\theta}&
104 \frac{\partial u^*_z}{\partial z^*}\end{array}\right)
105 }
106 \f]
107 </CENTER>
108 and \f$ \mbox{\boldmath$\nabla^*\cdot u^*$} \f$ is equal to the trace
109 of this matrix.
110 
111 We non-dimensionalise the equations, using a problem specific reference
112 length, \f$ {\cal L} \f$, and a timescale \f$ {\cal T}=1/\omega \f$, and use
113 Young's modulus to non-dimensionalise the body force and the stress tensor:
114 <CENTER>
115 \f[
116 \mbox{\boldmath$ \tau^*$} = E \, \mbox{\boldmath$ \tau$}, \qquad
117 r^* = {\cal L}\, r, \qquad z^* = {\cal L}\, z
118 \f]
119 \f[
120 \mbox{\boldmath$ u^*$} = {\cal L}\, \mbox{\boldmath$ u$}, \qquad
121 \mbox{\boldmath$ F^*$} = \frac{E}{\rho \cal L} \, \mbox{\boldmath$ F$},
122 \qquad
123 t^* = {\cal T}\, t.
124 \f]
125 </CENTER>
126 
127 The non-dimensional form of the linear elasticity equations is then given by
128 <CENTER>
129 \f[
130 \mbox{\boldmath$ \nabla\cdot\tau$}+\mbox{\boldmath$
131 F$}=-\Omega^2\mbox{\boldmath$ u$},
132 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1)
133 \f]
134 </CENTER>
135 where \f$\mbox{\boldmath$ \nabla$}=(\frac{\partial}{\partial
136 r},\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta},\frac{\partial}{\partial
137 z}) \f$,
138 <CENTER>
139 \f[
140 \mbox{\boldmath$ \tau $}=\frac{1}{1+\nu}\left(
141 \frac{\nu}{1-2\nu}(\mbox{\boldmath$\nabla\cdot u$})\textbf{I}+
142 \frac{1}{2}(\mbox{\boldmath$ \nabla u$}+\mbox{\boldmath$ \nabla
143 u$}^{{\rm T}})\right),
144 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (2)
145 \f]
146 </CENTER>
147 and the non-dimensional parameter
148 <CENTER>
149 \f[
150 \Omega = {\cal L}\omega \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E}}
151 \f]
152 </CENTER>
153 is the ratio of the elastic body's intrinsic timescale,
154  \f${\cal L} \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E}}\f$, to the problem-specific
155 timescale, \f$ {\cal T}=1/\omega \f$, that we used to non-dimensionalise time.
156 The boundary conditions are
157 <CENTER>
158 \f[
159 \mbox{\boldmath$ u$}=\mbox{\boldmath$ \hat{u}$}\,\,\textrm{on $
160 \partial D_d $},\quad
161 \mbox{\boldmath$ \tau$}\cdot {\bf n}
162 =\mbox{\boldmath$ \hat{\tau}$}\,\,\textrm{on $
163 \partial D_n $}.\f]
164 </CENTER>
165 
166 Given the assumed axisymmetry of the body we expand all quantities
167 in a Fourier series in the azimuthal coordinate \f$ \theta \f$ by
168 writing,
169 <CENTER>
170 \f[
171 \mbox{\boldmath$ u$}(r,\theta ,z)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}\mbox{\boldmath$ u$}^{(n)}(r,z){\rm e}^{{\rm i}
172 n\theta},\quad
173 \mbox{\boldmath$ F$} (r,\theta ,z)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}\mbox{\boldmath$ F$}^{(n)}(r,z){\rm e}^{{\rm i}
174 n\theta},\quad
175 \mbox{\boldmath$ \tau$} (r,\theta ,z)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}\mbox{\boldmath$ \tau$}^{(n)}(r,z){\rm e}^{{\rm i}
176 n\theta}
177 ,\f]
178 </CENTER>
179 This decomposition allows us to
180 remove the \f$ \theta \f$-dependence from the equations by writing
181 \f$ \frac{\partial\zeta}{\partial\theta}={\rm i}n\zeta \f$, where
182 \f$\zeta \f$ represents any physical parameter in the problem.
183 Furthermore, since the governing equations are linear, we can solve for each
184 Fourier component separately and simply specify the Fourier wavenumber
185 \f$ n \f$ as a parameter.

193 \section element_types Implementation
194 
195 Within \c oomph-lib, the non-dimensional version of the
196 two-dimensional Fourier-decomposed equations (1) with
197 the constitutive equations (2) are implemented
198 in the \c TimeHarmonicFourierDecomposedLinearElasticityEquations equations
199 class. Following our usual approach, discussed in the
200 <A HREF="../../../quick_guide/html/index.html"> (Not-So-)Quick
201 Guide,</A> this equation class is then combined with a geometric finite
202 element to form a fully-functional finite element.
203 For instance, the combination of the
204 \c TimeHarmonicFourierDecomposedLinearElasticityEquations
205 class with the geometric finite element \c QElement<2,3> yields a nine-node
206 quadrilateral element. As usual, the mapping
207 between local and global (Eulerian) coordinates within an element is given by,
208 <CENTER>
209 \f[
210 x_i = \sum_{j=1}^{N^{(E)}} X^{(E)}_{ij} \, \psi_j, \qquad
211 i=1,2,
212 \f]
213 </CENTER>
214 where the coordinates are enumerated as \f$ x_1=r, \ x_2 = z\f$.
215 \f$ N^{(E)} \f$ is the number of nodes in the element, \f$ X^{(E)}_{ij}
216 \f$ is the \f$ i \f$-th global (Eulerian) coordinate (enumerated as above)
217 of the \f$ j \f$-th \c Node in the element, and the \f$ \psi_j \f$ are
218 the element's shape functions, defined in the geometric
219 finite element.
220 
221 We allow for the presence of damping by allowing the constitutive
222 parameters and forcing frequency to be complex-valued. The three
223 components of the displacement field therefore have real and imaginary
224 parts and we store the six real-valued nodal unknowns in the order
225 \f$ Re\{u_r^{(n)}\}, Re\{u_z^{(n)}\}, Re\{u_\theta^{(n)}\},
226  Im\{u_r^{(n)}\}, Im\{u_z^{(n)}\}, Im\{u_\theta^{(n)}\}
227  \f$ and use the
228 shape functions to interpolate the displacements as
229 <CENTER>
230 \f[
231 u_i^{(n)} = \sum_{j=1}^{N^{(E)}} U^{(E)}_{ij} \, \psi_j, \qquad
232 i=1,...6,
233 \f]
234 </CENTER>
235 where \f$ U^{(E)}_{ij} \f$ is the \f$ i \f$-th displacement component
236 (enumerated as indicated above) at the \f$ j \f$-th \c Node in the element.

242 \section test The test problem
243 
244 The governing equations are fairly complicated and it is difficult
245 to come up with non-trivial analytical solutions that could be used
246 to validate the implementation. We therefore construct an analytical
247 solution by postulating a displacement field and providing a body
248 force that makes this a solution of the equations.
249 
250 Specifically we consider the time-harmonic non-axisymmetric deformation of
251 an annular elastic body that occupies the region \f$ r_{\rm min}\leq
252 r\leq r_{\rm max}, z_{\rm min}\leq
253 z\leq z_{\rm max}, 0\leq\theta\leq 2\pi \f$.
254 
255 The displacement field
256 <CENTER>
257 \f[
258 \mbox{\boldmath$u$}^{(n)}=
259 \left(\begin{array}{c}u_r^{(n)}\\u_\theta^{(n)}\\u_z^{(n)}\end{array}\right)=
260 \left(\begin{array}{c}r^3\cos\,
261 z\\r^3z^3\\r^3\sin\,z\end{array}\right)
262 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (3)
263 \f]
264 </CENTER>
265 is an exact solution of the governing equations if the body is
266 subject to a body force
267 <CENTER>
268 \f[
269 \mbox{\boldmath$ F$}^{(n)}=\left(\begin{array}{c}
270 -r(2{\rm i} nz^3\lambda+\cos\,
271 z\{(8+3r)\lambda-(n^2-16+r(r-3))\mu+r^2 \Lambda^2\})\\
272 -r\{8z^3\mu-n^2z^3(\lambda+2\mu)+r^2(z^3\Lambda^2+6\mu z)+{\rm i}
273 n\cos\, z((4+r)\lambda+(6+r)\mu)\}\\
274 r\sin\,
275 z\{(n^2-9)\mu+4r(\lambda+\mu)+r^2(\lambda+2\mu-\Lambda^2)\}-3{\rm
276 i} nr^2z^2(\lambda+\mu)\end{array}\right),
277 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (4)
278 \f]
279 </CENTER>
280 where \f$ \lambda = \nu/((1+\nu)(1-2\nu))\f$ and \f$ \mu =
281 1/(2(1+\nu))\f$ are the non-dimensional Lam&eacute; parameters
282 (non-dimensionalised on \f$ E \f$). The body is subject to
283 a non-zero traction on all four boundaries; for
284 example, on the inner boundary (where \f$ r=r_{\rm min} \f$) the
285 traction is
286 <CENTER>
287 \f[
288 \mbox{\boldmath$ \hat{\tau}$}^{(n)}_3=
289 \mbox{\boldmath$ \tau$}^{(n)}(r_{\rm min},z)\cdot (-{\bf e}_r) =
290 \left(\begin{array}{c}
291 -6r_{\rm min}^2\mu\cos\,
292 z-\lambda({\rm i} nr_{\rm min}^2z^3+r_{\rm min}^2(4+r_{\rm min})\cos\, z)
293 \\
294 -\mu r_{\rm min}^2(2z^3+{\rm i} n\cos\, z)
295 \\
296 -\mu r_{\rm min}^2\sin\, z(3-r_{\rm min})
297 \end{array}\right).
298 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (5)
299 \f]
300 </CENTER>
301 We choose to set this traction as a boundary condition, whilst
302 pinning the displacements on the remaining boundaries
303 where we impose a prescribed displacement according to (3).

448 \subsection comments Comments
449 - Given that we non-dimensionalised all stresses on Young's modulus
450  it seems odd that we provide the option to specify a non-dimensional
451  Young's modulus via the member function
452  \c TimeHarmonicFourierDecomposedLinearElasticityEquations::youngs_modulus_pt().
453  The explanation for this is that this function specifies the ratio of
454  the material's actual Young's modulus to the Young's modulus used in the
455  non-dimensionalisation of the equations. The capability to specify
456  such ratios is important in problems where the elastic body is made of
457  multiple materials with different constitutive properties. If the
458  body is made of a single, homogeneous material, the specification
459  of the non-dimensional Young's modulus is not required -- it
460  defaults to 1.0. In the example considered above, the specification
461  of the non-dimensional Young's modulus as \f$ 1 + 0.01 {\rm i} \f$
462  creates a small amount of damping in the material whose actual
463  stiffness is still characterised by the (real-valued and dimensional)
464  Young's modulus used to non-dimensionalise the equations.
465 - Note that we also allow Poisson's ratio (whose specification \e is
466  required) to be complex-valued. We are not aware of any meaningful physical
467  interpretation of non-real Poisson ratios but provide this option
468  because it appears to allow a better characterisation of some materials.
469 .

471 \subsection exercises Exercises
472 - Confirm that the specification of Poisson's ratio is required:
473  What happens if you comment out its assignment in the problem constructor?
474 - Confirm that the small imaginary part of the computed displacement field
475  for the test problem goes to zero under mesh refinement.
476 - Change the problem setup to the (less contrived) case where the
477  deformation of the cylinder is driven by a time-periodic pressure
478  load acting on the inside while its upper and lower ends are held
479  at a fixed position. (You can cheat -- there's
