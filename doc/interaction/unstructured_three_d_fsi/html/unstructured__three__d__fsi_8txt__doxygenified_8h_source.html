3 \mainpage Demo problem: 3D FSI on unstructured meshes
4 
5 
6 This tutorial demonstrates the use of unstructured meshes
7 in 3D fluid-structure interaction problems. We combine two
8 single-physics problems, namely
9 - <a href="../../../solid/unstructured_three_d_solid/html/index.html">
10  Large deformations of an elastic 3D bifurcating tube, loaded
11  by an internal pressure</a>
12  \n\n
13 - <a href="../../../navier_stokes/unstructured_three_d_fluid/html/index.html">
14  Flow through a rigid 3D bifurcating tube</a>
15 .
16 for which we have already created unstructured 3D meshes,
17 using <A HREF="http://www.wias-berlin.de/~si ">Hang Si's</A>
18 open-source mesh generator <A HREF="http://wias-berlin.de/software/tetgen//"> \c tetgen </A>.

23 \section the_problem The problem
24 The two figures below show a sketch of the problem. An applied pressure
25 drop drives fluid through an elastic, bifurcating tube whose
26 branches have approximately rectangular cross-sections. We solve this
27 problem as a fully-coupled fluid-structure interaction problem
28 in which the fluid provides the traction onto the solid whose
29 deformation changes the fluid domain.
30 
31 
32 
33 \image html problem_sketch_forward.gif "Sketch of the problem. "
34 \image latex problem_sketch_forward.eps "Sketch of the problem. " width=0.75\textwidth
35 
36 \image html problem_sketch_backward.gif "Sketch of the problem (reverse view). "
37 \image latex problem_sketch_backward.eps "Sketch of the problem (reverse view). " width=0.75\textwidth
38 
39 
40 As usual, we formulate the problem in non-dimensional form. For this
41 purpose we non-dimensionalise all lengths on the half-width, \f$ W
42 \f$, of the square inflow cross-section, and use the overall pressure drop,
43 \f$ \Delta P^* = P_{in}^* - P_{out}^* \f$ to define the (viscous)
44 velocity scale
45 \f[
46 {\cal U} = \frac{\Delta P^*\ W}{\mu}.
47 \f]
48 With this choice the Reynolds number becomes
49 \f[
50 Re = \frac{\rho {\cal U} W }{\mu} = \frac{ \Delta P^* \rho W^2 }{\mu^2},
51 \f]
52 and we choose to drive the flow by a dimensionless pressure drop of
53 \f$ \Delta P = 1. \f$ Using this non-dimensionalisation an
54 increase in the Reynolds number may be interpreted as in increase
55 in the applied (dimensional) pressure drop along the vessel. Note that
56 all pressures are defined relative to an external pressure, which we set
57 to zero.
58 
59 We assume that the solid's constitutive equation is given by
60 \c oomph-lib's generalised Hookean constitutive law and
61 non-dimensionalise the solid-mechanics stresses and tractions
62 with the Young's modulus \f$ E \f$.
63 
64 The FSI interaction parameter \f$ Q, \f$ which represents the ratio
65 of the (viscous) fluid stress scale to the reference stress used
66 to non-dimensionalise the solid stresses, is therefore given by
67 \f[
68 Q = \frac{\mu {\cal U}}{{\cal L} E} = \frac{\Delta P^*}{E} \ .
69 \f]

99 \section overview_implementation Overview of the implementation
100 
101 The general procedure described below is essentially the same as
102 that discussed in the <a href="../../unstructured_fsi/html/index.html">
103 two-dimensional unstructured FSI tutorial. </a> The use of
104 an unstructured mesh
105 means that the most convenient node-update strategy for the fluid mesh
106 is to treat it as a pseudo-elastic solid. Nonetheless,
107 the majority of the steps described below are the same as for
108 other fluid-structure-interaction problems.
109 
110  We reiterate that an
111  important prerequisite for the use of the automatic \c
112  FSI_functions is that each boundary between fluid and solid
113  meshes must be parametrised by boundary coordinates. Moreover, the
114  boundary-coordinate representations <b> must </b> be the same in both
115  the fluid and solid meshes. This use of a continuous-coordinate
116  representation of the boundaries means that the fluid and solid
117  meshes do not need to match at the boundaries; see the section \ref
118  matching_fluid_and_solid. Unfortunately, the construction of a global
119  surface parametrisation of a general surface is a non-trivial
120  problem. We side-step this problem by making use of the fact that any
121  third-party tetrahedral mesh generator represents domain
122  boundaries using a surface triangulation, or planar
123  surface facets. As the mesh is
124  imported into \c oomph-lib, each surface facet is treated as a
125  separate boundary and given a unique boundary identifier. The surface
126  parametrisation of each facet is then simply
127  given by local coordinates of the plane; see \ref boundary_coord.
128  A consequence of this
129  approach is that the physical boundaries will consist of several
130  mesh "boundaries", as already discussed in the single-physics problems.
131 
132 Since the driver code, discussed in detail below, is somewhat lengthy
133 (partly because of the large number of self-tests and diagnostics
134 included), we provide a brief overview of the main steps
135 required to solve this problem:
136 \n\n
137 -# Use the unstructured 3D mesh generator <A HREF="http://wias-berlin.de/software/tetgen//">
138  \c tetgen </A> to generate the solid mesh, using the procedure discussed in
139  <a href="../../../solid/unstructured_three_d_solid/html/index.html#mesh">
140  another tutorial.</a>
141  \n\n
142 -# Use the same procedure to generate the fluid mesh, as discussed in
143  <a href="../../../navier_stokes/unstructured_three_d_fluid/html/index.html#mesh">
144  the single-physics fluids tutorial.</a> Make sure that the
145  fluid mesh is derived from the \c SolidMesh base class to allow the use of
146  pseudo-elasticity to update the nodal positions in response
147  to the deformation of the domain boundary.
148  \n\n
149 -# Ensure that boundary coordinates are set up (consistently) on the
150  FSI interface between the two meshes. For meshes derived from
151  \c oomph-lib's \c TetgenMesh class, this may be done
152  by calling the function \c
153  TetgenMesh::setup_boundary_coordinates(); see the section
154  \ref boundary_coord for details.
155  \n\n
156 -# Attach \c FSISolidTractionElements to the faces of the "bulk"
157  solid elements that are exposed to the fluid flow. These
158  elements will apply the fluid traction to the solid.
159  \n\n
160 -# Combine the \c FSISolidTractionElements to a compound \c GeomObject
161  that provides a continuous representation of the solid's FSI
162  boundary, required by the \c
163  ImposeDisplacementByLagrangeMultiplierElements described below.
164  \n\n
165 -# Attach \c ImposeDisplacementByLagrangeMultiplierElements to the
166  faces of the "bulk" fluid elements that are adjacent to the
167  solid. These elements will employ Lagrange multipliers
168  to deform the pseudo-solid fluid mesh so that its shape remains
169  consistent with the motion of the solid's FSI boundary
170  (as described by the compound \c GeomObject created in the previous
171  step).
172  \n\n
173 -# Determine the "bulk" fluid elements that are adjacent to the
174  integration points of the \c FSISolidTractionElements,
175  using the function \c
176  FSI_functions::setup_fluid_load_info_for_solid_elements(...).
177  \n\n
178 .
179 
180 In our experience, just as in two-dimensions,
181 the most error-prone part of this procedure is the
182 identification of the mesh boundaries. In particular, if the
183 description of the FSI interface as viewed from the fluid and solid
184 meshes is inconsistent, the automatic matching of the unstructured
185 fluid and solid meshes will not work (see \ref boundary_coord for details). For
186 this reason, the driver code presented below generates a lot of
187 output that can be used to identify and fix such problems.
188 See also the section \ref go_wrong at the end of this tutorial.

197 global namespace. We define the Reynolds number, \f$ Re \f$, and

205 solid. For simplicity, the same constitutive equation will also be used for

208 equation can (and probably should) be used to control the mesh motion.

223 reference pressure is set to zero.)

638 \subsection boundary_coord How the boundary coordinates are generated
639 
640 The use of pseudo-elasticity for the node update in the fluid mesh
641 makes the solution of FSI problems extremely straightforward.
642 They key feature that allows the "automatic" coupling of the
643 unstructured fluid and solid meshes is the (consistent!) generation
644 of the boundary coordinates \f$ (\zeta_1,\zeta_2) \f$ along
645 the FSI interface. For reasons already mentioned, the procedure is more
646 involved than in the equivalent two-dimensional problem.
647 The implementation of this functionality in
648 \c TetgenMesh::setup_boundary_coordinates(...) exploits the
649 fact that
650 -# Meshes generated
651  by <A HREF="http://wias-berlin.de/software/tetgen//"> \c tetgen </A>
652  are bounded by planar facets, and \n\n
653 -# We insist that all facets are given distinct boundary IDs
654  when defining the domain in the tetgen <code>*.poly</code> file.
655 .
656 
657  The figure below illustrates the generation of the boundary
658 coordinates within a representative planar facet (shown in cyan)
659 that defines a particular mesh boundary.
660 
661 \image html boundary_coords_with_mesh.gif "Sketch illustrating the generation of boundary coordinates on a mesh boundary that is defined by a planar facet (shown in cyan). "
662 \image latex boundary_coords_with_mesh.eps "Sketch illustrating the generation of boundary coordinates on a mesh boundary that is defined by a planar facet (shown in cyan). " width=0.75\textwidth
663 
664 
665 Here is how we generate the boundary coordinates:
666 
667 -# We start by attaching \c FaceElements to the appropriate faces of
668  the "bulk" elements that are adjacent to the mesh boundary defined
669  by the facet. These \c FaceElements provide a surface triangulation
670  of the boundary. In the sketch above, the surface triangulation
671  is represented by the blue surface mesh. \n\n
672 -# We locate the "lower left" and "upper right" nodes
673  within the surface mesh, based on a lexicographical
674  ordering of the nodes' 3D coordinates. \n\n
675 -# The straight line from the "lower left" to the "upper right"
676  node (shown by the dashed red line) defines a direction that
677  is co-planar with the facet and therefore allows us to define
678  an in-plane unit vector, \f$ {\bf T}_1\f$, shown in red. \n\n
679 -# A second in-plane unit vector, \f$ {\bf T}_2\f$
680  (shown in yellow), can then be constructed by taking the cross-product
681  of \f$ {\bf T}_1\f$ and the outer unit normal \f$ {\bf N} \f$.
682  (The outer unit normal can be obtained from any of the adjacent
683  bulk elements.) \n\n
684 -# The orthonormal in-plane vectors \f$ {\bf T}_1\f$ and \f$ {\bf T}_2\f$
685  define a unique parametrisation of the boundary in terms of
686  the boundary coordinates \f$ (\zeta_1,\zeta_2) \f$, if we
687  insist that for every point on the boundary we have
688  \f[
689  {\bf R}(\zeta_1,\zeta_2) = {\bf R}_{\mbox{lower left}} +
690  \zeta_1 {\bf T}_1 + \zeta_2 {\bf T}_2.
691  \f]
692  This allows the assignment of boundary coordinates for each
693  of the nodes on this boundary. \n\n
694 -# Once this is done we can delete the \c FaceElements that defined
695  the surface triangulation. \n\n
696 .
697 
698 Since the generation of the boundary coordinates by the above
699 procedure only relies on the position of the "lower left" and
700 "upper right" vertices (which are
701 properties of the facet rather than the \c tetgen mesh) the
702 boundary coordinates are unique, regardless of the actual
703 discretisation generated by \c tetgen. Furthermore,
704 the boundary coordinates created from the fluid and solid meshes
705 will be consistent \e provided we reverse the direction of the outer
706 unit normal in one of the meshes. This is exactly what the
707 \c switch_normal flag in \c TetgenMesh::setup_boundary_coordinates(...)
708 is for. When generating the boundary coordinates for
709 our problem, we switched the direction of the normal
710 in the fluid mesh; see the section \ref fluid_mesh, above.
711

713 coordinates along the 12 mesh boundaries that constitute the
714 FSI boundary, when viewed from the fluid and the solid, respectively.
715 In each of the frames the contours indicate the value of
716 \f$ \zeta_1 \f$ within the respective boundary. Note that the
717 boundary coordinates established in the two meshes match, even
718 though the discretisations do not.

762 \subsection go_wrong What can go wrong?
763 Here are a few things that can (and often do) go wrong in
764 unstructured, three-dimensional FSI problems.
765 - <b>The facets that define the FSI boundary don't match:</b> \n\n
766  This tends to happen if the <code>*.poly</code> files
767  describing the fluid and solid meshes do not use
768  the same facets to describe the FSI interface. While we
769  do not expect the fluid and solid meshes themselves
770  to match, the representation of the FSI interface via
771  facets \c must be consistent. A simple way to ensure consistency
772  is to use the following procedure:
773  \n\n
774  -# Write the <code>*.poly</code> file for the fluid mesh.
775  Make sure that each facet is given a separate boundary ID
776  (at least for the facets describing the FSI interface).
777  \n\n
778  -# Solve a single-physics fluids problem on this mesh to check the
779  integrity of the mesh -- the solution of this problem will
780  also give you some insight into the expected flow field.
781  \n\n
782  -# Make a copy of the <code>*.poly</code> file for the fluid mesh
783  and modify it to describe the solid mesh, leaving the facets
784  that define the FSI interface unchanged. This automatically
785  ensures that the representation of the FSI boundary from the
786  fluid's and the solid's point of view is consistent.
787  \n\n
788  -# Solve a single-physics solids problem on this mesh to check the
789  integrity of the mesh, e.g. by applying some pressure loading on
790  the future FSI boundary -- the solution of this problem will
791  also give you some insight into the expected wall deformation.
792  \n\n
793  -# Couple the two problems, as shown in this tutorial.
794  \n\n
795  .
796  As an \b exercise, modify the tetgen <code>*.poly</code> files
797  and the driver code so that it can handle the extended domain
798  considered in the
799  <a href="../../../navier_stokes/unstructured_three_d_fluid/html/index.html#finer_mesh">
800  single-physics fluids problem.</a>
801  \n\n
802 - <b> An FSI (sub-)boundary is not planar:</b> \n\n
803  In an unstructured mesh, the FSI boundary will usually be
804  represented by a large number of distinct mesh boundaries.
805  The automatic setup of the boundary coordinates on these
806  mesh boundaries requires them to be (individually) planar -- at least in the
807  problem's initial configuration. Provided you have followed our advice
808  and associated each of the facets that define the FSI boundary
809  with a different boundary ID, you should not have any problems as
810  \c tetgen does not allow non-planar facets.
811  \n\n
812 - <b> The solver does not converge:</b> \n\n
813  A large-displacement FSI problem is a highly nonlinear problem and the
814  provision of a good initial guess for the Newton iteration
815  is essential. We tend to proceed as follows:
816  \n\n
817  -# Follow the steps outlined above, to study the constituent
818  single-physics problems first. This will already give you
819  some insight into the behaviour of the problem. For instance,
820  the solution of the single-physics Navier-Stokes equations at
821  the desired Reynolds number may have to be computed via a parameter
822  incrementation, starting from Stokes flow. Similarly, the
823  study of the single-physics solid problem will reveal what
824  load increments can be accommodated without causing the
825  Newton iteration to diverge.
826  \n\n
827  -# Combine the two single-physics problems
828  using the steps described in this tutorial.
829  \n\n
830  -# Switch off the fluid-structure interaction by
831  \n\n
832  -# setting the FSI parameter to zero, \f$ Q = 0, \f$
833  \n\n
834  -# commenting out the call to
835  \c FSI_functions::setup_fluid_load_info_for_solid_elements(...),
836  \n\n
837  -# not creating the Lagrange-multiplier elements that
838  enforce the solid displacements onto the fluid mesh.
839  .
840  \n\n
841  This completely uncouples the two problems, although they will be
842  solved simultaneously, allowing you
843  to replicate the single-physics parameter studies already
844  performed.
845  \n\n
846  -# Now switch on the various interactions, one-by-one, while
847  carefully validating the results at each stage. We tend to
848  proceed as follows:
849  \n\n
850  -# Solve the uncoupled problem at zero Reynolds number.
851  \n\n
852  -# Increase the Reynolds number in small increments to its
853  target value.
854  \n\n
855  -# Re-enable the call to
856  \c FSI_functions::setup_fluid_load_info_for_solid_elements(...)
857  and attach the Lagrange multiplier elements but keep
858  \f$ Q = 0. \f$ If the solid is not loaded by any
859  forces/tractions other than those arising from the fluid,
860  the solution should not change as the solid remains unloaded.
861  \n\n
862  -# Gently increase the value of the FSI parameter, \f$ Q \f$,
863  remembering that it represents the ratio of the typical
864  viscous fluid stresses to the stiffness of the solid.
865  Hence, even relatively small values of \f$ Q \f$
866  tend to lead to relatively large solid displacements.
867  Be gentle!
868  .
869  .
870  \n\n
