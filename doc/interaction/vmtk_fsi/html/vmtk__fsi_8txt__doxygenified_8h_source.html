3 \mainpage Demo problem: Steady finite-Reynolds-number flow through an elastic iliac bifurcation
4 This tutorial demonstrates how to simulate physiological
5 fluid-structure interaction problems, based on medical imaging
6 data, processed with the <a href="http://www.vmtk.org">Vascular
7 Modeling Toolkit.</a>
8 
9  We combine two single-physics problems, namely
10 - <a href="../../../solid/vmtk_solid/html/index.html">
11  The inflation of a blood vessel</a>
12 - <a href="../../../navier_stokes/vmtk_fluid/html/index.html">
13  Finite-Reynolds number flow through a rigid iliac bifurcation</a>
14 .
15 to study the steady flow through an elastic iliac bifurcation.
16 (Please refer to \c oomph-lib's
17 <a href="../../../meshes/mesh_from_vmtk/html/index.html">VMTK
18 tutorial</a> to find out how to generate \c oomph-lib meshes
19 from medical images.)
20 
21 The tutorial also provides a detailed discussion of the mechanism by which
22 \c FaceElements introduce additional unknowns into a problem.
23 This is important in the problem considered here because we
24 employ two different types of \c FaceElements, both of which
25 introduce additional unknowns. When dealing with nodes that
26 are attached to both types of \c FaceElements we must therefore be able
27 to determine which nodal values are associated with which \c FaceElement.
28 The section \ref face below shows how this is done.

50 \section prob The problem (and some results)
51 The animations below show the deformation of an iliac bifurcation
52 that conveys viscous fluid and deforms in response to the traction
53 that the fluid exerts onto it. As in the previous examples
54 where we considered the constituent single-physics
55 <a href="../../../navier_stokes/vmtk_fluid/html/index.html">
56 fluid</a> and <a href="../../../solid/vmtk_solid/html/index.html">
57 solid</a> problems, the meshes are obviously very coarse and the
58 boundary conditions are far from "physiologically realistic":
59 We keep the upstream and downstream "ends" of the vessel
60 wall in a fixed position and drive the (steady!) flow by applying
61 a constant pressure drop between the in- and outflow cross sections.
62 The different frames shown in the animation show results
63 for different wall stiffnesses, using the same setup as in
64 <a href="../../unstructured_three_d_fsi/html/index.html">another
65 tutorial.</a>
66 
67 We stress that the computations presented here serve as
68 quick-to-compute proof of concept and refer to the section
69 \ref comm_ex at the end of this tutorial for a discussion
70 on how to make the simulation more "realistic".

93 \section face How FaceElements introduce additional unknowns into a problem
94 
95 \c FaceElements are used widely throughout \c oomph-lib to apply
96 Neumann/flux/traction-type boundary conditions on the faces of
97 higher-dimensional "bulk" elements. Examples include:
98 - <a href="../../../poisson/two_d_poisson_flux_bc2/html/index.html">
99  the application of a flux boundary condition in a Poisson problem;</a>
100 - the application of traction boundary conditions in
101  <a href="../../../navier_stokes/rayleigh_traction_channel/html/index.html">
102  fluid</a> and
103  <a href="../../../solid/airy_cantilever/html/index.html">solid</a>
104  mechanics problems;
105 - <a href="../../../young_laplace/contact_angle/html/index.html">the
106  application of a contact-angle boundary condition for the
107  Young-Laplace equation.</a>
108 .
109 In all the examples listed above, the boundary conditions simply
110 add a contribution to the elements' residuals but they do not
111 introduce any additional unknowns into the problem.
112 
113 \c FaceElements may also be used to apply boundary conditions
114 via Lagrange multipliers. An example is given in the tutorial
115 that discusses
116 - <a href="../../../solid/prescribed_displ_lagr_mult/html/index.html">
117  the application of displacement boundary conditions for solid
118  mechanics problems</a>
119 .
120 In such problems, the Lagrange multipliers must be determined
121 as part of the solution, and storage for the associated discrete
122 unknowns is created at the nodes of the \c FaceElements.
123 
124  To explain the relevant details of the implementation we consider a
125 simple 2D Navier-Stokes problem discretised using nine-node Taylor-Hood
126 elements (in these elements each vertex node stores two discrete velocities and
127 one pressure; the other nodes store only two velocity degrees of
128 freedom). We assume that boundaries 0 and 1 are subject to
129 boundary conditions imposed via \c FaceElements, and that
130 each boundary condition introduces its own Lagrange multipliers field<b>s</b>.
131 [Yes, the plural is correct. As an example, consider the case of
132 imposing displacement constraints in a 2D solid mechanics problem via
133 Lagrange multipliers. In this approach
134 the imposition of the boundary condition requires \e two Lagrange
135 multipliers along each constrained boundary. Physically, the Lagrange
136 multipliers represent the two components of the surface traction required to
137 deform the boundary into the required shape; see
138 <a href="../../../solid/prescribed_displ_lagr_mult/html/index.html">
139 the relevant solid mechanics problem</a> for details.]
140 
141 The sketch below shows the discretisation of the domain, with the black
142 circles representing the nodes. The enlargement of the top right
143 corner also shows the discrete unknowns (nodal velocities and
144 pressures) stored at each node after the creation of the
145 "bulk" Navier-Stokes elements.

166 It is then easy to identify the additional nodal values associated
167 with the \c FaceElement in order to apply the boundary conditions

169 The methodology is illustrated in the

195 The identification of the additional degrees of freedom via a simple
196 offset from the degrees of freedom created by the "bulk" element
197 is now no longer possible. We therefore provide an alternative
198 mechanism to access the relevant information from the nodes
199 themselves via the function
200 \code
201 unsigned BoundaryNodeBase::index_of_first_value_assigned_by_face_element()
202 \endcode
203 which does exactly what it says. If only a single type of \c
204 FaceElement is attached to a (boundary) node, the unsigned that is returned
205 by this function is exactly the same as the unsigned that is returned
206 by the corresponding call to \c FaceElement::nbulk_value(...).
207 To cater for the case where multiple \c FaceElements are
208 attached to the same node, the above function can take an ID
209 (which defaults to zero) that identifies which type of
210 \c FaceElement we are dealing with, so the full interface is, in fact,
211 \code
212 unsigned BoundaryNodeBase::index_of_first_value_assigned_by_face_element(const unsigned& id=0)
213 \endcode
214 The ID must be established by the user, typically when the
215 constructor of the specific \c FaceElement is called. It can then
216 be passed on to the \c Nodes when the number of values at the nodes is
217 adjusted to accommodate the additional values required by the
218 \c FaceElement.
219

227 has the usual two arguments that specify the pointer to the "bulk"
228 element, and the index of the face that the \c FaceElement is to
229 be attached to. The final (optional) argument allows the specification

276 \section driver_code The driver code for the FSI problem
277 
278  The driver code for the flow through the elastic iliac is almost
279 identical to that in the
280 <a href="../../unstructured_three_d_fsi/html/index.html">
281 tutorial considering the same problem in a more simplistic
282 geometry.</a> Comparing the two driver codes using
283 
284 \code
285 sdiff demo_drivers/interaction/vmtk_fsi/vmtk_fsi.cc \
286  demo_drivers/interaction/unstructured_three_d_fsi/unstructured_three_d_fsi.cc
287 \endcode
288 
289 shows that the only differences relate to the facts that:
290 - although the meshes for the two problems are topologically
291  equivalent, the boundary numbers assigned by
292  \c Tetgen are different.
293 - we impose parallel flow at the vessel's in- and outflow
294  cross-sections, none of which are not aligned with any of the Cartesian
295  coordinate planes. The parallel flow and the imposed pressure
296  drop are therefore enforced by attaching
297  \c ImposeParallelOutflowElements, rather than
298  \c NavierStokesTractionElements to the in- and outflow boundaries.
299  We refer to the corresponding
300  <a href="../../../navier_stokes/vmtk_fluid/html/index.html#parallel_flow">
301  single-physics fluids problem</a> for more details on this aspect.
302 - The problem involves two different types of \c FaceElements:
303  - \c ImposeParallelOutflowElements are used
304  to impose parallel flow at the in- and outflow cross-sections.
305  - \c ImposeDisplacementByLagrangeMultiplerElements are used to
306  deform the boundary of the pseudo-solid fluid mesh
307  to reflect the changes in the geometry of the
308  vessel wall.
309  .
310  Since both \c FaceElements introduce additional unknowns
311  into the problem, we use the methodology described
312  <a href="#face">above</a> to distinguish between the two
313  types of Lagrange multipliers.
314 .
315 
316 
317 Once again, we shall discuss only those aspects of the code
318 that are changed from the driver code
319 discussed in the <a href="../../unstructured_three_d_fsi/html/index.html">
320 previous tutorial.</a>

336 \subsection constructor The Problem constructor
337 
338 The general structure of the problem constructor remains
339 unchanged. There are a few trivial changes in the import of fluid and solid
340 meshes to reflect the fact that the tetgen boundary numbers are different.
341 
342 When imposing the boundary conditions for the fluid mesh
343 we do <b> not </b> pin the transverse velocities at the in- and outlets because
344 the parallel flow is now imposed by Lagrange multiplier elements.
345 Hence the only boundary condition to be applied at the in- and
346 outflow cross-sections is the pinning of the nodal positions.
347 
348 The only significant change arises in the application of the boundary
349 conditions for the Lagrange multipliers. Recall that
350 \c ImposeDisplacementByLagrangeMultiplierElements are used
351 to deform the FSI boundary of the fluid mesh so that it
352 stays in contact with the vessel wall. This constraint
353 must be applied along the entire FSI boundary, apart from
354 the lines along which it meets the in- and outflow boundaries
355 where the position of the fluid nodes is already pinned.
356 Consequently, we pin the Lagrange multipliers in this part of
357 the mesh.
358 
359  Similarly, the \c ImposeParallelOutflowElements ensure that the
360 velocity in the in- and outflow cross-sections is orthogonal to
361 these cross-sections. This constraint must be applied along the
362 entirety of the in- and outflow boundaries, apart from the
363 lines along which they meet the FSI interface where the fluid
364 velocity is already determined by the no-slip condition.
365 Consequently, we also pin the second set of Lagrange multipliers
366 along this part of the fluid mesh boundary.
367 
368  We loop over all the fluid nodes on the FSI boundary:
369

371 \until boundary_node_pt(b,inod);
372 
373 For each node we apply the no-slip condition on the wall by
374 pinning all three velocity components,
375 
376 \until pin(2);
377 
378 and determine whether the node also happens to be located on the
379 in- or outflow cross-sections:
380 
381 \until now we know if the node is
382 
383 If it is, we pin the Lagrange multipliers associated
384 with the \c ImposeParallelFlowElements, using
385 the \c BoundaryNodeBase::index_of_first_value_assigned_by_face_element(...)
386 function referred to <a href="#face">above</a>, and specifying
387 the appropriate ID.
388 
389 \until }
390 
391 We repeat the same procedure for the Lagrange multipliers associated
392 with the \c ImposeDisplacementByLagrangeMultiplierElements:
393 
394 \until }
395 
396 As usual we document the position of the nodes at which we pinned
397 the Lagrange multipliers in a file to allow for an external sanity check.

399 \until end of BC for fluid mesh

442 \subsection realism Making the simulation more "realistic"
443 The simulation shown at the beginning of this tutorial is
444 obviously very crude and suffers from (at least) the sum of the
445 shortcomings that we identified in the tutorials for the constituent
446 single-physics
447 <a href="../../../navier_stokes/vmtk_fluid/html/index.html#realism">fluid</a>
448 and <a href="../../../solid/vmtk_solid/html/index.html#finer"> solid</a>
449 problems. You should consider repeating the computation using finer
450 meshes (consult \c oomph-lib's
451 <a href="../../../meshes/mesh_from_vmtk/html/index.html">VMTK
452 tutorial</a> for details) and explore the use of "flow extensions"
453 which allow the (inevitably artificial) boundary conditions to be applied
454 further from the region of interest. Adding time-dependence to the
455 problem, e.g. by subjecting the flow to a periodic fluctuation
456 in the applied pressure drop would be another interesting
457 exercise.
