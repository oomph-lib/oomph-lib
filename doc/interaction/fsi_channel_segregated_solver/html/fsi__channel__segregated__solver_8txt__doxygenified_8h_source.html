3 \mainpage Segregated solvers for fluid-structure-interaction problems: Revisiting the flow in a 2D collapsible channel
4 
5 In this document we discuss the implementation of segregated solution
6 strategies for multi-physics problems, in particular fluid-structure
7 interaction, within \c oomph-lib. The method illustrated by
8 revisiting the fluid-structure interaction problem of
9 <A HREF="../../fsi_collapsible_channel/html/index.html">
10  finite-Reynolds-number flow in a 2D collapsible channel;</A> an
11  example discussed in detail in <CENTER>
12 <a href="http://www.springerlink.com/content/m3r6318701g338g4/">Heil, M., Hazel, A.L. & Boyle, J. (2008): Solvers for large-displacement fluid-structure interaction problems: Segregated vs. monolithic approaches. Computational Mechanics.</a>
13 </CENTER>
14 
15 where we compare the relative performance of segregated and monolithic
16 solvers. Since the paper comes to the conclusion that, despite
17 various claims in the literature, segregated solvers are not
18 necessarily more efficient than fully-coupled monolithic schemes
19 (of the type employed in \c oomph-lib) you should also consult
20 the <a href="../../../preconditioners/fsi/html/index.html">related
21 tutorial on the monolithic solution of the problem
22 with \c oomph-lib's FSI preconditioner.</A>

36 <B>Flow in a 2D collapsible channel </B>
37 </CENTER>
38 
39 The figure below shows a sketch of the problem:
40 Flow is driven by a
41 prescribed Poiseuille flow \f$U^{*}_{p}\f$ through a 2D channel of width
42 \f$ H^* \f$ and total length
43 \f$ L^*_{total} = L^*_{up} + L^*_{collapsible} + L^*_{down}. \f$
44 The upstream and downstream lengths of the channel are rigid, whereas
45 the upper wall in the central section is an elastic membrane whose
46 shape is parametrised by a Lagrangian coordinate, \f$ \xi^* \f$ , so
47 that the position vector to the moving wall is given by
48 \f$ {\bf R}_w^*(\xi^*,t^*) \f$ . The wall is loaded by the
49 external pressure \f$ p_{ext}^* \f$ and by the traction
50 that the viscous fluid exerts on it.
51 
52 \image html collapsible_channel_sketch.gif "Sketch of the problem. "
53 \image latex collapsible_channel_sketch.eps "Sketch of the problem. " width=0.75\textwidth
54 
55 The non-dimensionalisation and governing equations have already been
56 discussed in the
57 <A HREF="../../fsi_collapsible_channel/html/index.html"> previous
58 (monolithic) example. </A>
59 The problem is not quite the same, however, because the upstream
60 boundary condition is now one of prescribed flow, rather than prescribed
61 pressure:
62 - Prescribed inflow,
63  \f[
64  {\bf u}(x_1,x_2) = {\bf u}_{p}(x_1,x_2) =
65  6 \ x_2 \ (1-x_2) \ {\bf e}_1.
66  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1)
67  \f] at \f$ x_{1}=0\f$.
68 .
69 All other boundary conditions remain the same.

82 \section reslt Results
83 The behaviour of the system under the prescribed-inflow boundary
84 conditions is somewhat different to its behaviour when the pressure
85 drop is prescribed. In the first instance, we consider steady states,
86 in which all time-derivatives are neglected. The figure below shows
87 steady flows at a Reynolds number of \f$ Re=500 \f$ and two values
88 of the fluid-structure-interaction parameter, \f$ Q =
89 10^{-4} \f$ (upper) and \f$ Q = 10^{-2}\f$ (lower). For low values of
90 \f$Q\f$, corresponding to weak fluid-structure interaction, the
91 deformation of the wall is approximately symmetric, being dominated by
92 the external pressure. As \f$Q\f$
93 increases, the influence of fluid traction can been seen in the
94 asymmetric deformation of the elastic wall. The viscous pressure
95 drop along the tube leads to higher pressure upstream (causing an
96 outward deformation) and lower pressures downstream (causing an inward
97 deflection).
98 
99 \image html steady_flows.gif "Steady flows at Re=500 and Q=10e-4 (upper), Q=10e-2 (lower). "
100 \image latex steady_flows.eps "Steady flows at Re=500 and Q=10e-4 (upper), Q=10e-2 (lower). " width=0.8\textwidth
101 
102 The overall behaviour of the system can be characterised by steady
103 load-displacement curves in which the vertical position of a
104 control point on the elastic
105 section of the channel wall is plotted as a function of the external
106 pressure.
107 
108 \image html steady_trace.gif "Load-displacement curve: the vertical position of a control point on the elastic wall (located at 50, 50, 60 and 70 percent of its length for Q = 0, 10e-4, 10e-3 and 10e-2, respectively) as a function of the external pressure. "
109 \image latex steady_trace.eps "Load-displacement curve: the vertical position of a control point on the elastic wall (located at 50, 50, 60 and 70 percent of its length for Q = 0, 10e-4, 10e-3 and 10e-2, respectively) as a function of the external pressure. " width=0.5\textwidth
110 
111  At low \f$Q\f$, the displacement is directly proportional to
112 the external pressure. As \f$Q\f$ increases the curves shift to the
113 right because a large external pressure is required to keep the wall
114 in its undeformed position;
115 a consequence of the increased viscous pressure drop \em and the
116 boundary condition that \f$p=0\f$ at the outlet. A second consequence
117 of increasing \f$Q\f$ is that (at finite Reynolds number) a
118 smaller increase in external pressure is required to achieve
119 a given degree of collapse. This is because the Bernoulli
120 effect reduces the fluid pressure in the region that is
121 most strongly collapsed and therefore increases the compressive
122 load on the wall. For \f$Q = 10^{-2}\f$ two limit
123 points develop on the load-displacement curve, indicating that the wall
124 "snaps through" into a collapsed buckled configuration when
125 \f$p_{ext}\f$ becomes sufficiently large. The appearance of the
126 limit points means that it is no longer possible to perform the
127 steady parameter study by slowly increasing \f$p_{ext}: \f$
128 At sufficiently large values of \f$Q\f$ the displacement of the control
129 point is not a single-valued function of the external pressure
130 \f$ p_{ext}\f$. However, the application of "displacement control",
131 described in the tutorial discussing the
132 <A HREF="../../../beam/steady_ring/html/index.html">
133 large-displacement post-buckling of an elastic ring</A> is sufficient
134 to circumvent this difficulty: We treat the external pressure as an unknown
135 and control the channel's collapse by prescribing the vertical position
136 of the control point, \f$ x_2^{[ctrl]}.\f$ This resolves the problem
137 because the curve \f$ p_{ext}(x_2^{[ctrl]})\f$ is single-valued,
138 allowing us to perform the parameter study by slowly increasing
139 the wall collapse by reducing \f$ x_2^{[ctrl]}\f$, computing the
140 pressure required to achieve this deformation as part of the solution.

145 \section overview Overview: Segregated solution strategies with oomph-lib
146 
147 The general methodology for setting up fluid-structure-interaction
148 problems is discussed in
149 <A HREF="../../fsi_collapsible_channel/html/index.html#overview">another
150 tutorial; </A>
151 and we shall assume that the standard monolithic problem has already
152 been written. In the present example, the monolithic problem class \c
153 FSICollapsibleChannelProblem is
154 specified in the header file
155 <A HREF="../../../../demo_drivers/interaction/fsi_channel_seg_and_precond/fsi_chan_problem.h">
156 fsi_chan_problem.h</A>
157 
158 Having specified the monolithic (fully-coupled) discretisation, our segregated
159 solution strategy proceeds by alternating between fluid and solid
160 solves: Initially, the degrees of freedom
161 associated with the (pure) solid mechanics problem are "pinned" and the
162 global assembly procedure is modified to omit the corresponding
163 solid elements. The Newton solver will, therefore, solve the fluid
164 equations with a "frozen" wall shape. Next, the degrees of freedom
165 associated with the (pure) fluid mechanics problem are pinned and the
166 original boundary conditions for the solid mechanics problem are
167 re-assigned. The assembly procedure
168 is now modified so that only solid elements contribute to the
169 global system. The Newton solver will then solve for a new wall shape
170 corresponding to the tractions exerted by the given flow
171 field. At this point we allow for under-relaxation, i.e. we provide
172 the option to increment the solid mechanics degrees of freedom
173 by a fraction of the change computed by the Newton solver.
174 These two steps are repeated in a fixed-point iteration which
175 continues until a given convergence criterion is satisfied, or a
176 maximum number of iterations is exceeded.
177 We note that different linear solvers/preconditioners
178 may be specified for solution of the linear systems
179 arising during the Newton iteration for the isolated "fluid" and
180 "solid" problems, allowing the re-use of optimal solution methods
181 for individual sub-problems. This is generally perceived to be one
182 of the key advantages of segregated solvers.

187 \section how_its_done Brief discussion of the implementation
188 
189 \subsection seg_problem The SegregatableFSIProblem
190 
191 <b>a. Overall structure</b>
192 
193 The
194 <A HREF="../../../the_data_structure/html/classoomph_1_1SegregatableFSIProblem.html">
195  \c SegregatableFSIProblem </A> class is used to implement our
196 segregated solution strategy within \c oomph-lib. The most important
197 problem-specific task is to divide all the problem data
198 into distinct fluid and solid degrees of freedom
199 and to partition the monolithic mesh into a mesh of fluid
200 elements and a mesh of solid elements. The problem-specific
201 partitioning should be implemented in the (pure) virtual member
202 function
203 \code
204  /// Identify fluid and solid data
205  virtual void identify_fluid_and_solid_dofs(Vector<Data*>& fluid_data_pt,
206  Vector<Data*>& solid_data_pt,
207  Mesh*& fluid_mesh_pt,
208  Mesh*& solid_mesh_pt)=0;
209 \endcode
210  which returns vectors of fluid and solid data and the meshes of fluid
211  and solid elements. This virtual function is called within the
212  member function
213 \code
214  /// \short Set up segregated solver. The optional boolean argument
215  /// defaults to true and causes the identify_fluid_and_solid_dofs(...)
216  /// to be called again. This is required, e.g. if any of the
217  /// meshes were adapted since the previous call to the segregated solver.
218  void setup_segregated_solver(const bool &full_setup_of_fluid_and_solid_dofs=true)
219 \endcode
220  which \em must be
221  called immediately before every segregated solve. The optional boolean
222  flag may be set to \c false if the solid and fluid meshes
223  have not changed between solves (i.e. if no spatial adaptation was
224  performed since the last call to the segregated solver).
225  The \c setup_segregated_solver(...)
226  function must still be called, however, in order that
227  data associated with convergence acceleration techniques is reset to
228  its default values.
229 
230 <b>b. The segregated solvers</b>
231 
232  The class inherits from the standard \c Problem
233  class, which provides the standard (monolithic) \c newton_solve() and
234  related functions. Thus any \c SegregatedFSIProblem can be solved
235  "monolithically" as normal and, moreover, it is straightforward to ensure that
236  exactly the same system is being solved when comparing monolithic and
237  segregated solutions. The segregated solution strategy is implemented in
238  the analogous member functions:
239  - The equivalent of the monolithic \c Problem::newton_solve() is
240  \code
241  SegregatedFSIProblem::segregated_solve();
242  \endcode
243  - The equivalent of the monolithic \c Problem::steady_newton_solve() is
244  \code
245  SegregatedFSIProblem::steady_segregated_solve();
246  \endcode
247  - Finally, the equivalent of \c Problem::unsteady_newton_solve(dt) is
248  \code
249  PicardConvergenceData unsteady_segregated_solve(const double &dt);
250  \endcode
251  .
252 All three functions return an instance of a \c PicardConvergenceData
253 object which stores the convergence statistics of the segregated solve.
254 
255  In addition, the virtual member functions
256  \code
257  SegregatedFSIProblem::actions_before_segregated_solve()
258  \endcode
259  \code
260  SegregatedFSIProblem::actions_after_segregated_solve()
261  \endcode
262  \code SegregatedFSIProblem::actions_before_segregated_convergence_check()
263  \endcode
264  are provided to allow the user to specify any actions, such as
265 initialisation of
266  counters, mesh updates, output, etc, that should be performed before or after
267  each complete segregated solve. Note that the \c Problem member functions
268  \code
269  Problem::actions_before_newton_solve()
270 \endcode
271 \code
272  Problem::actions_after_newton_solve()
273 \endcode
274 \code
275  Problem::actions_before_newton_convergence_check()
276  \endcode
277  are called as usual during the Newton solve of each
278  sub-problem and may be used for fine-grained operations that should
279  be performed before or after each fluid or solid solve.
280  For this purpose, the \c SegregatedFSIProblem provides a flag,
281 \c int \c SegregatedFSIProblem::Solve_type that indicates which
282 (sub-)solve is currently being performed. The flag can take
283 the (enumerated) values \c SegregatedFSIProblem::Full_solve,
284 \c SegregatedFSIProblem::Fluid_solve and
285 \c SegregatedFSIProblem::Solid_solve, allowing the user to perform
286 specific actions during the distinct sub-solves.
287 
288 <b>c. Choosing the convergence criterion</b>
289 
290 
291  Other public member functions provided
292  by the \c SegregatedFSIProblem class are used to specify the
293  convergence criterion for the global fixed-point iteration:
294  \code
295  /// Base convergence based on max. global residual
296  void assess_convergence_based_on_max_global_residual(const double &tol)
297  \endcode
298  \code
299  /// Base convergence on maximum absolute change of solid degrees of freedom
300  void assess_convergence_based_on_absolute_solid_change(const double &tol)
301  \endcode
302  \code
303  /// Base convergence on maximum relative change of solid degrees of freedom
304  void assess_convergence_based_on_relative_solid_change(const double &tol)
305  \endcode
306  If a tolerance is not specified the default \c
307  Problem::Newton_solver_tolerance is used.
308 
309 <b>d. Under-relaxation</b>
310 
311  Finally, there are several member functions that are used to specify
312  the convergence-acceleration techniques:
313  -# <b>Static under-relaxation:</b>
314  \n\n
315  \code
316  //Use under-relaxation for solid degrees of freedom and specify
317  //the optional under-relaxation parameter. The default of 1.0
318  //corresponds to no under-relaxation.
319  void use_under_relaxation (const double &omega=1.0)
320  \endcode
321  If this function is called, under-relaxation is performed after the
322  solid sub-solve, i.e. each solid degree of freedom, \f$ s \f$, say
323  is updated via
324  \f[
325  s = s_{new} + (1-\omega) (s_{old} - s_{new})
326  \f]
327  where \f$ s_{new} \f$ is the new value computed by the Newton solver
328  and \f$ s_{old} \f$ is its previous value.
329  -# <b>Adaptive under-relaxation:</b>
330  \n\n
331  \code
332  //Boolean flag that controls whether Irons & Tuck extrapolation
333  //is used to dynamically modify the under-relaxation parameter for
334  //the under-relaxation of the solid degrees of freedom.
335  void enable_irons_and_tuck_extrapolation ()
336  \endcode
337  If this function is called (and if under-relaxation is enabled)
338  the under-relaxation parameter \f$ \omega \f$ is adjusted
339  throughout the fixed-point iteration, using Irons & Tucks
340  convergence acceleration procedure; see Irons, B.M. & Tuck, R.C.
341  "A version of the Aitken accelerator for computer iteration".
342  International Journal of Numerical Methods in Engineering <b>1</b>,
343  275-277 (1969).
344  -# <b>Pointwise Aitken-acceleration:</b>
345  \n\n
346  \code
347  //Set a boolean flag that controls whether pointwise Aitken
348  //extrapolation is used. The optional argument specifies the Picard
349  //Iteration after which the extrapolation is to be used for the first
350  //time. The default value is zero.
351  void enable_pointwise_aitken (const unsigned &pointwise_aitken_start)
352  \endcode
353  If this function is called, the classical Aitken extrapolation is
354  used to accelerate the convergence of (individual) solid degrees of freedom
355  after every three iterations.
356  .

410 \subsection identify Identifying the fluid and solid degrees of freedom
411 
412 The underlying monolithic problem provides pointers to the
413 fluid and solid (sub-)meshes via the member data
414 \code
415  AlgebraicCollapsibleChannelMesh<ELEMENT>* Bulk_mesh_pt;

421 \c SegregatedFSICollapsibleChannelProblem::bulk_mesh_pt()
422 and \c SegregatedFSICollapsibleChannelProblem::wall_mesh_pt(),
423 and so the identification of fluid and solid degrees of freedom is
424 reasonably straightforward. The only complication arises because we
425 may, or may not, be using displacement control which introduces
426 a further element into the global mesh. Displacement control
427 affects the solid problem suggesting that the (variable) external
428 pressure should be regarded as a solid degrees of freedom and the \c

438

445 solution of the solid problem.

488 \subsection comments Comments
489 
490 
491 - <b>(In-)efficiency of \c setup_segregated_solver()</b> \n\n
492  In our simple example code, we did not employ spatial adaptivity. It is
493  not necessary, therefore, to (re-)identify the fluid and solid degrees of
494  freedom before each solve, the default (safe) behaviour of \c
495  setup_segregated_solver(). Nonetheless, data associated with
496  the techniques used to accelerate the convergence of the Picard
497  iterations must be reset before each segregated solve. In the
498  more complex driver code, a boolean
499  flag \c bool \c full_setup is used as an argument to \c
500  setup_segregated_solver() which modifies the behaviour, as
501  indicated below.
502  \code
503  // Boolean flag used to specify whether a full setup of solid and fluid dofs
504  // is required
505  bool full_setup = true;
506 
507  // Parameter study
508  for (unsigned istep=0;istep<Flags::Nsteps;istep++)
509  {
510  // Setup segregated solver
511  setup_segregated_solver(full_setup);
512 
513  [...]
514 
515  steady_segregated_solve()
516 
517  [...]
518 
519  //We no longer need a full setup of the dofs
520  full_setup = false;
521  }
522  \endcode
