3 \mainpage The equations of time-harmonic linear elasticity and use of PMLs
4 
5 The aim of this tutorial is to demonstrate the solution of the
6 time-harmonic equations of linear elasticity in Cartesian
7 coordinates. These equations are useful to describe forced, time-harmonic
8 oscillations of elastic bodies. The implementation extends
9 the standard formulation discussed in
10 <a href="../../time_harmonic_linear_elasticity/elastic_annulus/html/index.html">
11 another tutorial,</a>
12 which is modified to incorporate perfectly matched layers (PMLs) as an
13 alternative to classical absorbing/approximate boundary conditions or DtN maps.
14 
15 We first present details on the relevant theory and its extensions and
16 proceed with an example problem of time-harmonic oscillations on an infinite
17 domain with an interior circular boundary.

39 \section theory Theory
40 Consider an infinite, linearly elastic body (of density \f$ \rho \f$, Young's
41 modulus \f$ E \f$ and Poisson's ratio \f$ \nu \f$), occupying
42 the region \f$ D \f$
43 whose inner boundary is \f$ \partial D_d \f$. Assuming that the body performs
44 time-harmonic oscillations of frequency of \f$ \omega \f$
45 its motion is governed by the equations of time-harmonic
46 linear elasticity
47 <CENTER>
48 \f[
49 {\pmb{\nabla}^*}\cdot\pmb{\tau}^*+
50 \rho \pmb{F}^*={\rm -\rho\omega^2}\pmb{u}^*,
51 \f]
52 </CENTER>
53 where the \f$ x_{i}^* \f$ are the Cartesian coordinates, and
54 the time-periodic stresses, body force and displacements are given by
55 \f$ {\rm Re}\{\pmb{\tau^*}(x_{i}^*){\rm e}^{-{\rm i}\omega
56 t^*}\} \f$,
57 \f$ {\rm Re}\{\mathbf{F}^*(x_{i}^*){\rm e}^{-{\rm i}\omega
58 t^*}\} \f$ and \f$ {\rm Re}\{\mathbf{u}^*(x_{i}^*){\rm e}^{-{\rm i}\omega
59 t^*}\} \f$ respectively. Note that, as usual, the superscript
60 asterisk notation is used to distinguish dimensional quantities
61 from their non-dimensional counterparts where required.
62 
63 The body is subject to imposed time-harmonic displacements
64 \f$ {\rm Re}\{\mathbf{\hat{u}}^* {\rm e}^{-{\rm i}\omega
65 t^*}\} \f$
66 along \f$ \partial D_d \f$. This requires that
67 <CENTER>
68 \f[
69 \mathbf{u}^*=\mathbf{\hat{u}}^*\,\,\textrm{on $
70 \partial D_d. $ }
71 \f]
72 </CENTER>
73 
74 The stresses and displacements are related by the constitutive equations
75 <CENTER>
76 \f[
77 \pmb{\tau}^*=\frac{E}{1+\nu}\left(
78 \frac{\nu}{1-2\nu}(\pmb{\nabla}^*\cdot\mathbf{u}^*)\textbf{I}+
79 \frac{1}{2}(\pmb{\nabla}^*\mathbf{u}^*+(\pmb{\nabla}^*\mathbf{u}^*){{\rm T}})\right),
80 \f]
81 </CENTER>
82 where \f$ (\pmb{\nabla}^*\mathbf{u}^*)^{{\rm T}}
83 \f$ represents the transpose of \f$ \pmb{\nabla}^*\mathbf{u}^*.
84 \f$
85 
86 We non-dimensionalise the equations, using a problem specific reference
87 length, \f$ {\cal L} \f$, and a timescale \f$ \displaystyle {\cal T}=\frac{1}{\omega} \f$, and use
88 Young's modulus to non-dimensionalise the body force and the stress tensor:
89 <CENTER>
90 \f[
91 \pmb{\tau}^* = E \, \pmb{\tau}, \qquad
92 x_{i}^* = {\cal L}\, x_{i}
93 \f]
94 \f[
95 \mathbf{u}^* = {\cal L}\, \mathbf{u}, \qquad
96 \mathbf{F}^* = \frac{E}{\rho \cal L} \, \mathbf{F},
97 \qquad
98 t^* = {\cal T}\, t.
99 \f]
100 </CENTER>
101 
102 The non-dimensional form of the equations is then given by
103 <CENTER>
104 \f[
105 \pmb{\nabla}\cdot\pmb{\tau}+\mathbf{F}=-\Omega^2\mathbf{u},
106 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1)
107 \f]
108 </CENTER>
109 with the non-dimensional constitutive relation,
110 <CENTER>
111 \f[
112 \pmb{\tau}=\frac{1}{1+\nu}\left(
113 \frac{\nu}{1-2\nu}(\pmb{\nabla}\cdot\mathbf{u})\textbf{I}+
114 \frac{1}{2}(\pmb{\nabla}\mathbf{u}+(\pmb{\nabla}\mathbf{u})^{{\rm T}})\right).
115 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (2)
116 \f]
117 </CENTER>
118 The non-dimensional parameter
119 <CENTER>
120 \f[
121 \Omega = {\cal L}\omega \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E}}
122 \f]
123 </CENTER>
124 is the ratio of the elastic body's intrinsic timescale,
125  \f$\displaystyle {\cal L} \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E}}\f$, to the problem-specific
126 timescale,\f$ \displaystyle {\cal T}=\frac{1}{\omega} \f$, that we
127 used to non-dimensionalise time. \f$ \Omega \f$ can be interpreted as a
128 non-dimensional version of the excitation frequency; alternatively/equivalently
129 \f$ \Omega^2 \f$ may be interpreted as a non-dimensional density.
130 The boundary conditions are set as
131 <CENTER>
132 \f[
133 \mathbf{u}=\mathbf{\hat{u}}\,\,\textrm{on $
134 \partial D_d $. }
135 \f]
136 </CENTER>

141 \section pml Perfectly matched layers
142 The idea behind perfectly matched layers is illustrated in the
143 figure below. The actual physical/mathematical problem
144 has to be solved in the infinite domain \f$ D \f$ (shown on the left),
145 with a boundary condition at a certain distance
146 from the source of the waves ensuring the suitable decay
147 of the solution at large distances from the region of interest.
148 
149 If computations are performed in a finite computational domain, \f$
150 D_c \f$ , (shown in the middle), spurious wave reflections are likely to
151 be generated at the artificial boundary \f$
152 \partial D_c \f$ of the computational domain.
153 
154 The idea behind PML methods is to surround the actual computational
155 domain \f$ D_c \f$ with a layer of "absorbing" material whose
156 properties are chosen such that the outgoing waves are absorbed
157 within it, without creating any artificial reflected waves at the interface
158 between the PML layer and the computational domain.
159 
160 
161 \image html pml.gif "Sketch illustrating the idea behind perfectly matched layers. "
162 \image latex pml.eps "Sketch illustrating the idea behind perfectly matched layers. " width=0.75\textwidth
163 
164 Our implementation of the perfectly matched layers follows the
165 development in
166 <a href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0021999106004487">
167 A. Bermudez, L. Hervella-Nieto, A. Prieto, and
168 R. Rodriguez "An optimal perfectly matched layer with unbounded
169 absorbing function for time-harmonic acoustic scattering problems"
170 Journal of Computational Physics <b>223</b> 469-488 (2007)</a>
171 and we assume the boundaries of the computational domain to be aligned
172 with the coordinate axes, as shown in the sketch above.
173 
174 The method requires a slight generalisation of the equations,
175 achieved by introducing the complex coordinate mapping
176 \f[
177 \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} \to \frac{1}{\gamma_j} \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j}
178  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (3)
179 \f]
180 within the perfectly matched layers.
181 
182 The choice of \f$ \gamma_j \f$ depends on the orientation of the
183 PML layer. Since we are restricting ourselves to axis-aligned
184 mesh boundaries we need to distinguish three different cases, as
185 shown in the sketch below:
186 
187 
188 \image html PMLDomain.gif "Sketch illustrating the geometry of the perfectly matched layers. "
189 \image latex PMLDomain.eps "Sketch illustrating the geometry of the perfectly matched layers. " width=0.75\textwidth
190 
191 We follow the work of
192 <a href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0045782502006424">
193 U. Basu and A. Chopra "Perfectly matched layers for time-harmonic
194 elastodynamics of unbounded domains: theory and finite-element
195 implementation",
196 Computer Methods in Applied Mechanics and Engineering <b>192</b>
197 1337-1375(2003)</a> and choose:
198 - For layers that are aligned with the y axis (such as the left
199  and right PML layers in the sketch, with vertical stripes) we set
200  \f[
201  \gamma_{x}(x) =
202  1 +i\frac{f_0}{a_0}\ \sigma_{x}(x)
203  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \mbox{ \ \ \ with \ \ \ }
204  \sigma_{x}(x) = \frac{1}{|X_{PML_{out}}-x|}-\frac{1}{|X_{PML_{out}}-X_{PML_{in}}|}, \ \ \ \ \
205  (4)
206  \f]
207  where \f$ a_0 = \sqrt{2.0(1.0+\nu)}\Omega\f$, \f$ f_0 \f$ is a
208  constant to be tuned, \f$ X_{PML_{in}} \f$ is the x-coordinate
209  of the inner boundary of the PML layer (the coordinate of the
210  interface with the physical domain), \f$ X_{PML_{out}} \f$ is the x-coordinate of the outer boundary
211  of the PML layer , and
212  \f[
213  \gamma_y = 1.
214  \f]
215 - For layers that are aligned with the x axis (such as the top
216  and bottom PML layers in the sketch, with horizontal stripes) we set
217  \f[
218  \gamma_x = 1,
219  \f]
220  and
221  \f[
222  \gamma_{y}(y) =
223  1+i\frac{f_0}{a_0} \ \sigma_{y}(y)
224  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \mbox{ \ \ \ with \ \ \ }
225  \sigma_{y}(y) = \frac{1}{|Y_{PML_{out}}-y|}-\frac{1}{|Y_{PML_{out}}-Y_{PML_{in}}|}, \ \ \ \ \
226  (5)
227  \f]
228  where \f$ Y_{PML} \f$ is the y-coordinate of the outer boundary
229  of the PML layer.
230 - In corner regions (diagonally striped) that are bounded by two
231  axis-aligned PML layers (with outer coordinates \f$ X_{PML} \f$ and
232  \f$ Y_{PML} \f$) we set
233  \f[
234  \gamma_{x}(x) =
235  1 +i\frac{f_0}{a_0}\ \sigma_{x}(x)
236  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \mbox{ \ \ \ with \ \ \ }
237  \sigma_{x}(x) = \frac{1}{|X_{PML_{out}}-x|}-\frac{1}{|X_{PML_{out}}-X_{PML_{in}}|} \ \ \ \ \
238  (6)
239  \f]
240  and
241  \f[
242  \gamma_{y}(y) =
243  1+i\frac{f_0}{a_0} \ \sigma_{y}(y)
244  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \mbox{ \ \ \ with \ \ \ }
245  \sigma_{y}(y) = \frac{1}{|Y_{PML_{out}}-y|}-\frac{1}{|Y_{PML_{out}}-Y_{PML_{in}}|}. \ \ \ \ \
246  (7)
247  \f]
248 - Finally, in the actual computational domain (outside
249  the PML layers) we set
250  \f[
251  \gamma_{x}(x) = \gamma_{y}(y) = 1.
252  \f]
253 .
254 
255 In our numerical experiments \f$ f_0 = 20.0 \f$ was found to be an
256 appropriate value, which gives acceptable numerical behaviour.
257 This is well aligned with the cited paper, where this parameter
258 ranges from roughly \f$ 10.0 \f$ to \f$ 50.0 \f$.

263 \subsection impl Implementation of the perfectly matched layers within oomph-lib
264 
265 The finite-element-discretised equations
266 (modified by the PML terms discussed above) are implemented in
267 the \c PMLTimeHarmonicLinearElasticityEquations<DIM> class which is templated
268 by the spatial dimension, \c DIM. As usual, we provide
269 fully functional elements by combining these with geometric
270 finite elements (from the Q and T families -- corresponding (in 2D)
271 to triangles and quad elements). By default, the PML modifications
272 are disabled, i.e. \f$ \gamma_{x}(x) \f$ and \f$ \gamma_{y}(y) \f$ are both
273 set to 1.
274 
275  The generation of suitable 2D PML meshes along the axis-aligned
276 boundaries of a given bulk mesh is facilitated by helper
277 functions which automatically erect layers of (quadrilateral)
278 PML elements. The layers are built from
279 \c QPMLTimeHarmonicLinearElasticityElement<2,NNODE_1D> elements and
280 the parameter \c NNODE_1D is automatically chosen to match that of
281 the elements in the bulk mesh. The bulk mesh can contain quads or triangles
282 (as shown in the specific example presented below).
283 
284 For instance, to erect a PML layer (of width \c width, with \c n_pml elements
285 across the width of the layer) on the "right" boundary
286 (with boundary ID \c b_bulk) of the bulk mesh pointed to by \c
287 bulk_mesh_pt, a call to
288 \code
289  TwoDimensionalPMLHelper::create_right_pml_mesh
290  <PMLLayerElement<ELASTICITY_ELEMENT> >
291  (Solid_mesh_pt, right_boundary_id,
292  Global_Parameters::n_x_right_pml,
293  Global_Parameters::width_x_right_pml);
294 \endcode
295 returns a pointer to a newly-created mesh that contains the
296 PML elements which are automatically attached to the
297 boundary of the bulk mesh (i.e. the \c Nodes on the
298 outer boundary of the bulk mesh are shared (pointed to), rather than
299 duplicated, by the
300 elements in the PML mesh). The PML-ness of the elements is
301 automatically enabled, i.e. the functions \f$ \gamma_x(x) \f$
302 and \f$ \gamma_y(y) \f$ are set as described above. Finally,
303 zero Dirichlet boundary conditions are applied to the real and
304 imaginary parts of the solution on the outer boundary of the
305 PML layer.
306 
307 Similar helper functions exist for PML layers on other axis-aligned
308 boundaries, and for corner PML meshes; see the code listings
309 provided below. Currently, we only provide this functionality for
310 convex 2D computational domains, but the generalisation to non-convex
311 boundaries and 3D is straightforward (if tedious) to implement
312 (Any volunteers?).

318 \section element_types Implementation
319 
320 Within \c oomph-lib, the non-dimensional version of equations
321 (1) with
322 the constitutive equations (2) are implemented
323 in the \c PMLTimeHarmonicLinearElasticityEquations<DIM> equations
324 class, where the template parameter \c DIM indicates the spatial dimension.
325 Following our usual approach, discussed in the
326 <A HREF="../../../doc/quick_guide/html/index.html"> (Not-So-)Quick
327 Guide,</A> this equation class is then combined with a geometric finite
328 element to form a fully-functional finite element.
329 For instance, the combination of the
330 \c PMLTimeHarmonicLinearElasticityEquations<2>
331 class with the geometric finite element \c QElement<2,3> yields a nine-node
332 quadrilateral element. As usual, the mapping
333 between local and global (Eulerian) coordinates within an element is given by,
334 <CENTER>
335 \f[
336 x_i = \sum_{j=1}^{N^{(E)}} X^{(E)}_{ij} \, \psi_j, \qquad
337 i=1,2,
338 \f]
339 </CENTER>
340 where \f$ N^{(E)} \f$ is the number of nodes in the element, \f$ X^{(E)}_{ij}
341 \f$ is the \f$ i \f$-th global (Eulerian) coordinate of
342 the \f$ j \f$-th \c Node
343 in the element, and the \f$ \psi_j \f$ are
344 the element's shape functions, defined in the geometric
345 finite element.
346 
347 All the constitutive parameters are real. The two
348 components of the displacement field have a real and imaginary part.
349 We store the four real-valued nodal unknowns in the order
350 \f$ {\rm Re}\{u_x\}, {\rm Re}\{u_y\}, {\rm Im}\{u_x\}, {\rm Im}\{u_y\}
351 \f$ and use the shape functions to interpolate the displacements as
352 <CENTER>
353 \f[
354 u_i^{(n)} = \sum_{j=1}^{N^{(E)}} U^{(E)}_{ij} \, \psi_j, \qquad
355 i=1,...4,
356 \f]
357 </CENTER>
358 where \f$ U^{(E)}_{ij} \f$ is the \f$ i \f$-th displacement component
359 (enumerated as described above) at the \f$ j \f$-th \c Node in the element.

365 \section test A specific example: outward propagation of elastic waves from the surface of a cylindrical object
366 
367 We consider the time-harmonic deformation of
368 a 2D elastic body that occupies the region outside
369 a circle whose diameter we use as the lengthscale \f$ \cal L \f$.
370  We impose a displacement \f$ {\mathbf{u}}(r,\theta)=
371  u_0 {\mathbf{e}}_r + u_1 {\mathbf{e}}_{\theta} \f$ on the inner
372  boundary (to generate a combination of pressure waves and shear waves)
373 and allow for a formulation with perfectly matched layers on the outer
374 boundary of the computational domain, which should allow for a smooth propagation of the elastic
375 waves with no interference from the restriction of the problem to a
376 finite computational domain. \f$ {\mathbf{e}}_r \f$ is the unit
377 vector in the radial direction, whereas \f$ {\mathbf{e}}_{\theta} \f$
378 is the unit vector in azimuthal direction.
379 
380 It is easy to find an analytical solution of this problem by working
381 in polar coordinates and exploiting the axisymmetry of the solution by
382 writing the displacements as
383 \f$ \displaystyle {\mathbf{u}} = U(r) {\mathbf{e}}_r \f$
384 or
385 \f$ \displaystyle {\mathbf{u}} = U(r) {\mathbf{e}}_{\theta} \f$.
386 The displacement \f$ U(r) \f$ is then governed in both cases by
387 an equation of type
388 <CENTER>
389 \f[
390 \frac{d}{d r}\left(\frac{U}{r}+\frac{dU}{dr}\right) + k^2 U=0,
391 \f]
392 </CENTER>
393 where \f$ \displaystyle k^2 = k_{r}^2=
394 \frac{\Omega^2}{\lambda+2\mu}\f$ for the radial component and
395 \f$ \displaystyle k^2 = k_{\theta}^2=
396 \frac{\Omega^2}{\mu}\f$ for the azimuthal component. We also note that
397 <CENTER>
398 \f[
399 \lambda = \frac{\nu}{(1+\nu)(1-2\nu)} \qquad \textrm{and}
400 \qquad \mu = \frac{1}{2(1+\nu)}
401 \f]
402 </CENTER>
403 are the non-dimensional Lame parameters.
404 The solution of these equations is given in each case by:
405 <CENTER>
406 \f[
407 U(r) = H_1(kr).
408 \f]
409 </CENTER>
410 where \f$ H_1 \f$ is the Hankel function of first kind.
411 The two solutions can then be converted to Cartesian coordinates and added
412 together in an appropriate manner.
413 The details of the procedure can be found in
414 the <code> GlobalParameters::exact_u() </code> function.
415 
416 We note that even though a relatively simple analytical solution
417 (in polar coordinates!) exists for this problem, it is a non-trivial
418 test case for our code which solves the governing equations in Cartesian
419 coordinates. It is also a highly relevant test case in the context of
420 testing perfectly matched layers, since the solution contains both
421 shear and compression waves.

633 \subsection comments Comments
634 - If you inspect the <A HREF="../../../demo_drivers/pml_time_harmonic_linear_elasticity/time_harmonic_elasticity_driver.cc">driver code</a>
635  you will notice that it also contains relevant code to perform
636  spatially adaptive simulations of the problem -- the adaptive
637  version of the code is selected with \c \#ifdefs.
638 
639 - The choice for the absorbing functions in our implementation of
640 the PMLs is not unique. There are alternatives varying in both order and
641 continuity properties. The current form is the result of several
642 feasibility studies and comparisons found in
643 <a href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0021999106004487">
644 Bermudez et al.</a> For Helmholtz equations these damping functions produce an
645 acceptable result in most practical situations without further
646 modifications. For very specific applications, alternatives may
647 need to be used and can easily be implemented within the
648 existing framework.

652 \subsection ex Exercises
653 \subsubsection pmlsize Changing perfectly matched layer parameters
654 Confirm that only a relatively small number of PML elements (across the thickness
655 of the PML layer) is required to effectively damp the outgoing waves.
656 It is also interesting to explore the effect of increasing the number
657 of elements of the mesh inside the perfectly matched layers.
658 
659 Another parameter
660 that can be adjusted is the geometrical thickness of the perfectly matched
661 layers. In the case of linear elasticity (as opposed to for example
662 the Helmholtz equation), the thickness of these layers must be
663 relatively large to obtain the best results. Try to explain why and take
664 note of the differences between the two mentioned problems.
665 
666 \subsubsection adaptivity Spatial adaptivity
667 The driver code discussed above already contains the straightforward
668 modifications required to enable spatial adaptivity. Explore this
669 (by recompiling the code with -DADAPTIVE) and explain why spatial
670 adaptivity is not particularly helpful for the test problem
671 discussed above.
672 
673 \subsubsection diff_order Linear and cubic finite elements
674 The driver code also contains (commented out) modifications
675 that allow the simulation to be performed with
676 three-node (linear) and ten-node (cubic) triangles. Explore the
677 performance of these elements and confirm that the helper functions
678 correctly create matching (four-node and sixteen-node) quad elements
679 in the PML layers.
680 
681 
682 \subsubsection default Default values for problem parameters
683 Following our usual convention, we provide default values
684 for problem parameters where this is sensible.
685 Some parameters, such as the elasticity tensor, do need to be set since there are no obvious
686 defaults. If \c oomph-lib is compiled in \c PARANOID mode,
687 an error is thrown if the relevant pointers haven't been set.
688 Without paranoia, you get a segmentation fault...
689 
690 Confirm that this is the case by commenting out the relevant
691 assignments.
692 
693 \subsubsection non_convex Non-convex PML boundaries
694 As discussed above, we currently provide helper functions to
695 attach PML layers to axis-aligned boundaries of 2D meshes with
696 convex outer boundaries. Essentially, this restricts us to
697 rectangular computational domains. Extend this capability by developing
698 methodologies to
699 - deal with non-convex domain boundaries. We suggest you
700  create PML meshes for the non-convex corners first, then
701  create the axis-aligned meshes (note that these have to
702  share nodes with the already-created elements that occupy the
703  non-convex corners), and then create the corner meshes
704  for the convex corners (as before). When you're done, let
705  us know -- this would be a really useful addition to
706  oomph-lib's machinery. We're happy to help!
707 - Repeat the same exercise in 3D -- somewhat less trivial
708  (so we're even keener for somebody to have a go!)
709 .
