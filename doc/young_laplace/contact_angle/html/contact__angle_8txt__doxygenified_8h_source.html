3\mainpage Example problem: The Young Laplace equation with contact angle boundary conditions
4
5
6In this document we demonstrate the adaptive solution of the Young
7Laplace equation with contact angle boundary conditions. We
8start by reviewing the physical background in the context of
9a representative model problem, and then discuss the spine-based
10representation of free contact lines and the implementation of
11the contact angle boundary condition along such lines.

34\section model A model problem
35The figure below shows a sketch of a T-junction in a microchannel
36with a rectangular cross-section. (The front wall has been removed for
37clarity). Fluid is being pushed quasi-steadily along the (vertical)
38main channel and is in the process of entering the T-junction.
39We assume that the air-liquid interface (shown in red) remains
40pinned at the two sharp edges (at \f$ y=const.\f$) where the channels meet,
41while the meniscus forms a quasi-static contact angle, \f$ \gamma \f$,
42with the smooth front and back walls.
43
44
45\image html t_junction_contact_angle_sketch.gif "A typical problem: Fluid propagates quasi-steadily through a T-junction that connects two channels of rectangular cross-section. "
46\image latex t_junction_contact_angle_sketch.eps "A typical problem: Fluid propagates quasi-steadily through a T-junction that connects two channels of rectangular cross-section. " width=0.6\textwidth
47
48
49It is of interest to determine the maximum pressure that the meniscus
50can withstand: if the driving pressure is less than that
51value, the fluid will not be able to propagate past the T-junction.

61\subsection spine Spine-based representation of the meniscus
62
63<a href="../../young_laplace/html/index.html">Recall</a>
64that we parametrised the meniscus by two intrinsic coordinates
65as \f$ {\bf R}(\zeta_1,\zeta_2) \in {R}^3 \f$, where
66\f$(\zeta_1,\zeta_2) \in D \in {R}^2\f$. Furthermore,
67we parametrised the domain boundary,
68\f$\partial D\f$, by a scalar coordinate \f$\xi\f$ so that,
69\f[
70{\partial D} = \bigg\{ (\zeta_1,\zeta_2) \ \bigg| \ (\zeta_1,\zeta_2) =
71\left( \zeta_1^{[\partial D]}(\xi), \
72\zeta_2^{[\partial D]}(\xi) \right) \bigg\}.
73\f]
74The normal to the meniscus is then given by
75\f[
76{\bf N} = \frac{{\bf R}_{,1} \times {\bf R}_{,2} }
77{|{\bf R}_{,1} \times {\bf R}_{,2}|},
78\f]
79where a comma denotes partial differentiation with respect to one of the
80intrinsic coordinates, \f$ (\zeta_1, \zeta_2). \f$
81
82Along the contact line we define two unit vectors, \f${\bf T}_t\f$
83and \f${\bf T}_n\f$, that are tangential to the meniscus.
84\f${\bf T}_t\f$ is tangent to the contact line
85while \f${\bf T}_n\f$ is normal to it and points away from the
86meniscus, as shown in the sketch below.
87
88We split the domain boundary \f$ \partial D \f$ so that \f$ \partial D =
89\partial D_{\rm pinned} \cup \partial D_{\rm angle} \f$ and
90assume that along \f$\partial D_{\rm pinned}\f$ the meniscus is
91pinned,
92\f[
93\left. {\bf R} \right|_{\partial D_{\rm pinned}} = {\bf R}_{\rm pinned}(\xi),
94\f]
95where \f${\bf R}_{\rm pinned}(\xi) \f$ is given.
96On \f$\partial D_{\rm angle}\f$ the meniscus meets the wall at a prescribed
97contact angle \f$\gamma\f$ so that
98\f[
99\left. \left( ({\bf T}_t \times {\bf N}_{\rm wall}) \cdot {\bf T}_n\right)
100\right|_{\partial D_{angle}} = \cos \gamma,
101\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1)
102\f]
103where \f${\bf N}_{\rm wall}\f$ is the outer unit normal to the wall
104as shown in this sketch:
105
106
107\image html contact_angle_sketch.gif "Sketch of the meniscus, the contact line along which it meets the wall, and the spine-based representation of the meniscus. "
108\image latex contact_angle_sketch.eps "Sketch of the meniscus, the contact line along which it meets the wall, and the spine-based representation of the meniscus. " width=0.6\textwidth
109
110The figure also illustrates the spine-based representation of the meniscus
111in the form
112\f[
113{\bf R}(\zeta_1,\zeta_2) =
114{\bf B}(\zeta_1,\zeta_2) +
115u(\zeta_1,\zeta_2) \ {\bf S}(\zeta_1,\zeta_2)
116\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (2)
117\f]
118where the spine basis \f${\bf R}(\zeta_1,\zeta_2)\f$ and
119spines \f${\bf S}(\zeta_1,\zeta_2)\f$ are pre-determined
120vector fields, chosen such that
121\n
122- The mapping from \f$(\zeta_1,\zeta_2)\f$ to \f${\bf R}(\zeta_1,\zeta_2)\f$
123 is one-to-one, at least for the meniscus shapes of interest.
124 \n\n
125- Along the parts of the boundary where the contact line is
126 pinned we have
127 \f[
128 \left. {\bf B}\right|_{\partial D_{\rm pinned}} = {\bf R}_{\rm pinned}
129 \f]
130 so that the pinned boundary condition may be enforced by
131 setting \f$ u|_{\partial D_{\rm pinned}} = 0.\f$
132 \n\n
133.
134\subsection contact_angle_term Computation of the contact-angle term in the variational principle
135
136<a href="../../young_laplace/html/index.html">Recall</a>
137that the variational principle that determines the shape of the
138meniscus contained the line term
139\f[
140\delta \Pi_{\rm contact \ line} =
141\oint_{\partial D } {\bf T}_n \cdot \delta {\bf R}
142\left| \frac{\partial {\bf R}} {\partial \xi}\right| \ d\xi.
143\f]
144Along \f$ \partial D_{pinned} \f$ the line integral vanishes
145because \f$ \delta {\bf R}\big|_{ \partial D_{pinned} } ={\bf 0} \f$.
146The line integral can therefore be written as
147\f[
148\delta \Pi_{\rm contact \ line} =
149\int_{\partial D_{\rm angle}} {\bf T}_n \cdot \delta {\bf R}
150\left| \frac{\partial {\bf R}}{\partial \xi}\right| \ d\xi,
151\f]
152or, using the spine-based representation of the meniscus, (2),
153\f[
154\delta \Pi_{\rm contact\ line} =
155\int_{\partial D_{\rm angle}} {\bf T}_n \cdot {\bf S} \ \delta u
156\left| \frac{\partial {\bf R}}{\partial \xi}\right| \ d\xi.
157\f]
158
159We shall now demonstrate that the integrand in this expression can be expressed
160in terms of the contact angle boundary
161condition (1). We start with several observations:
162-# \f${\bf T}_t\f$ is tangential to the wall.
163 \n\n
164-# Since \f${\bf N}_{\rm wall}\f$ is normal to the wall,
165 \f${\bf T}_t \times {\bf N}_{\rm wall}\f$ is tangential to the wall
166 and orthogonal to \f${\bf T}_t\f$.
167 \n\n
168-# \f${\bf S}\f$ is tangential to the wall and can therefore
169 be decomposed into its components parallel to \f$ {\bf T}_t \f$
170 and \f${\bf T}_t \times {\bf N}_{\rm wall} \f$ as
171 \f[
172 {\bf S} = \alpha {\bf T}_t + \beta ( {\bf T}_t \times {\bf N}_{\rm
173 wall} )
174 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (3)
175 \f]
176 for some values of \f$\alpha\f$ and \f$\beta\f$. In fact,
177 \f[
178 \beta = {\bf S}
179 \cdot ( {\bf T}_t \times {\bf N}_{\rm wall} )
180 \f]
181-# During the computation it is most convenient to perform all
182 calculations in terms of quantities that are easily obtained from
183 the parametrisation of the meniscus as this avoids having to
184 specify \f${\bf N}_{\rm wall}\f$ explicitly. For this purpose we
185 exploit that \f${\bf T}_t\f$ and \f${\bf S}\f$ are tangential
186 to the wall and not parallel to each other (unless the
187 parametrisation of the meniscus by (2) is no longer
188 one-to-one). Therefore \f${\bf N}_{\rm wall}\f$ can be obtained
189 from quantities that are intrinsic to the meniscus representation
190 via
191 \n\n
192 \f[
193 {\bf N}_{\rm wall} = \frac{ {\bf S} \times {\bf T}_t }
194 {|{\bf S} \times {\bf T}_t|}
195 \f]
196 and thus
197 \f[
198 \beta = {\bf S}
199 \cdot \left( {\bf T}_t \times
200 \frac{ {\bf S} \times {\bf T}_t }
201 {|{\bf S} \times {\bf T}_t|} \right)
202 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (4)
203 \f]
204-# Given (3) and the fact that \f${\bf T}_t \cdot {\bf
205 T}_n =0\f$, we have
206 \n\n
207 \f[
208 {\bf S} \cdot {\bf T}_n = \beta ( {\bf T}_t \times {\bf N}_{\rm wall}
209 ) \cdot {\bf T}_n
210 \f]
211 and with (1):
212 \f[
213 {\bf S} \cdot {\bf T}_n = \beta \cos \gamma.
214 \f]
215 Hence, the line integral may be written as
216 \f[
217 \delta \Pi_{\rm contact\ line} =
218 \int_{\partial D_{\rm angle}} \beta \cos \gamma \ \delta u
219 \left| \frac{\partial {\bf R}}{\partial \xi}\right| \ d\xi,
220 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (5)
221 \f]
222 where \f$ \beta \f$ is given by (4).
223.
224Equation (5) is easily discretised
225by finite elements. Within \c oomph-lib, the line integral
226is decomposed into \c FaceElements that are attached to the
227"bulk" Young-Laplace elements that are adjacent to the
228contact line. The imposition of the contact angle boundary condition
229for the Young Laplace equation is therefore as easy as the application
230of Neumann boundary conditions for a Poisson equation, say.

268\section code The driver code
269The modifications to the driver code required to impose the contact
270angle boundary conditions are very similar to those used in other driver
271codes for problems with Neumann-type boundary conditions. We attach
272\c FaceElements to the appropriate faces of the "bulk" Young-Laplace
273elements detach/re-attach them before and after any spatial
274adaptation of the "bulk" mesh.

461\section comm_ex Comments and Exercises
462
463\subsection ic How to generate a good initial guess for the solution
464
465We already commented on the need to provide a "good" initial
466guess for the solution in order to ensure the convergence of the Newton
467iteration. In the <a href="../../young_laplace/html/index.html">
468previous example</a> this was easy because the flat meniscus
469(clearly a solution of the Young-Laplace equations for
470zero curvature) also satisfied the boundary conditions. In the
471present example, and in many others, this is not the case. In such
472problems it may be difficult to generate initial guesses for the meniscus
473shape that are sufficiently close to actual solution.
474
475In such cases it may be necessary to compute the initial solution
476to the problem whose behaviour we wish to investigate during the
477actual parameter study via a preliminary auxiliary continuation procedure that
478transforms an easier-solve-problem (for which a good initial guess
479can be found) into the actual problem.
480
481
482Explore this approach in the present problem by implementing the
483following steps:
484-# Set the contact angle to \f$ 90^o \f$ and solve the problem, using
485 the "flat" meniscus as the initial guess. The "flat" meniscus
486 is, of course, the exact solution for zero control displacement and/or zero
487 curvature.
488 \n\n
489-# Now start a preliminary continuation procedure in which the contact angle
490 is adjusted in small steps until it reaches the desired value. Keep
491 the prescribed control displacement (or the meniscus curvature)
492 constant during this procedure.
493 \n\n
494-# The solution for the desired contact angle may now be used
495 as the initial guess for the actual parameter study in which
496 the control displacement (or the meniscus curvature) are increased
497 while the contact angle is kept fixed.
498.

502\subsection lim Limitations of the current approach -- suggestions for improvement
503
504One of the main disadvantages of the approach adopted here
505is that the spine vector fields \f$ {\bf B} \f$
506and \f$ {\bf S} \f$ must be specified <em>a priori</em>. For sufficiently
507complicated meniscus shapes (or for menisci that undergo
508large changes in shape as their curvature is varied) the choice
509of suitable spines may be very difficult.
510\n\n
511One (possible) solution to this problem could be (we haven't tried
512it!) to occasionally update the spine representation. For instance,
513assume that we have computed a meniscus shape in the form
514\n\n
515\f[
516\widehat{\bf R} = {\bf R}(\zeta_1,\zeta_2) =
517{\bf B}(\zeta_1,\zeta_2) +
518u(\zeta_1,\zeta_2) \ {\bf S}(\zeta_1,\zeta_2)
519\f]
520with an associated normal vector \f$ \widehat{\bf N} \f$.
521We can reparametrise this shape by setting
522\f[
523{\bf B} := \widehat{\bf R},
524\f]
525\f[
526{\bf S} := \widehat{\bf N},
527\f]
528and
529\f[
530u := 0
531\f]
532before continuing the computation. Provided this is done
533sufficiently frequently, i.e. long before
534the displacement along the spines has become so large
535that the mapping from \f$ (\zeta_1,\zeta_2) \f$ to
536\f$ {\bf R}(\zeta_1,\zeta_2)\f$ is about to become non-one-to-one,
537this should allow the computation of arbitrarily large meniscus
538deflections. Try it out and let us know how it works!
539\n\n
540
541\subsection zero Zero contact angles
542
543Our problem formulation suffers from an additional, more
544fundamental problem: it cannot be used to solve problems
545with zero contact angle. This is because for zero contact angles
546the equilibrium solution is no longer a minimiser of the
547variational principle: given a solution at which the
548meniscus meets the wall at zero contact angle, it is always
549possible to extend the meniscus with an arbitrary-length
550"collar" along the wall without changing the overall energy
551of the system. As a result, the position of the contact line becomes
552increasingly ill-defined as the contact angle \f$ \gamma \f$ is reduced,
553causing the Newton method to converge very slowly (and ultimately
554not at all) as \f$ \gamma \to 0. \f$
555
