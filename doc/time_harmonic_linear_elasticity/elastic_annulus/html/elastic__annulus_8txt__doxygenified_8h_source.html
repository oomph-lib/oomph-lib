3 \mainpage The equations of time-harmonic linear elasticity
4 
5 The aim of this tutorial is to demonstrate the solution of the
6 time-harmonic equations of linear elasticity in cartesian
7 coordinates. These equations are useful to describe forced, time-harmonic
8 oscillations of elastic bodies.

30 \section theory Theory
31 Consider a linearly elastic body (of density \f$ \rho \f$, Young's
32 modulus \f$ E \f$ and Poisson's ratio \f$ \nu \f$), occupying
33 the region \f$ D \f$
34 whose boundary is \f$ \partial D \f$. Assuming that the body performs
35 time-harmonic oscillations of frequency of \f$ \omega \f$
36 its motion is governed by the equations of time-harmonic
37 linear elasticity
38 <CENTER>
39 \f[
40 \pmb{\nabla}^*\cdot\pmb{\tau}^*+
41 \rho \mathbf{F}^*=-\rho\omega^2 \mathbf{u}^*,
42 \f]
43 </CENTER>
44 where the \f$ x_{i}^* \f$ are the cartesian coordinates, and
45 the time-periodic stresses, body force and displacements are given by
46 \f$ {\rm Re}\{\pmb{\tau^*}(x_{i}^*){\rm e}^{-{\rm i}\omega
47 t^*}\} \f$,
48 \f$ {\rm Re}\{\mathbf{F}^*(x_{i}^*){\rm e}^{-{\rm i}\omega
49 t^*}\} \f$ and \f$ {\rm Re}\{\mathbf{u}^*(x_{i}^*){\rm e}^{-{\rm i}\omega
50 t^*}\} \f$ respectively. Note that, as usual, the superscript
51 asterisk notation is used to distinguish dimensional quantities
52 from their non-dimensional counterparts where required.
53 
54 The body is subject to imposed time-harmonic displacements
55 \f$ {\rm Re}\{\mathbf{\hat{u}}^* {\rm e}^{-{\rm i}\omega
56 t^*}\} \f$
57 along \f$ \partial D_d \f$, and to an imposed time-harmonic traction
58 \f$ {\rm Re}\{ \pmb{\hat{\tau}}^* {\rm e}^{-{\rm i}\omega
59 t^*}\} \f$ along \f$ \partial D_n \f$
60 where \f$ \partial D=\partial D_d\cup\partial D_n \f$. This requires that
61 <CENTER>
62 \f[
63 \mathbf{u}^*=\mathbf{\hat{u}}^*\,\,\textrm{on $
64 \partial D_d $ },\quad
65 \pmb{\tau}^* \cdot
66 {\mathbf{n}}=\pmb{\hat{\tau}}^*\,\,\textrm{on $
67 \partial D_n $ }
68 \f]
69 </CENTER>
70 where \f$ {\bf n} \f$ is the outer unit normal on the boundary.
71 
72 The stresses and displacements are related by the constitutive equations
73 <CENTER>
74 \f[
75 \pmb{\tau}^*=\frac{E}{1+\nu}\left(
76 \frac{\nu}{1-2\nu}(\pmb{\nabla}^*\cdot\mathbf{u}^*)\textbf{I}+
77 \frac{1}{2}(\pmb{\nabla}^*\mathbf{u}^*+\pmb{\nabla}^*\mathbf{u}^{*{\rm T}})\right),
78 \f]
79 </CENTER>
80 where \f$ \pmb{\nabla}^*\mathbf{u}^{*{\rm T}}
81 \f$ represents the transpose of \f$ \pmb{\nabla}^*\mathbf{u}^*
82 \f$.
83 
84 We non-dimensionalise the equations, using a problem specific reference
85 length, \f$ {\cal L} \f$, and a timescale \f$ \displaystyle {\cal T}=\frac{1}{\omega} \f$, and use
86 Young's modulus to non-dimensionalise the body force and the stress tensor:
87 <CENTER>
88 \f[
89 \pmb{\tau}^* = E \, \pmb{\tau}, \qquad
90 x_{i}^* = {\cal L}\, x_{i}
91 \f]
92 \f[
93 \mathbf{u}^* = {\cal L}\, \mathbf{u}, \qquad
94 \mathbf{F}^* = \frac{E}{\rho \cal L} \, \mathbf{F},
95 \qquad
96 t^* = {\cal T}\, t.
97 \f]
98 </CENTER>
99 
100 The non-dimensional form of the equations is then given by
101 <CENTER>
102 \f[
103 \pmb{\nabla}\cdot\pmb{\tau}+\mathbf{F}=-\Omega^2\mathbf{u},
104 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1)
105 \f]
106 </CENTER>
107 with the non-dimensional constitutive relation,
108 <CENTER>
109 \f[
110 \pmb{\tau}=\frac{1}{1+\nu}\left(
111 \frac{\nu}{1-2\nu}(\pmb{\nabla}\cdot\mathbf{u})\textbf{I}+
112 \frac{1}{2}(\pmb{\nabla}\mathbf{u}+\pmb{\nabla}\mathbf{u}^{{\rm T}})\right).
113 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (2)
114 \f]
115 </CENTER>
116 The non-dimensional parameter
117 <CENTER>
118 \f[
119 \Omega = {\cal L}\omega \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E}}
120 \f]
121 </CENTER>
122 is the ratio of the elastic body's intrinsic timescale,
123  \f$\displaystyle {\cal L} \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E}}\f$, to the problem-specific
124 timescale,\f$ \displaystyle {\cal T}=\frac{1}{\omega} \f$, that we
125 used to non-dimensionalise time. \f$ \Omega \f$ can be interpreted as a
126 non-dimensional version of the excitation frequency; alternatively/equivalently
127 \f$ \Omega^2 \f$ may be interpreted as a non-dimensional density.
128 The boundary conditions are
129 <CENTER>
130 \f[
131 \mathbf{u}=\mathbf{\hat{u}}\,\,\textrm{on $\partial D_d $ },\quad
132 \pmb{\tau} \cdot
133 {\mathbf{n}}=\pmb{\hat{\tau}}\,\,\textrm{on $\partial D_n $ }.
134 \f]
135 </CENTER>

144 \section element_types Implementation
145 
146 Within \c oomph-lib, the non-dimensional version of equations
147 (1) with
148 the constitutive equations (2) are implemented
149 in the \c TimeHarmonicLinearElasticityEquations<DIM> equations
150 class, where the template parameter \c DIM indicates the spatial dimension.
151 Following our usual approach, discussed in the
152 <A HREF="../../../quick_guide/html/index.html"> (Not-So-)Quick
153 Guide,</A> this equation class is then combined with a geometric finite
154 element to form a fully-functional finite element.
155 For instance, the combination of the
156 \c TimeHarmonicLinearElasticityEquations<2>
157 class with the geometric finite element \c QElement<2,3> yields a nine-node
158 quadrilateral element. As usual, the mapping
159 between local and global (Eulerian) coordinates within an element is given by,
160 <CENTER>
161 \f[
162 x_i = \sum_{j=1}^{N^{(E)}} X^{(E)}_{ij} \, \psi_j, \qquad
163 i=1,2,
164 \f]
165 </CENTER>
166 where \f$ N^{(E)} \f$ is the number of nodes in the element, \f$ X^{(E)}_{ij}
167 \f$ is the \f$ i \f$-th global (Eulerian) coordinate of
168 the \f$ j \f$-th \c Node
169 in the element, and the \f$ \psi_j \f$ are
170 the element's shape functions, defined in the geometric
171 finite element.
172 
173 All the constitutive parameters are real. The two
174 components of the displacement field have a real and imaginary part.
175 We store the four real-valued nodal unknowns in the order
176 \f$ {\rm Re}\{u_x\}, {\rm Re}\{u_y\}, {\rm Im}\{u_x\}, {\rm Im}\{u_y\}
177 \f$ and use the shape functions to interpolate the displacements as
178 <CENTER>
179 \f[
180 u_i^{(n)} = \sum_{j=1}^{N^{(E)}} U^{(E)}_{ij} \, \psi_j, \qquad
181 i=1,...4,
182 \f]
183 </CENTER>
184 where \f$ U^{(E)}_{ij} \f$ is the \f$ i \f$-th displacement component
185 (enumerated as described above) at the \f$ j \f$-th \c Node in the element.

191 \section test A test problem: Oscillations of an elastic annulus
192 
193 We consider the time-harmonic axisymmetric deformation of
194 a 2D annular elastic body that occupies the region \f$ r_{\rm min}\leq
195 r\leq r_{\rm max}, 0\leq\theta\leq 2\pi \f$, shown in the left half
196 of the sketch below. We impose a constant-amplitude
197 axisymmetric displacement \f$ {\mathbf{u}}(r_{\rm min},\theta)=
198 u_0 (1-{\rm i}) {\mathbf{e}}_r\f$ on the inner boundary
199 and a constant-amplitude pressure load
200 \f$ \pmb{\hat{\tau}} = -P_0 {\mathbf{e}}_r \f$ on the outer boundary.
201 (\f$ {\mathbf{e}}_r \f$ is the unit vector in the radial
202 direction).
203 
204 
205 \image html sketch.gif "Sketch of the test problems. "
206 \image latex sketch.eps "Sketch of the test problems. " width=0.8\textwidth
207 
208 
209 It is easy to find an analytical solution of this problem by working
210 in polar coordinates and exploiting the axisymmetry of the solution by
211 writing the displacement as
212 \f$ \displaystyle {\mathbf{u}} = U(r) {\mathbf{e}}_r \f$.
213 The radial displacement \f$ U(r) \f$ is then governed by
214 <CENTER>
215 \f[
216 \frac{d}{d r}\left(\frac{U}{r}+\frac{dU}{dr}\right) + k^2 U=0,
217 \f]
218 </CENTER>
219 where \f$ \displaystyle k^2= \frac{\Omega^2}{\lambda+2\mu}\f$ and
220 <CENTER>
221 \f[
222 \lambda = \frac{\nu}{(1+\nu)(1-2\nu)} \qquad \textrm{and}
223 \qquad \mu = \frac{1}{2(1+\nu)}
224 \f]
225 </CENTER>
226 are the non-dimensional Lame parameters.
227 The solution of this equation is given by:
228 <CENTER>
229 \f[
230 U(r) = aJ_1(kr)+bY_1(kr).
231 \f]
232 </CENTER>
233 where \f$ J_1 \f$ and \f$ Y_1 \f$ are Bessel functions
234 of the first and second kind, respectively.
235 The amplitudes \f$ a \f$ and \f$ b \f$ can be found using the
236 boundary conditions on \f$ r_{\rm min} \f$ and \f$ r_{\rm max} \f$.
237 In the driver code discussed below, the (lengthy) expressions
238 for \f$ a \f$ and \f$ b \f$ in terms
239 of the problem parameters can be found in
240 the <code> GlobalParameters::exact_u() </code> function.
241 
242 We note that even though a relatively simple analytical solution
243 (in polar coordinates!) exists for this problem, it is a non-trivial
244 test case for our code which solves the governing equations in cartesian
245 coordinates. However, to show that we can also compute non-trivial
246 solutions, we also consider the case where the annular region
247 has a "gap" and therefore occupies only a fraction (90%) of the circumference.
248 This creates two additional boundaries (the radial lines bounding
249 the "gap" and we subject these to the same pressure
250 that acts on the outer boundary, as shown in the right half of the
251 sketch above.

452 \subsection comments Comments
453 
454 - We did not discuss the mesh generation in detail here:
455  The mesh is created by straightforward overloading
456  of \c oomph-lib's existing rectangular quad mesh -- the constructor
457  simply adjusts the nodal positions (exactly as suggested in the
458  <A HREF="../../../quick_guide/html/index.html#distorted_mesh">
459  "Not-so-quick" guide</a>.) A little bit of extra work is required
460  to enforce periodicity on the mesh for the case without a gap
461  in the annular region because two of the boundaries in
462  the original mesh then overlap. How this is dealt with is discussed in
463  <A HREF="../../../navier_stokes/rayleigh_channel/html/index.html#periodic">
464  another tutorial.</a>
465  \n\n
466 - If you inspect the <A HREF="../../../../demo_drivers/time_harmonic_linear_elasticity/elastic_annulus/time_harmonic_elastic_annulus.cc">driver code</a>
467  you will notice that it also contains relevant code to perform
468  spatially adaptive simulations of the problem -- the adaptive
469  version of the code is selected with \c \#ifdefs. Dealing with the
470  periodic boundary conditions for spatially adaptive meshes
471  requires a few additional steps, but they are also discussed
472  <A HREF="../../../linear_elasticity/refineable_periodic_load/html/index.html#constructor">elsewhere,</a>
473  so we won't discuss them here.

477 \subsection exercises Exercises
478 - Change the parameter study performed by the driver code such that
479  the loop varies the frequency parameter \f$ \Omega^2 \f$. Assess the effect
480  of an increase in \f$ \Omega^2 \f$ on the accuracy of the
