30 \section equation The governing equations
31 
32 The figure below shows a sketch of a general elasticity
33 problem. A linearly elastic solid body occupies the domain
34 \f$ V \f$ and is loaded by a body
35 force \f$ {\bf F}^* \f$ and by a surface traction
36 \f$ {\bf t}^* \f$ which is applied along part of its boundary,
37 \f$ A_{\rm tract} \f$. The displacement is prescribed along the
38 remainder of the boundary, \f$ A_{\rm displ} \f$, where
39 \f$ \partial V = A_{\rm tract} \cup A_{\rm displ} \f$.
40 
41 \image html Solid_boundary_conds.gif "Sketch of a general elasticity problem: A linearly elastic body is loaded by a body force and is exposed to a prescribed traction along part of its boundary while the displacement is prescribed along the remainder of the boundary. "
42 \image latex Solid_boundary_conds.eps "Sketch of a general elasticity problem: A linearly elastic body is loaded by a body force and is exposed to a prescribed traction along part of its boundary while the displacement is prescribed along the remainder of the boundary. " width=0.5\textwidth
43 
44 We adopt an Eulerian approach and describe the
45 deformation in terms of the displacement field
46 \f$ {\bf u}^*\left( x_i^*,t^* \right) \f$ where
47 \f$ x_i^* \f$ and \f$ t^* \f$ are the spatial coordinates and time,
48 respectively. Throughout this document we will use index notation
49 and the summation convention, and use asterisks to distinguish
50 dimensional quantities from their non-dimensional counterparts.
51 Denoting the density of the body by \f$ \rho \f$, the deformation
52 is governed by the Cauchy equations,
53 <CENTER>
54 \f[
55 \frac{\partial \tau_{ij}^*}{\partial x_j^*} + \rho F_i^*
56 =
57 \rho \frac{\partial^2 u_i^*}{\partial t^{2*}},
58 \f]
59 </CENTER>
60 where \f$ \tau_{ij}^* \f$ is the Cauchy stress tensor which, for a
61 linearly elastic solid, is given by
62 <CENTER>
63 \f[
64 \tau_{ij}^* = E_{ijkl}^* \ e_{kl},
65 \f]
66 </CENTER>
67 where \f$ e_{kl} \f$ is the strain tensor,
68 <CENTER>
69 \f[
70 e_{ij} = \frac{1}{2}
71 \left( \frac{\partial u_i^*}{\partial x_j^*}+
72  \frac{\partial u_j^*}{\partial x_i^*}\right).
73 \f]
74 </CENTER>
75 \f$ E_{ijkl} \f$ is the 4th order elasticity tensor, which for
76 a homogeneous and isotropic solid is
77 <CENTER>
78 \f[
79 E_{ijkl}^* = \frac{E}{1+\nu} \left(\frac{\nu}{1-2\nu} \delta_{ij}
80  \delta_{kl} + \delta_{ik} \delta_{jl} \right),
81 \f]
82 </CENTER>
83 where \f$ E \f$ is Young's modulus, \f$ \nu \f$ is the
84 Poisson ratio and \f$ \delta_{ij} \f$ is the Kronecker delta.
85 Thus the Cauchy stress is given in terms of the displacement
86 derivatives
87 by
88 <CENTER>
89 \f[
90 \tau_{ij}^* = \frac{E}{1+\nu} \left(\frac{\nu}{1-2\nu} \
91 \delta_{ij} \ \frac{\partial u_k^*}{\partial x_k^*} +
92 \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\partial u_i^*}{\partial x_j^*}+
93 \frac{\partial u_j^*}{\partial x_i^*} \right) \right).
94 \f]
95 </CENTER>
96 
97 
98 We non-dimensionalise the equations, using a problem specific reference
99 length, \f$ {\cal L} \f$, and a timescale \f$ {\cal T} \f$, and use
100 Young's modulus to non-dimensionalise the body force and the stress,
101 <CENTER>
102 \f[
103 \tau_{ij}^* = E \, \tau_{ij}, \qquad
104 x_i^* = {\cal L}\, x_i, \qquad
105 u_i^* = {\cal L}\, u_i,
106 \f]
107 \f[
108 t^* = {\cal T}\, t, \qquad
109 F_i^* = \frac{E}{\rho \cal L} \, F_i. \qquad
110 \f]
111 </CENTER>
112 
113 The non-dimensional form of the Cauchy equations is then given by
114 <CENTER>
115 \f[
116 \frac{\partial \tau_{ij}}{\partial x_j} + F_i
117 =
118 \Lambda^2 \frac{\partial^2 u_i}{\partial t^2},
119 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1)
120 \f]
121 </CENTER>
122 where
123 <CENTER>
124 \f[
125 \tau_{ij} = \frac{1}{1+\nu} \left(\frac{\nu}{1-2\nu} \
126 \delta_{ij} \ \frac{\partial u_k}{\partial x_k} +
127 \frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j}+
128 \frac{\partial u_j}{\partial x_i} \right) \right).
129 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (2)
130 \f]
131 </CENTER>
132 The parameter
133 <CENTER>
134 \f[
135 \Lambda = \frac{{\cal L} \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E}} }{\cal T},
136 \f]
137 </CENTER>
138 is the ratio of the elastic body's intrinsic timescale,
139  \f$ {\cal L} \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E}} \f$, to the problem-specific
140 timescale, \f$ {\cal T} \f$, that we used to non-dimensionalise time.
141 
142 The displacement constraints provide
143 a Dirichlet condition for the displacements,
144 \f[
145 u_i = u_i^{[\rm prescribed]} \ \ \ \ \ \ \mbox{on $A_{\mathrm{displ}}$},
146 
147 \f]
148 while the traction boundary conditions require that
149 \f[
150 t_i = \tau_{ij} n_j \ \ \ \ \ \ \mbox{on $A_{\mathrm{tract}},$}
151 \f]
152 where the \f$ n_j \f$ are the components of the outer unit normal
153 to the boundary.
154 
155 In this tutorial we only consider steady problems for which
156 the equations reduce to
157 <CENTER>
158 \f[
159 \frac{\partial \tau_{ij}}{\partial x_j} + F_i = 0.
160 \f]
161 </CENTER>

171 \subsection el The elements
172 Within \c oomph-lib, the non-dimensional version of the
173 <CODE>DIM</CODE>-dimensional Cauchy equations (1) with
174 the constitutive equations (2) are implemented
175 in the \c LinearElasticityEquations<DIM> equations class. Following
176 our usual approach, discussed in the
177 <A HREF="../../../quick_guide/html/index.html"> (Not-So-)Quick
178 Guide,</A> this equation class is then combined with a geometric finite
179 element to form a fully-functional finite element.
180 For instance, the combination of the \c LinearElasticityEquations<2>
181 class with the geometric finite element \c QElement<2,3> yields a nine-node
182 quadrilateral linear elasticity element. As usual, the mapping
183 between local and global (Eulerian) coordinates within an element is given by,
184 <CENTER>
185 \f[
186 x_i = \sum_{j=1}^{N^{(E)}} X^{(E)}_{ij} \, \psi_j, \qquad
187 i=1,2\quad [\mbox{and }3],
188 \f]
189 </CENTER>
190 where \f$ N^{(E)} \f$ is the number of nodes in the element,
191 \f$ X^{(E)}_{ij} \f$ is the \f$ i \f$-th global (Eulerian) coordinate
192 of the \f$ j \f$-th \c Node in the element, and the \f$ \psi_j \f$ are
193 the element's shape functions, defined in the geometric
194 finite element.
195 
196 The cartesian displacement components \f$ u_1, \f$ \f$ u_2, \f$
197 [and \f$ u_3 \f$] are stored as nodal values, and the
198 shape functions are used to interpolate the displacements as
199 <CENTER>
200 \f[
201 u_i = \sum_{j=1}^{N^{(E)}} U^{(E)}_{ij} \, \psi_j, \qquad
202 i=1,2\quad [\mbox{and }3],
203 \f]
204 </CENTER>
205 where \f$ U^{(E)}_{ij} \f$ is the \f$ i \f$-th displacement component
206 at the \f$ j \f$-th \c Node in the element. Nodal values of the displacement
207 components are accessible via the access function
208 
209 \code
210 LinearElasticityEquations<DIM>::u(i,j)
211 \endcode
212 
213 which returns the \f$ i \f$-th displacement component stored at the element's
214 \f$ j \f$-th \c Node.

222 To illustrate the solution of the steady equations of linear
223 elasticity, we consider the 2D problem shown in the sketch below.

235 \f[
236 \frac{\partial \tau_{ij}}{\partial x_j} + F_i=0,
237  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (3)
238 \f]
239 in the domain \f$ D = \{x_1 \in [-\infty,+\infty], x_2 \in [0,L_y]\} \f$,

246 \f[
247 \left. \mathbf{t}\right|_{x_2 = L_y}=\left(-A \cos{\left(\frac{2 \pi x_1}{L_x}\right)}, -A \sin{\left(\frac{2 \pi x_1}{L_x}\right)}\right),
248 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (5)
249 \f]
250 on the top boundary, and symmetry conditions at \f$ x_1 = 0 \f$ and \f$ x_1 = L_x \f$,
251 \f[
252 \left. {\bf u}\right|_{x_1=0} = \left.{\bf u}\right|_{x_1=L_x}.

398 \section comments Comments and Exercises
399 
400 \subsection nondim Comments
401 
402 As discussed in the introduction, the non-dimensional version of the
403 steady Cauchy equations only contains a single non-dimensional
404 parameter, the Poisson ratio \f$ \nu \f$ which is passed to the
405 constructor of the \c IsotropicElasticityTensor. If you inspect
406 the relevant source code
407 <a href="../../../../src/linear_elasticity/elasticity_tensor.h">
408 src/linear_elasticity/elasticity_tensor.h</a> you will find that this
409 constructor has a second
410 argument which defaults to one. This argument plays the role of
411 Young's modulus and is best interpreted as the ratio of the material's
412 actual Young's modulus to the (nominal) Young's modulus used
413 in the non-dimensionalisation of the equations. The ability to provide
414 this ratio is important if different regions of the body contain
415 materials with different material properties.
416 
417 \subsection exercises Exercises
418 
419 -# Fix the size of the domain and set the displacements along the
420  bottom boundary to the exact solution for the infinite depth case,
421  i.e. \f$ {\bf u} = {\bf u}^{[\mathrm{exact}]} \f$, using the \c
422  Global_Parameters::Finite flag. Then investigate how the solution
423  converges to the exact solution for increasing numbers of elements.
424 -# Try varying the depth of the domain by changing
425  \c Global_Parameters::Ly while maintaining a constant spatial
426  resolution (i.e. increasing the number of elements -- this is
427  already done in the driver code where we compute \c ny in terms of \c
428  Global_Parameters::Ly ) and compare how the solution converges to the
429  exact solution of the infinite depth case.
