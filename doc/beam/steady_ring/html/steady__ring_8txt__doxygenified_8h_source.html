3 \mainpage Demo problem: Large-displacement post-buckling of a pressure-loaded thin-walled elastic ring -- displacement-control techniques
4 
5 In this example we study a more challenging beam problem: The
6 non-axisymmetric buckling of a thin-walled elastic
7 ring, loaded by a spatially-constant external pressure, \f$ p_{ext}\f$ .
8 For sufficiently small positive (or arbitrarily large negative)
9 values of \f$ p_{ext}\f$ the ring deforms axisymmetrically and in
10 this mode it is very stiff, implying that large changes in pressure
11 are required to change its radius. However, if \f$ p_{ext}\f$ exceeds
12 a critical threshold, the axisymmetric configuration becomes unstable,
13 causing the ring to buckle non-axisymmetrically. Once buckled,
14 the ring is much more flexible and small changes in \f$
15 p_{ext}\f$ are sufficient to induce dramatic changes in its shape.
16 
17 The rapid change in stiffness following the buckling makes it
18 difficult to compute strongly buckled solutions by continuation
19 methods as the solution computed for a certain value of \f$ p_{ext}\f$
20 may represent a poor approximation of the solution at a
21 slightly larger pressure. In extreme cases, this can
22 cause the Newton method (whose convergence relies on the provision of
23 good initial guesses for the unknowns) to diverge.
24 
25 We will demonstrate the use of so-called "displacement control" techniques
26 to overcome these problems. Displacement-control techniques are
27 useful in problems in which some \e a-priori knowledge about the
28 expected displacement field allows us to re-formulate the
29 problem. Rather than prescribing the load on the elastic solid
30 and computing the displacement field, we prescribe the displacement
31 of a carefully-selected material "control" point and regard the
32 load required to achieve this displacement as an unknown.

40 <B>Non-axisymmetric buckling of a pressure-loaded, thin-walled elastic ring</B>
41 </CENTER>
42 
43 We wish to compute the deformation of a linearly-elastic, circular
44 ring of undeformed radius \f$ R_0 \f$ and wall thickness \f$ h^* \f$,
45 subject to a spatially-constant external pressure \f$ p_{ext}^* \f$.
46 We choose the undeformed radius \f$ R_0 \f$ as the lengthscale
47 for the non-dimensionalisation of the problem and assume that
48 \f$ h = h^*/R_0 \ll 1 \f$ , justifying the use of beam theory.
49 As discussed in <A HREF="../../tensioned_string/html/index.html">
50 the previous example</A> we scale the pressure on the ring's
51 effective Young's modulus, \f$ p_{ext} = p_{ext}^*/E_{eff} \f$ ,
52 where \f$ E_{eff} = E/(1-\nu^2) \f$ . We use the non-dimensional
53 arclength \f$ \xi \in [0,2\pi] \f$ along the ring's undeformed
54 centreline as the Lagrangian coordinate and parametrise the position vector
55 to the ring's undeformed centreline as
56 \f[
57 {\bf r}_w(\xi) = \left(
58 \begin{array}{c}
59 \cos(\xi) \\
60 \sin(\xi)
61 \end{array}
62 \right).
63 \f]
64 We wish to compute the position vector \f$ {\bf R}_w(\xi) \f$
65 to the deformed ring's centreline.

83 \section theory The expected deformation and the displacement-control formulation of the problem
84 Standard linear stability analysis [see,
85 e.g., G.J. Simitses "An introduction to the elastic stability of
86 structures", Prentice-Hall, (1976)] predicts the ring to become
87 unstable to non-axisymmetric perturbations with an azimuthal
88 wavenumber of \f$ N=2 \f$ at a pressure
89 of
90 \f[
91 p_{ext}^{[buckl]} = 3 \times \frac{1}{12} h^3.
92 \f]
93 For \f$ p_{ext} > p_{ext}^{[buckl]} \f$ we therefore expect the ring to
94 deform into a shape similar to the one indicated by the dashed line
95 in the above sketch. As the ring buckles non-axisymmetrically,
96 the material point on
97 the vertical symmetry line (i.e. the material point with Lagrangian coordinate
98 \f$ \xi=\pi/2\f$ ) moves radially inwards. This makes it
99 an excellent control point for this problem as we can "sweep" through
100 the entire range of the ring's post-buckling deformation by
101 varying its \f$ x_2 \f$ -- coordinate
102 from \f$ R_{ctrl} =1 \f$ (corresponding to the undeformed,
103 axisymmetric configuration) to \f$ R_{ctrl} = 0 \f$
104 (corresponding to a configuration in which the ring is collapsed
105 to the point of opposite wall contact). We note that Flaherty,
106 Keller & Rubinow's analysis [SIAM J. Appl. Math \b 23, 446--455
107 (1972)], based on an inextensible beam model, predicts opposite
108 wall contact to occur at an external pressure of
109 \f[
110 p_{ext}^{[owc]} = 5.22 \times \frac{1}{12} h^3.
111 \f]

164 \section results Results
165 
166 The figure below shows computed ring shapes for \f$ h^*/R_0 =
167 1/20 \f$. They were obtained in two phases: During the first phase
168 of the computation we subjected the ring to a load of the form
169 \f[
170 {\bf f} = \bigg( p_{ext} - p_{cos} \cos(2 \xi) \bigg) \, {\bf N}
171 \f]
172 where \f$ {\bf N} \f$ is the outer unit normal on the deformed ring
173 and \f$ p_{cos} \ll 1 \f$ is a small cosinusoidal perturbation
174 to the external pressure which forces the ring to buckle
175 "in the right direction". The undeformed configuration was
176 used as the initial guess for an initial computation with
177 \f$ p_{ext} = 0\f$ [or, in the case of displacement control,
178 \f$ R_{ctrl} = 1\f$ ]. Subsequently we increased \f$ p_{ext} \f$
179 [or decreased \f$ R_{ctrl}\f$ ] in small steps, using the previously
180 computed solutions for the displacement field [and \f$ p_{ext} \f$ ]
181 as initial guesses for the unknowns. This procedure was continued until
182 the ring was collapsed up to the point of opposite wall contact.
183 During the second phase, we set \f$ p_{cos} = 0\f$ and reversed the
184 procedure to re-trace the deformation to the axisymmetric state.

223 \section displ_control Applying displacement control in oomph-lib
224 
225 To facilitate the solution of solid mechanics problems with
226 displacement-control techniques, \c oomph-lib provides a
227 \c DisplacementControlElement, which may be used to
228 add the displacement control constraint to the global system of
229 equations that is solved by Newton's method. Applying displacement
230 control in a solid mechanics problem involves the following
231 straightforward steps:
232 -# <B>Formulate the solid mechanics problems in its "standard
233  form":</B> \n\n In this formulation the (scalar) load level is
234  prescribed and the displacements are regarded as unknowns. \n\n
235 -# <B>Change the representation of the (scalar) load level to allow
236  it to be an unknown in the Problem:</B> \n\n In the original
237  formulation, the load level
238  is likely to have been represented by a double precision number.
239  To allow the load level to be regarded as an unknown, it must be
240  represented by a value in a \c Data object. Currently,
241  \c oomph-lib's \c DisplacementControlElement expects the load level
242  to be the one-and-only value in the load \c Data object. We note
243  that computations with a prescribed load are still possible and
244  simply require pinning of the value that represents the load. \n\n
245 -# <B>"Tell" the solid mechanics elements that the load level is a (potential)
246  unknown:</B> \n\n
247  Since the load on the solid mechanics elements affects their
248  residuals, their elemental Jacobian matrices (which contain
249  the derivatives of the entries in the elemental residual vector
250  with respect to \e all unknowns that affect it) must take the
251  dependence on the (potentially) unknown load level into account. This
252  may be achieved by adding the \c Data object that stores the load
253  level to the element's external \c Data (i.e. \c Data whose
254  values \e affect the element's residual vector, but whose values are
255  not determined \e by the element). External \c Data is
256  automatically included in an element's equation numbering
257  procedure. Furthermore, since the elemental Jacobian matrices of
258  \c SolidFiniteElements are generated by finite-differencing,
259  the derivatives of the element's residual vector with respect
260  to the load level are computed automatically. Consequently, the
261  application of displacement control does not require a
262  re-implementation of the solid mechanics elements. \n\n
263 -# <B>Identify a material point in the solid that can serve as a
264  "control point":</B> \n \n Ideally, this control point should move
265  monotonically along one of the (Eulerian) coordinate directions as the
266  deformation of the solid body increases. \n\n
267 -# <B>Create a \c DisplacementControlElement and add it to the
268  \c Mesh.</B> \n\n
269  The \c DisplacementControlElement adds the displacement constraint
270  to the global system of equations and thus provides the
271  additional equation required to determine the unknown load
272  level. We note that the \c DisplacementControlElement has
273  two constructors: \n\n
274  - The first version expects a pointer to the \c Data object
275  whose one-and-only value contains the unknown load level.
276  This version of the constructor is appropriate in cases where
277  the load \c Data has already been created elsewhere (as described
278  above). \n\n
279  - The second version of the constructor creates the required
280  load \c Data object internally and provides access to it via
281  a pointer-based access function. \n\n
282  .
283  The section \ref global_data provides a more detailed discussion
284  of when to use which version of the constructor.
285  \n\n
286 -# <B>Done!</B> \n\n Set the desired value of the control displacement
287  and solve the problem. \n\n
288 .
289 
290 The driver code discussed below illustrates these steps.

485 \section further Further comments and Exercises
486 \subsection global_data "Internal" and "external" Data in elements and "global" Data in Problems
487 
488 In the section \ref constructor we encountered two
489 different constructors for the \c DisplacementControlElement.
490 - The first version (in which the \c Data object that contains the
491  unknown load value is created by the constructor) is most natural
492  for the problem considered here as the load \c Data is
493  created specifically for the purpose of allowing displacement
494  control to be applied. It therefore natural to regard the
495  \c DisplacementControlElement as being "in charge of" the load
496  \c Data , and storing it in the element's "internal Data".
497  (<A HREF="../../../the_data_structure/html/index.html">Recall</A>
498  that once \c Data is stored in an element's internal \c Data,
499  the element becomes responsible for performing tasks such as equation
500  numbering, timestepping, etc. that must be performed exactly once
501  for each \c Data value in the \c Problem.) \n\n
502 - The second version of the constructor is appropriate for problems
503  in which the load \c Data has already been created by another
504  element, implying that the other element is already "in charge"
505  of the \c Data object and performs the equation numbering,
506  timestepping etc. for its values. In that case, the load \c Data
507  is regarded as "external Data" for the \c
508  DisplacementControlElement.
509 .
510 In our example code, we simulated the second scenario
511 by creating the load \c Data object before calling the
512 second version of the constructor. While this ensures that
513 the load \e can be regarded as an unknown in the problem,
514 the \c Problem remains "unaware" of the additional unknown,
515 as none of the elements in the \c Problem's mesh is "in charge"
516 of it. While this is clearly a
517 somewhat artificial scenario, \c oomph-lib provides a
518 mechanism for handling such cases: Adding a \c Data object
519 to the \c Problem's "global Data" by calling
520 
521 \code
522 Problem::add_global_data(...)
523 \endcode
524 
525 puts the \c Problem itself "in charge" of this \c Data.
