3 \mainpage Demo problem: Large-amplitude non-axisymmetric oscillations of a thin-walled elastic ring
4 
5 In this document we discuss the solution of a time-dependent
6 beam problem: The large-amplitude oscillations of a thin-walled
7 elastic ring. Specifically we shall
8 - demonstrate how to specify initial conditions for time-dependent
9  simulations with \c oomph-lib's \c KirchhoffLoveBeam elements,
10 - demonstrate how to use the \c dump/restart functions for \c KirchhoffLoveBeam
11  elements,
12 - show that timesteppers from the \c Newmark family
13  - can be used with variable timesteps,
14  - conserve energy,
15  - can allocate and maintain storage for the solution at previous timesteps.
16 .

23 <B>Large-amplitude oscillations of a thin-walled elastic beam</B>
24 </CENTER>
25 
26 We wish to compute the large-amplitude oscillations of a
27 linearly-elastic, circular
28 ring of undeformed radius \f$ R_0 \f$ and wall thickness \f$ h^* \f$ ,
29 subject to a transient pressure load
30 \f[
31 p_{ext}\left(\xi ,t \right) =
32 \left\{
33 \begin{array}{ll}
34 -P_{cos}\cos\left(2\xi\right) & \mbox{for $t<T_{kick}$} \\
35 0 & \mbox{for $t>T_{kick}$}
36 \end{array}
37 \right.
38 \mbox{\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1) }
39 \f]
40 which initiates an oscillation in which the ring deforms into a
41 non-axisymmetric mode, as indicated in the sketch below:
42 
43 \image html ring_sketch.gif "Sketch of the buckling ring. "
44 \image latex ring_sketch.eps "Sketch of the buckling ring. " width=0.75\textwidth
45 
46 We choose the same non-dimensionalisation as in
47 <A HREF="../../steady_ring/html/index.html"> the previous steady
48 example</A> and parametrise the position vector
49 to the ring's undeformed centreline as
50 \f[
51 {\bf r}_w(\xi) = {\bf R}_w(\xi,t=0) = \left(
52 \begin{array}{c}
53 \cos(\xi) \\
54 \sin(\xi)
55 \end{array}
56 \right),
57 \f]
58 where the non-dimensional arclength \f$ \xi \in [0,2\pi] \f$
59 along the ring's undeformed centreline acts as the Lagrangian
60 coordinate. Assuming that the ring is at rest for \f$ t\le 0 \f$ ,
61 we wish to compute the position vector to the deformed ring's
62 centreline, \f$ {\bf R}_w(\xi,t) \f$ , for \f$ t > 0 \f$ .

246 \subsection prev How to retrieve the solution at previous timesteps
247 
248 The next few lines illustrate how to retrieve (and document) the ring shape at
249 previous timesteps.
250 <A HREF="../../../linear_wave/two_d_linear_wave/html/index.html#IC">Recall
251 </A> that \c Newmark timesteppers are implicit, single-step
252 timestepping schemes that compute approximations for the
253 time-derivatives, based on the solution at the current time level,
254 and on "history values" that represent quantities at a single previous
255 timestep. In some applications (particularly in fluid-structure
256 interaction problems) it is necessary to keep track of the solution
257 at additional previous timesteps. Storage for such additional history values
258 is allocated (and managed) by the generalised \c Newmark<NSTEPS>
259 timesteppers if \c NSTEPS > 1. We stress that these
260 additional history values are not involved in the approximation
261 of the time-derivatives; they are simply stored and updated by the
262 timestepper when the solution is advanced to the next timestep.
263 
264 
265 <A HREF="../../../unsteady_heat/two_d_unsteady_heat/html/index.html#IC">Recall
266 </A> also that the member function \c TimeStepper::nprev_values() may
267 be used to determine how many of the history values that are stored in an
268 associated \c Data object represent the solution at previous
269 timesteps. Finally,
270 <A HREF="../../../unsteady_heat/two_d_unsteady_heat/html/index.html#IC">recall
271 </A> that history values that represent the solution at previous
272 timesteps are always stored before those that represent "generalised" history
273 values (such as approximations of the first time-derivative at the
274 previous timestep, etc). It is therefore always possible to determine
275 how many previous solutions are stored in a \c Data object, and where
276 they are stored.

401 \section comments Comments and Exercises
402 \subsection timescale The default non-dimensionalisation of time
403 The non-dimensionalisation of the principle of virtual displacements
404 that forms the basis of \c oomph-lib's beam elements, was
405 discussed in detail in <A HREF="../../tensioned_string/html/index.html">
406 an earlier example</A>. However, since this is the first
407 time-dependent beam problem it is worth re-iterating that, by default, time is
408 non-dimensionalised on the timescale for extensional deformations
409 i.e. on the natural timescale
410 \f[
411 {\cal T}_{natural} = {\cal L} \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E_{eff}}}
412 \f]
413 of oscillations in which the beam
414 is stretched/compressed along its centreline. The relation between
415 the dimensional time \f$ t^* \f$ and its non-dimensional equivalent \f$ t
416 \f$ is given by
417 \f[
418 t^* = \underbrace{{\cal L}
419 \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E_{eff}}}}_{{\cal T}_{natural}} \ t,
420 \f]
421 where \f$ {\cal L} \f$ is the lengthscale used to non-dimensionalise all
422 lengths (in the present example \f$ {\cal L} = R_0 \f$ ,
423 the undeformed radius of the ring),
424 \f$ \rho \f$ is the density of the ring, and \f$ E_{eff} = E/(1-\nu^2)\f$
425 is the "effective" 1D Young's modulus of the beam, formed with
426 its 3D Young's modulus \f$ E \f$ , and its Poisson ratio \f$ \nu \f$ .
427 
428 This non-dimensionalisation of time is consistent with the
429 non-dimensionalisation of all stresses/loads on \f$ E_{eff}\f$.
430 It implies that if the beam deforms in a mode in which its
431 deformation is dominated by bending effects, the numerical
432 values for the non-dimensional load are relatively
433 small (indicating that the loads required to induce a deformation
434 of a given size are much smaller if the ring deforms in a
435 bending-dominated mode, than in a mode in which it is dominated
436 by extensional deformations), while the non-dimensional
437 period of the oscillation is relatively large (indicating that bending
438 oscillations occur at a much smaller frequency than oscillations in
439 which the beam's deformation is dominated by extensional deformations).
440

444 \subsection energies The default non-dimensionalisation for the kinetic and potential (strain) energies
445 
446 With the default non-dimensionalisation discussed above, the non-dimensional
447 kinetic and potential (strain) energies are given by
448 \f[
449 \Pi_{strain} = \frac{\Pi_{strain}^*}{{\cal L} \, h^* E_{eff} } =
450 \frac{1}{2}\int \left( \gamma^2 +
451 \frac{1}{12}\left(\frac{h^*}{{\cal L}}\right)^2
452 \kappa^2 \right) \ d \xi
453 \f]
454 and
455 \f[
456 \Pi_{kin} = \frac{\Pi_{kin}^*}{{\cal L} \, h^* E_{eff}}
457 = \frac{1}{2}
458 \int
459 \frac{\partial {\bf R}_w}{\partial t} \cdot
460 \frac{\partial {\bf R}_w}{\partial t} \ d \xi,
461 \f]
462 respectively. Conservation of energy implies that
463 \f[
464 \Pi_{kin} + \Pi_{strain} = const.
465 \f]
466 if there is no external forcing. The plot of the energies
467 shown at the beginning of this document shows that the
468 time-integration with the Newmark method is energy-conserving.
469 
470 <HR>
471 \subsection change_T Changing the timescale used to non-dimensionalise the equations
472 
473 It is possible to non-dimensionalise the governing equations on
474 a different timescale, \f$ {\cal T} \f$ , so that
475 \f[
476 t^* = {\cal T} \ t.
477 \f]
478 This is achieved by overwriting the default assignment
479 for the ratio
480 \f[
481 \Lambda = \frac{{\cal T}_{natural}}{{\cal T}} =
482 \frac{{\cal L}}{{\cal T}} \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E_{eff}}},
483 \f]
484 of the natural timescale \f$ {\cal T}_{natural}\f$ and
485 the time \f$ {\cal T} \f$ used to non-dimensionalise the equations.
486 
487 By default, we have \f$ \Lambda = 1 \f$ but the member function
488 \code
489 KirchhoffLoveBeamEquations::lambda_sq_pt()
490 \endcode
491 may be used to assign a different value for the \e square of the
492 timescale ratio which may also be interpreted as the
493 non-dimensional density. The case \f$ \Lambda^2=0 \f$ therefore
494 corresponds to the case of zero wall inertia.
495 (We store \f$ \Lambda^2 \f$ rather than \f$ \Lambda
496 \f$ itself because the governing equations contain only the square of the
497 timescale ratio). As with most other physical parameters,
498 \f$ \Lambda^2 \f$ must be defined as a global variable, preferably
499 by adding it to the namespace that contains the problem parameters,
500 e.g.
501 
502 \code
503 //====start_of_namespace============================
504 /// Namespace for global parameters
505 //==================================================
506 namespace Global_Physical_Variables
507 {
508 
509  /// Square of timescale ratio, i.e. the non-dimensional density
510  double Lambda_sq=4.0;
511 
512  [...]
513 
514 } // end of namespace
515 \endcode
516 
517 ...and passing a pointer to \f$ \Lambda^2 \f$ to the elements.
518 The statement can be added to the loop over the elements that
519 passes the pointer to the pressure load to the elements.
520 
521 \code
522 
523  //Loop over the elements to set physical parameters etc.
524  for(unsigned i=0;i<n_element;i++)
525  {
526  // Cast to proper element type
527  ELEMENT *elem_pt = dynamic_cast<ELEMENT*>(mesh_pt()->element_pt(i));
528 
529  // Pass pointer to square of timescale ratio (non-dimensional density)
530  elem_pt->lambda_sq_pt() = &Global_Physical_Variables::Lambda_sq;
531 
532  [...]
533 
534  }
535 
536 \endcode
537 
538 The definition of the non-dimensional kinetic energy
539 used in \c KirchhoffLoveBeamEquations::get_energy(...) incorporates
540 the timescale ratio by computing the non-dimensional kinetic energy as
541 \f[
542 \Pi_{kin} =
543 \frac{\Pi_{kin}^*}{{\cal L} \, h^* E_{eff}} =
544 \frac{1}{2} \Lambda^2
545 \int
546 \frac{\partial {\bf R}_w}{\partial t} \cdot
547 \frac{\partial {\bf R}_w}{\partial t} \ d \xi
548 \f]
549 which, for the default assignment \f$ \Lambda^2 = 1 \f$ , reduces
550 to the definition given above.

553 \subsection exercises Exercises
554 -# As discussed in \ref timescale , the period of the oscillation
555  ( \f$ \approx 170 \f$ ) computed in the above example has a
556  large numerical value because
557  time was non-dimensionalised on a timescale that is representative
558  for oscillations in which the ring's motion is dominated by
559  extensional (rather than bending) deformations. Furthermore,
560  because the load is non-dimensionalised on the ring's extensional
561  (rather than its bending) stiffness, a very small load
562  \f$ p_{cos} = 10^{-4} \f$ was sufficient to induce large-amplitude
563  oscillations.
564  \n \n
565  Change the load function to a spatially-constant external pressure
566  (which will deform the ring axisymmetrically so that its
567  motion is dominated by extensional deformations) to confirm
568  that in this mode
569  -# \f$ {\cal O}(1) \f$ pressures are required to induce
570  \f$ {\cal O}(1) \f$ axisymmetric displacements, and
571  -# the period of the axisymmetric oscillations is
572  \f$ {\cal O}(1) \f$.
573  .
574  [\b Hint: You will have to reduce the timestep to capture the
575  much faster axisymmetric oscillations.]
576 -# Use the principle of virtual displacements (without prestress)
577  \f[
578  \int_0^{L} \left[
579  \gamma \ \delta \gamma +
580  \frac{1}{12} h^2 \kappa
581  \ \delta \kappa -
582  \left(\frac{1}{h} \sqrt{\frac{A}{a}} \ {\bf f} - \Lambda^2
583  \frac{\partial^2 {\bf R}_w}{\partial t^2} \right) \cdot
584  \delta {\bf R}_w
585  \right] \ \sqrt{a} \ d\xi = 0,
586  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (2)
587  \f]
588  to show (analytically) that for bending-dominated deformations (i.e.
589  deformations for which \f$ |\gamma| \ll |\kappa| \f$)
590  -# an increase in the wall thickness \f$ h \f$ by a factor 2, say, will
591  -# reduce the amplitude of the ring's static deformation
592  by a factor of 8 (thicker rings are stiffer)
593  -# reduces the period of the unforced oscillations
594  (i.e. the oscillations it performs when \f$ {\bf f} =
595  {\bf 0} \f$ ) by factor of 4 (thicker
596  rings oscillate more rapidly).
597  .
598  -# an increase in the ring's density \f$ \rho \sim \Lambda^2\f$
599  by a factor of 2, say, increases the period of its
600  bending-dominated oscillations by a factor of 4 (heavier rings
601  oscillate more slowly).
602  .
603  Combine these results to show that
604  a ring of wall thickness \f$ h \f$ and a density \f$ \rho \f$,
605  subject to a forcing of magnitude \f$ f \f$ will deform
606  (approximately) as a ring of wall thickness \f$ \alpha h \f$ with
607  a density \f$ \alpha^2 \rho \f$, subject to a forcing of magnitude
608  \f$ \alpha^3 f\f$.
