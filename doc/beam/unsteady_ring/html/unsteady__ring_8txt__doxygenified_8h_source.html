3\mainpage Demo problem: Large-amplitude non-axisymmetric oscillations of a thin-walled elastic ring
4
5In this document we discuss the solution of a time-dependent
6beam problem: The large-amplitude oscillations of a thin-walled
7elastic ring. Specifically we shall
8- demonstrate how to specify initial conditions for time-dependent
9 simulations with \c oomph-lib's \c KirchhoffLoveBeam elements,
10- demonstrate how to use the \c dump/restart functions for \c KirchhoffLoveBeam
11 elements,
12- show that timesteppers from the \c Newmark family
13 - can be used with variable timesteps,
14 - conserve energy,
15 - can allocate and maintain storage for the solution at previous timesteps.
16.

22<CENTER>
23<B>Large-amplitude oscillations of a thin-walled elastic beam</B>
24</CENTER>
25
26We wish to compute the large-amplitude oscillations of a
27linearly-elastic, circular
28ring of undeformed radius \f$ R_0 \f$ and wall thickness \f$ h^* \f$ ,
29subject to a transient pressure load
30\f[
31p_{ext}\left(\xi ,t \right) =
32\left\{
33\begin{array}{ll}
34-P_{cos}\cos\left(2\xi\right) & \mbox{for $t<T_{kick}$} \\
350 & \mbox{for $t>T_{kick}$}
36\end{array}
37\right.
38\mbox{\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1) }
39\f]
40which initiates an oscillation in which the ring deforms into a
41non-axisymmetric mode, as indicated in the sketch below:
42
43\image html ring_sketch.gif "Sketch of the buckling ring. "
44\image latex ring_sketch.eps "Sketch of the buckling ring. " width=0.75\textwidth
45
46We choose the same non-dimensionalisation as in
47<A HREF="../../steady_ring/html/index.html"> the previous steady
48example</A> and parametrise the position vector
49to the ring's undeformed centreline as
50\f[
51{\bf r}_w(\xi) = {\bf R}_w(\xi,t=0) = \left(
52\begin{array}{c}
53\cos(\xi) \\
54\sin(\xi)
55\end{array}
56\right),
57\f]
58where the non-dimensional arclength \f$ \xi \in [0,2\pi] \f$
59along the ring's undeformed centreline acts as the Lagrangian
60coordinate. Assuming that the ring is at rest for \f$ t\le 0 \f$ ,
61we wish to compute the position vector to the deformed ring's
62centreline, \f$ {\bf R}_w(\xi,t) \f$ , for \f$ t > 0 \f$ .

88The red line with square markers in the figure below shows the
89time-history of the ring's control displacement: At \f$ t=0 \f$
90the ring is in its initial undeformed configuration and we have
91\f$ R_{ctrl} = 1 \f$ . Application of the cosinusoidal pressure

93the ring to deform non-axisymmetrically in the mode shape shown

101which it traverses with finite velocity at \f$ t \approx 100 \f$ .
102The ring overshoots the axisymmetric state and deforms in the
103"opposite" direction to the deformation during the initial stages of collapse.
104It reverses its motion again when it reaches a second non-axisymmetric
105extreme at \f$ t \approx 150 \f$ . This is seen most clearly in the
106<A HREF="../figures/unsteady_ring.avi">animation of the ring's motion. </A>

246\subsection prev How to retrieve the solution at previous timesteps
247
248The next few lines illustrate how to retrieve (and document) the ring shape at
249previous timesteps.
250<A HREF="../../../linear_wave/two_d_linear_wave/html/index.html#IC">Recall
251</A> that \c Newmark timesteppers are implicit, single-step
252timestepping schemes that compute approximations for the
253time-derivatives, based on the solution at the current time level,
254and on "history values" that represent quantities at a single previous
255timestep. In some applications (particularly in fluid-structure
256interaction problems) it is necessary to keep track of the solution
257at additional previous timesteps. Storage for such additional history values
258is allocated (and managed) by the generalised \c Newmark<NSTEPS>
259timesteppers if \c NSTEPS > 1. We stress that these
260additional history values are not involved in the approximation
261of the time-derivatives; they are simply stored and updated by the
262timestepper when the solution is advanced to the next timestep.
263
264
265<A HREF="../../../unsteady_heat/two_d_unsteady_heat/html/index.html#IC">Recall
266</A> also that the member function \c TimeStepper::nprev_values() may
267be used to determine how many of the history values that are stored in an
268associated \c Data object represent the solution at previous
269timesteps. Finally,
270<A HREF="../../../unsteady_heat/two_d_unsteady_heat/html/index.html#IC">recall
271</A> that history values that represent the solution at previous
272timesteps are always stored before those that represent "generalised" history
273values (such as approximations of the first time-derivative at the
274previous timestep, etc). It is therefore always possible to determine
275how many previous solutions are stored in a \c Data object, and where
276they are stored.
277
278To document the shape of a \c HermiteBeamElement at a previous timestep,
279the \c HermiteBeamElement provides an additional three-argument
280output function that may be called as follows:
281
282\until end of output of previous solutions
283
284At timestep \c t, these statements create the output files
285\c RESLT/ring t\c -0\c .dat, \c RESLT/ring t\c -1\c .dat, \c
286RESLT/ring t\c -2\c .dat, \c RESLT/ring t\c -3\c .dat, which contain the

401\section comments Comments and Exercises
402\subsection timescale The default non-dimensionalisation of time
403The non-dimensionalisation of the principle of virtual displacements
404that forms the basis of \c oomph-lib's beam elements, was
405discussed in detail in <A HREF="../../tensioned_string/html/index.html">
406an earlier example</A>. However, since this is the first
407time-dependent beam problem it is worth re-iterating that, by default, time is
408non-dimensionalised on the timescale for extensional deformations
409i.e. on the natural timescale
410\f[
411{\cal T}_{natural} = {\cal L} \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E_{eff}}}
412\f]
413of oscillations in which the beam
414is stretched/compressed along its centreline. The relation between
415the dimensional time \f$ t^* \f$ and its non-dimensional equivalent \f$ t
416\f$ is given by
417\f[
418t^* = \underbrace{{\cal L}
419\sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E_{eff}}}}_{{\cal T}_{natural}} \ t,
420\f]
421where \f$ {\cal L} \f$ is the lengthscale used to non-dimensionalise all
422lengths (in the present example \f$ {\cal L} = R_0 \f$ ,
423the undeformed radius of the ring),
424\f$ \rho \f$ is the density of the ring, and \f$ E_{eff} = E/(1-\nu^2)\f$
425is the "effective" 1D Young's modulus of the beam, formed with
426its 3D Young's modulus \f$ E \f$ , and its Poisson ratio \f$ \nu \f$ .
427
428This non-dimensionalisation of time is consistent with the
429non-dimensionalisation of all stresses/loads on \f$ E_{eff}\f$.
430It implies that if the beam deforms in a mode in which its
431deformation is dominated by bending effects, the numerical
432values for the non-dimensional load are relatively
433small (indicating that the loads required to induce a deformation
434of a given size are much smaller if the ring deforms in a
435bending-dominated mode, than in a mode in which it is dominated
436by extensional deformations), while the non-dimensional
437period of the oscillation is relatively large (indicating that bending
438oscillations occur at a much smaller frequency than oscillations in
439which the beam's deformation is dominated by extensional deformations).
440

444\subsection energies The default non-dimensionalisation for the kinetic and potential (strain) energies
445
446With the default non-dimensionalisation discussed above, the non-dimensional
447kinetic and potential (strain) energies are given by
448\f[
449\Pi_{strain} = \frac{\Pi_{strain}^*}{{\cal L} \, h^* E_{eff} } =
450\frac{1}{2}\int \left( \gamma^2 +
451\frac{1}{12}\left(\frac{h^*}{{\cal L}}\right)^2
452\kappa^2 \right) \ d \xi
453\f]
454and
455\f[
456\Pi_{kin} = \frac{\Pi_{kin}^*}{{\cal L} \, h^* E_{eff}}
457= \frac{1}{2}
458\int
459\frac{\partial {\bf R}_w}{\partial t} \cdot
460\frac{\partial {\bf R}_w}{\partial t} \ d \xi,
461\f]
462respectively. Conservation of energy implies that
463\f[
464\Pi_{kin} + \Pi_{strain} = const.
465\f]
466if there is no external forcing. The plot of the energies
467shown at the beginning of this document shows that the
468time-integration with the Newmark method is energy-conserving.
469

471\subsection change_T Changing the timescale used to non-dimensionalise the equations
472
473It is possible to non-dimensionalise the governing equations on
474a different timescale, \f$ {\cal T} \f$ , so that
475\f[
476t^* = {\cal T} \ t.
477\f]
478This is achieved by overwriting the default assignment
479for the ratio
480\f[
481\Lambda = \frac{{\cal T}_{natural}}{{\cal T}} =
482\frac{{\cal L}}{{\cal T}} \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E_{eff}}},
483\f]
484of the natural timescale \f$ {\cal T}_{natural}\f$ and
485the time \f$ {\cal T} \f$ used to non-dimensionalise the equations.
486
487By default, we have \f$ \Lambda = 1 \f$ but the member function
488\code
489KirchhoffLoveBeamEquations::lambda_sq_pt()
490\endcode
491may be used to assign a different value for the \e square of the
492timescale ratio which may also be interpreted as the
493non-dimensional density. The case \f$ \Lambda^2=0 \f$ therefore
494corresponds to the case of zero wall inertia.
495(We store \f$ \Lambda^2 \f$ rather than \f$ \Lambda
496\f$ itself because the governing equations contain only the square of the
497timescale ratio). As with most other physical parameters,
498\f$ \Lambda^2 \f$ must be defined as a global variable, preferably
499by adding it to the namespace that contains the problem parameters,
500e.g.
501
502\code
503//====start_of_namespace============================
504/// Namespace for global parameters
505//==================================================
506namespace Global_Physical_Variables
507{
508
509 /// Square of timescale ratio, i.e. the non-dimensional density
510 double Lambda_sq=4.0;
511
512 [...]
513
514} // end of namespace
515\endcode
516
517...and passing a pointer to \f$ \Lambda^2 \f$ to the elements.
518The statement can be added to the loop over the elements that
519passes the pointer to the pressure load to the elements.
520
521\code
522
523 //Loop over the elements to set physical parameters etc.
524 for(unsigned i=0;i<n_element;i++)
525 {
526 // Cast to proper element type
527 ELEMENT *elem_pt = dynamic_cast<ELEMENT*>(mesh_pt()->element_pt(i));
528
529 // Pass pointer to square of timescale ratio (non-dimensional density)
530 elem_pt->lambda_sq_pt() = &Global_Physical_Variables::Lambda_sq;
531
532 [...]
533
534 }
535
536\endcode
537
538The definition of the non-dimensional kinetic energy
539used in \c KirchhoffLoveBeamEquations::get_energy(...) incorporates
540the timescale ratio by computing the non-dimensional kinetic energy as
541\f[
542\Pi_{kin} =
543\frac{\Pi_{kin}^*}{{\cal L} \, h^* E_{eff}} =
544\frac{1}{2} \Lambda^2
545\int
546\frac{\partial {\bf R}_w}{\partial t} \cdot
547\frac{\partial {\bf R}_w}{\partial t} \ d \xi
548\f]
549which, for the default assignment \f$ \Lambda^2 = 1 \f$ , reduces
550to the definition given above.
