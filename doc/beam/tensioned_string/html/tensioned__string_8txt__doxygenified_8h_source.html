32 \section non_dim Theory and non-dimensionalisation
33 
34 \c oomph-lib's beam elements are based on geometrically
35 nonlinear Kirchhoff-Love beam theory with incrementally linear
36 constitutive equations (Young's modulus \f$ E \f$ and Poisson's ratio
37 \f$ \nu \f$). The equations are implemented in non-dimensional form,
38 obtained by non-dimensionalising all length on some lengthscale
39 \f$ {\cal L} \f$, by scaling the stresses and the applied traction on the
40 beam's effective Young's modulus \f$ E_{eff} = E/(1-\nu^2) \f$
41 and by non-dimensionalising time on some timescale \f$ {\cal T} \f$ so that
42 the dimensional (identified by an asterisk) and non-dimensional
43 variables are related by
44 \f[
45 L^* = {\cal L} \ L,
46 \f]
47 \f[
48 h^* = {\cal L} \ h,
49 \f]
50 \f[
51 t^* = {\cal T} \ t,
52 \f]
53 \f[
54 p_{ext}^* = E_{eff} \ p_{ext},
55 \f]
56 and
57 \f[
58 \sigma_0^* = E_{eff} \ \sigma_0.
59 \f]
60 The beam's undeformed shape is parametrised by a non-dimensional Lagrangian
61 coordinate \f$ \xi = \xi^*/{\cal L} \f$ so that the non-dimensional
62 position vector to a material particle on the beam's centreline in the
63 undeformed
64 configuration is given by \f$ {\bf r}_w(\xi) \f$ . We denote the
65 unit normal to the beam's undeformed centreline by \f$ {\bf n} \f$.
66 The applied traction \f$ {\bf f} = {\bf f}^*/E_{eff} \f$
67 (a force per unit deformed length of the beam) deforms the beam,
68 causing its material particles to be displaced to their
69 new positions \f$ {\bf R}_w(\xi) \f$; the unit
70 normal to the beam's deformed centreline is \f$ {\bf N} \f$.
71 
72 
73 The non-dimensional form of the principle of virtual displacements
74 that governs the beams deformation is then given by
75 \f[
76 \int_0^{L} \left[
77 (\sigma_0 + \gamma) \ \delta
78 \gamma +
79 \frac{1}{12} h^2 \kappa
80  \ \delta \kappa -
81 \left(\frac{1}{h} \sqrt{\frac{A}{a}} \ {\bf f} - \Lambda^2
82 \frac{\partial^2 {\bf R}_w}{\partial t^2} \right) \cdot
83 \delta {\bf R}_w
84  \right] \ \sqrt{a} \ d\xi = 0,
85 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1)
86 \f]
87 where
88 \f[
89 a = \frac{\partial {\bf r}_w}{\partial \xi}
90 \cdot \frac{\partial {\bf r}_w}{\partial \xi},
91 \f]
92 and
93 \f[
94 A = \frac{\partial {\bf R}_w}{\partial \xi}
95 \cdot \frac{\partial {\bf R}_w}{\partial \xi},
96 \f]
97 represent the squares of the lengths of infinitesimal material line elements
98 in the undeformed and the deformed configurations, respectively.
99 \f[
100 ds = \sqrt{a} \ d\xi \mbox{\ \ \ \ and \ \ \ } dS = \sqrt{A} \ d\xi.
101 \f]
102 \f$ A \f$ and \f$ a \f$ may be interpreted as the "1x1 metric
103 tensors" of the beam's centreline, in the deformed and undeformed
104 configurations, respectively. The ratio \f$ \sqrt{A/a} \f$ represents
105 the "extension ratio" or the "stretch" of the beam's centreline.
106 
107  We represent the curvature of the beam's centreline before and
108 after the deformation by
109 \f[
110 b = {\bf n} \cdot \frac{\partial^2 {\bf
111 r}_w}{d\xi^2}
112 \f]
113 and
114 \f[
115 B = {\bf N} \cdot \frac{\partial^2 {\bf
116 R}_w}{d\xi^2}
117 \f]
118 respectively. The ("1x1") strain and and bending "tensors" \f$ \gamma \f$ and
119 \f$ \kappa \f$ are then given by
120 \f[
121 \gamma = \frac{1}{2}\left(A-a\right)
122 \mbox{\ \ \ and \ \ \ }
123 \kappa = - \left( B-b \right).
124 \f]
125 Finally,
126 \f[
127 \Lambda = \frac{{\cal L}}{{\cal T}} \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E_{eff}}}
128 \f]
129 is the ratio of the natural timescale of the beam's in-plane
130 extensional oscillations,
131 \f[
132 {\cal T}_{natural} = {\cal L} \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E_{eff}}},
133 \f]
134 to the timescale \f$ {\cal T}\f$ used in the non-dimensionalisation of the
135 equations. \f$ \Lambda^2 \f$ may be interpreted as
136 the non-dimensional wall density, therefore \f$ \Lambda=0 \f$
137 corresponds to the case without wall inertia.
138 
139 
140 \anchor hermite
141 \c oomph-lib's \c HermiteBeamElement provides a discretisation of the
142 variational principle (1) with one-dimensional, isoparametric,
143 two-node Hermite solid mechanics elements. In these elements, the Eulerian
144 positions of the nodes, accessible via \c Node::x(...), are regarded
145 as unknowns, and Hermite interpolation is used to interpolate
146 the position between the nodes, so that the Eulerian position of material
147 points within an element is given by
148 \f[
149 x_i(s) = \sum_{j=1}^2 \sum_{k=1}^2 X_{ijk} \ \psi_{jk}(s)
150 \mbox{\ \ \ for $i=1,2$},
151 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (2)
152 \f]
153 where \f$ s \in [-1,1] \f$ is the element's 1D local coordinate.
154 The functions \f$ \psi_{jk}(s) \f$ are the one-dimensional
155 Hermite shape functions
156 \f[ \psi_{11}(s) = \frac{1}{4}\left(s^3 - 3s + 2\right), \f]
157 \f[ \psi_{12}(s) = \frac{1}{4}\left(s^3 - s^2 - s +1 \right), \f]
158 \f[ \psi_{21}(s) = \frac{1}{4}\left(2 + 3 s - s^3\right), \f]
159 \f[ \psi_{22}(s) = \frac{1}{4}\left(s^3 + s^2 -s -1 \right). \f]
160 They have the property that
161 - \f$ \psi_{j1} = 1 \f$ at node \f$ j \f$ and \f$ \psi_{j1} = 0 \f$
162  at the other node. Furthermore, \f$ d\psi_{j1}/ds = 0 \f$ at
163  both nodes.
164 - \f$ d\psi_{j2}/ds = 1 \f$ at node \f$ j \f$ and \f$ d\psi_{j2}/ds = 0 \f$
165  at the other node. Furthermore, \f$ \psi_{j2} = 0 \f$ at
166  both nodes.
167 .
168 The mapping (2) therefore provide an
169 independent interpolation for the position and its derivative
170 with respect to the local coordinate \f$ s \f$. As a result
171 we have two types of (generalised) nodal coordinates:
172 - \f$ X_{ij1} \f$ represents the \f$ i \f$-th coordinate of the element's
173  local node \f$ j \f$.
174 - \f$ X_{ij2} \f$ represents the derivative of \f$ i \f$-th coordinate
175  with respect to the local coordinate \f$ s \f$, evaluated
176  at the element's local node \f$ j \f$.
177 .
178 This representation ensures the
179 \f$ C^1 \f$ continuity of the wall shape, required by
180 the variational principle (1) which contains second derivatives
181 of \f$ {\bf R}_w \f$. Physically, the inter-element continuity
182 of the slope is enforced by the beam's nonzero bending stiffness.
183 
184 \anchor solid_bound
185 The two "types" of "generalised" positional degrees of freedom
186 are accessible via the function \c Node::x_gen(k,i), where
187 \c Node::x_gen(0,i) \f$ \equiv \f$ \c Node::x(i).
188 The two types of positional degrees of freedom correspond to
189 the two types of boundary conditions (pinned and clamped) that can be applied
190 at the ends of the beam. Mathematically, the number of boundary
191 conditions reflects the fact that the Euler-Lagrange equations
192 of the variational principle are of fourth-order.

333 \section approx An approximate analytical solution
334 
335 Before discussing the details of the numerical solution, we will derive an
336 approximate analytical solution of the problem. The analytical solution
337 will be useful to validate the computational results, and the derivation
338 will allow us to discuss certain aspects of the theory in more detail.
339 
340 We start by parametrising the beam's undeformed shape as
341 \f[
342 {\bf r}_w(\xi) =
343 \left(
344 \begin{array}{c}
345 \xi \\
346 0
347 \end{array}
348 \right)
349 \mbox{ \ \ \ \ where $\xi \in [0,L].$}
350 \f]
351 The 1x1 "metric tensor" associated with this parametrisation is given by
352 \f[
353 a = \frac{{\bf r}_w(\xi)}{d\xi} \cdot \frac{{\bf r}_w(\xi)}{d\xi} = 1,
354 \f]
355 consistent with the fact that the Lagrangian coordinate \f$ \xi \f$
356 is the arclength along the beam's undeformed centreline.
357 
358 
359 If the beam is thin (so that \f$ h \ll 1 \f$) bending effects
360 will be confined to thin boundary layers near its
361 ends. The beam will therefore behave (approximately)
362 like a "string under tension" and its deformed shape will
363 be an arc of a circle. All material line elements will be stretched
364 by the same amount so that the tension is spatially uniform. The position
365 vector to the material particles on the beam's deformed centreline is
366 therefore given by
367 \f[
368 {\bf R}_w(\xi) =
369 \left(
370 \begin{array}{c}
371 \frac{1}{2}\left(1+\frac{\sin(-\alpha/2+\alpha\xi/L)}
372 {\sin(\alpha/2)}\right) \\
373 \frac{\cos(-\alpha/2+\alpha\xi/L)-\cos(\alpha/2)}
374 {2\sin(\alpha/2)}
375 \end{array}
376 \right)
377 \mbox{ \ \ \ \ where $\xi \in [0,L].$}
378 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (3)
379 \f]
380 (See the \ref comments for a more detailed discussion of this
381 analytical solution.) Here \f$ \alpha \f$ is the opening angle
382 of the circular arc as shown in this sketch:
383 
384 
385 \image html string.gif "Sketch illustrating the approximate analytical solution for the problem. "
386 \image latex string.eps "Sketch illustrating the approximate analytical solution for the problem. " width=0.75\textwidth
387 
388 This deformation generates a uniform stretch of
389 \f[
390 \frac{dS}{ds} = \sqrt{\frac{A}{a}} = \frac{1}{2} \frac{\alpha}{\sin(\alpha/2)},
391 \f]
392 corresponding to a uniform strain
393 \f[
394 \gamma = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{4}\frac{\alpha^2}{\sin^2(\alpha/2)}
395 - 1 \right).
396 \f]
397 The incremental Hooke's law predicts a linear relation between
398 the (2nd Piola Kirchhoff) stress, \f$ \sigma \f$, and the
399 (Green) strain, \f$ \gamma \f$, so that
400 \f[
401 \sigma = \sigma_0 + \gamma,
402 \f]
403 where \f$ \sigma_0 \f$ is the axial pre-stress that acts in the undeformed
404 configuration.
405 
406 An elementary force balance shows that the pressure
407 \f$ p_{ext} \f$ required to deform the beam into the
408 shape specified by (3), is given by "Laplace's law"
409 \f[
410 p_{ext} =\frac{{\tt T}}{R}
411 \f]
412 where
413 \f[
414 R = \frac{L}{2 \sin(\alpha/2)}
415 \f]
416 is the radius of the circular arc,
417 and the axial tension \f$ {\tt T} \f$ is given by
418 \f[
419 {\tt T} = h \ (\sigma_0 + \gamma) \ \left|\frac{d{\bf R}_w}{d\xi}\right|
420 = h \ (\sigma_0 + \gamma) \ \sqrt{A}.
421 \f]
422 In this expression we have used the fact that the 2nd Piola Kirchhoff
423 stress \f$ \sigma \f$ decomposes the (physical) stress vector into the
424 vector \f$ d{\bf R}_w/d\xi \f$. This vector is tangent to the
425 deformed centreline but not necessarily a unit vector.
426 
427 The pressure required to deform the beam into a circular arc
428 with opening angle \f$ \alpha \f$ is therefore given by
429 \f[
430 p_{ext} =
431  \frac{\alpha h}{L}
432  \ \left(\sigma_0 +
433 \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{4}\frac{\alpha^2}{\sin^2(\alpha/2)} - 1
434 \right)
435 \right).
436 \f]
437 
438 
439 To facilitate comparisons with the numerical solutions, we determine
440 the opening angle \f$ \alpha \f$ as a function of the vertical
441 displacement \f$ d \f$ of the beam's midpoint by determining the
442 auxiliary angle \f$ \beta \f$ from
443  \f[
444 \tan\beta = \frac{2d}{L}.
445 \f]
446 The opening angle then follows from
447 \f[
448 \tan\left(\frac{\alpha}{2}\right) = \tan(\pi -
449  2\beta) = - \tan 2\beta = \frac{2\tan\beta}{\tan^{2}\beta -1}.
450 \f]
451 
452 Here is a comparison between the computed and analytically predicted
453 values for the pressure \f$ p_{ext} \f$, required to deflect
454 the beam's midpoint by the specified displacement, \f$ d \f$.
455

463 <CENTER><B>Comment: Geometric and constitutive nonlinearities</B></CENTER>
464 The comparison between the computational results and the analytical
465 predictions is very satisfying but is important to realise that
466 the agreement only validates the numerical solution, not the
467 physical model. \c oomph-lib's \c HermiteBeamElement is based on a \b geometrically
468 \b nonlinear theory, implying that the kinematics of the deformation
469 are captured exactly for arbitrarily large displacements and
470 rotations. However, the use of \b incrementally \b linear \b constitutive
471 \b equations in the variational principle
472 (1) can only be justified if the strain is small. This
473 is clearly not the case for the deformations shown above. The
474 combination of geometric nonlinearity with linear constitutive
475 equations can, however, be justified in applications in which the beam
476 undergoes large displacements with little extension of its centreline.
477 This typically occurs in stability problems, such as the
478 <a href="../../steady_ring/html/index.html">
479 buckling of a circular ring under external pressure</a>, considered
480 in <a href="../../steady_ring/html/index.html">another example</a>.

532 \section problem The problem class
533 
534 The problem class has five member functions, only two of which are
535 non-trivial:
536  - the problem constructor, \c ElasticBeamProblem(...), whose
537  arguments specify the number of elements and the beam's
538  undeformed length.
539  - the function \c parameter_study(), which computes the beam's
540  deformation for a range of external pressures.
541 .
542 
543  In the present problem, the functions
544 \c Problem::actions_before_newton_solve() and \c
545 Problem::actions_after_newton_solve() are not required, so remain
546 empty. The function
547 \c ElasticBeamProblem::mesh_pt() overloads the
548 (virtual) function \c Problem::mesh_pt() to return a pointer
549 to the specific mesh used in this problem. This avoids explicit re-casts
550 when member functions of the specific mesh need to be accessed.
551 
552  The class also includes three private data members which store
553 a pointer to a node at which the displacement is documented,
554 the length of the domain, and a pointer to the geometric object
555 that specifies the beam's undeformed shape.

564 \section constructor The Problem constructor
565 We start by creating the undeformed centreline of the beam as a
566 \c StraightLine, one of \c oomph-lib's standard geometric objects.
567 
568 \skipline start_of_constructor
569 \until Undef_beam_pt
570 
571  We then construct the a one-dimensional Lagrangian mesh in
572  two-dimensional space, using the previously-constructed geometric
573  object to set the initial positions of the nodes.
574 
575  \skipline Create
576  \until OneDLagrangianMesh
577 
578 The \c OneDLagrangianMesh is a \c SolidMesh whose constituent nodes are

652 \section comments Exercises and Comments
653 
654 -# Modify the code so that one end of the beam is no longer fixed in
655  space. What happens? Why?
656 -# Increase the bending effects by increasing the beam's
657  thickness to \f$ h=0.1 \f$, say, and by "clamping" its ends,
658  so that \f$ d({\bf R}_w(\xi)\cdot {\bf e}_y)/d\xi = 0 \f$ at \f$ \xi=0 \f$
659  and \f$ \xi=L \f$. This condition can be enforced by pinning the
660  "type 1" (slope) positional degree of freedom in the vertical
661  (1) direction at both ends; this requires the insertion of the statement
662  \code
663  mesh_pt()->boundary_node_pt(b,0)->pin_position(1,1);
664  \endcode
665  in the loop in the \c Problem constructor.
666  You will have to adjust the number of elements
667  to fully resolve the bending boundary layers.
668 -# \c oomph-lib's default \anchor newton_cust nonlinear solver, \c
669  Problem::newton_solve(...), provides an implementation of
670  the Newton method, which has the attractive feature
671  that it converges quadratically -- provided
672  a good initial guess for the solution is available. Good initial guesses
673  can often (usually?) be generated by computing the solution via a
674  sequence of substeps, as in the above example where we started with
675  a known solution (the undeformed beam -- the exact solution
676  for \f$ p_{ext} = p_{ext}^{(0)} =0 \f$ ) and used it as the
677  initial guess for the solution at a small external
678  pressure, \f$ p_{ext}^{(1)} \f$. When the Newton method converged,
679  we used the computed
680  solution as the initial guess for the solution at a
681  slightly larger pressure, \f$ p_{ext}^{(2)} \f$, etc.
682  If the increase in the load (or some other control parameter) is
683  too large, the Newton method will diverge. To avoid unnecessary
684  computations, the Newton iteration is terminated if:
685  - the number of iterations exceeds \c
686  Problem::Max_newton_iterations (which has a default value of 10)
687  - the residual exceeds \c Problem::Max_residuals (which has a
688  default value of 10.0)
689  .
690  The Newton method continues until the maximum residual
691  has been reduced to \c Problem::Newton_solver_tolerance, which has
692  a default value of \f$ 10^{-8} \f$.
693  \n\n
694  All three values are protected data members of the \c Problem base class
695  and can therefore be changed in any specific \c Problem. For
696  instance, in the problem considered above, the undeformed beam
697  provides a poor approximation of its equilibrium shape at the
698  first pressure value.
699  The Newton method still converges (very slowly initially, then
700  quadratically as it approaches the exact solution), even though
701  the initial maximum residual has a relatively large value of
702  19.6. Here are some exercises that explore the convergence
703  characteristics of the Newton method:
704  -# Experiment with the Newton solver and find the largest
705  value for the load increment, \c pext_increment, for which
706  the Newton method still converges.
707  -# Explain why the Newton method converges
708  very slowly for small values of \f$ p_{ext} \f$ and much more rapidly
709  at larger values, even though the load increment, \c
710  pext_increment, remains constant.
711  -# Compare the solutions for different values of
712  \c Problem::Newton_solver_tolerance. Is the default value
713  \f$ 10^{-8} \f$ adequate? Reduce it to \f$ 10^{-12} \f$ and
714  \f$ 10^{-18} \f$. What do you observe?
