32\section non_dim Theory and non-dimensionalisation
33
34\c oomph-lib's beam elements are based on geometrically
35nonlinear Kirchhoff-Love beam theory with incrementally linear
36constitutive equations (Young's modulus \f$ E \f$ and Poisson's ratio
37\f$ \nu \f$). The equations are implemented in non-dimensional form,
38obtained by non-dimensionalising all length on some lengthscale
39\f$ {\cal L} \f$, by scaling the stresses and the applied traction on the
40beam's effective Young's modulus \f$ E_{eff} = E/(1-\nu^2) \f$
41and by non-dimensionalising time on some timescale \f$ {\cal T} \f$ so that
42the dimensional (identified by an asterisk) and non-dimensional
43variables are related by
44\f[
45L^* = {\cal L} \ L,
46\f]
47\f[
48h^* = {\cal L} \ h,
49\f]
50\f[
51t^* = {\cal T} \ t,
52\f]
53\f[
54p_{ext}^* = E_{eff} \ p_{ext},
55\f]
56and
57\f[
58\sigma_0^* = E_{eff} \ \sigma_0.
59\f]
60The beam's undeformed shape is parametrised by a non-dimensional Lagrangian
61coordinate \f$ \xi = \xi^*/{\cal L} \f$ so that the non-dimensional
62position vector to a material particle on the beam's centreline in the
63undeformed
64configuration is given by \f$ {\bf r}_w(\xi) \f$ . We denote the
65unit normal to the beam's undeformed centreline by \f$ {\bf n} \f$.
66The applied traction \f$ {\bf f} = {\bf f}^*/E_{eff} \f$
67(a force per unit deformed length of the beam) deforms the beam,
68causing its material particles to be displaced to their
69new positions \f$ {\bf R}_w(\xi) \f$; the unit
70normal to the beam's deformed centreline is \f$ {\bf N} \f$.
71
72
73The non-dimensional form of the principle of virtual displacements
74that governs the beams deformation is then given by
75\f[
76\int_0^{L} \left[
77(\sigma_0 + \gamma) \ \delta
78\gamma +
79\frac{1}{12} h^2 \kappa
80 \ \delta \kappa -
81\left(\frac{1}{h} \sqrt{\frac{A}{a}} \ {\bf f} - \Lambda^2
82\frac{\partial^2 {\bf R}_w}{\partial t^2} \right) \cdot
83\delta {\bf R}_w
84 \right] \ \sqrt{a} \ d\xi = 0,
85\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1)
86\f]
87where
88\f[
89a = \frac{\partial {\bf r}_w}{\partial \xi}
90\cdot \frac{\partial {\bf r}_w}{\partial \xi},
91\f]
92and
93\f[
94A = \frac{\partial {\bf R}_w}{\partial \xi}
95\cdot \frac{\partial {\bf R}_w}{\partial \xi},
96\f]
97represent the squares of the lengths of infinitesimal material line elements
98in the undeformed and the deformed configurations, respectively.
99\f[
100ds = \sqrt{a} \ d\xi \mbox{\ \ \ \ and \ \ \ } dS = \sqrt{A} \ d\xi.
101\f]
102\f$ A \f$ and \f$ a \f$ may be interpreted as the "1x1 metric
103tensors" of the beam's centreline, in the deformed and undeformed
104configurations, respectively. The ratio \f$ \sqrt{A/a} \f$ represents
105the "extension ratio" or the "stretch" of the beam's centreline.
106
107 We represent the curvature of the beam's centreline before and
108after the deformation by
109\f[
110b = {\bf n} \cdot \frac{\partial^2 {\bf
111r}_w}{d\xi^2}
112\f]
113and
114\f[
115B = {\bf N} \cdot \frac{\partial^2 {\bf
116R}_w}{d\xi^2}
117\f]
118respectively. The ("1x1") strain and and bending "tensors" \f$ \gamma \f$ and
119\f$ \kappa \f$ are then given by
120\f[
121\gamma = \frac{1}{2}\left(A-a\right)
122\mbox{\ \ \ and \ \ \ }
123\kappa = - \left( B-b \right).
124\f]
125Finally,
126\f[
127\Lambda = \frac{{\cal L}}{{\cal T}} \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E_{eff}}}
128\f]
129is the ratio of the natural timescale of the beam's in-plane
130extensional oscillations,
131\f[
132{\cal T}_{natural} = {\cal L} \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{E_{eff}}},
133\f]
134to the timescale \f$ {\cal T}\f$ used in the non-dimensionalisation of the
135equations. \f$ \Lambda^2 \f$ may be interpreted as
136the non-dimensional wall density, therefore \f$ \Lambda=0 \f$
137corresponds to the case without wall inertia.
138
139
140\anchor hermite
141\c oomph-lib's \c HermiteBeamElement provides a discretisation of the
142variational principle (1) with one-dimensional, isoparametric,
143two-node Hermite solid mechanics elements. In these elements, the Eulerian
144positions of the nodes, accessible via \c Node::x(...), are regarded
145as unknowns, and Hermite interpolation is used to interpolate
146the position between the nodes, so that the Eulerian position of material
147points within an element is given by
148\f[
149x_i(s) = \sum_{j=1}^2 \sum_{k=1}^2 X_{ijk} \ \psi_{jk}(s)
150\mbox{\ \ \ for $i=1,2$},
151\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (2)
152\f]
153where \f$ s \in [-1,1] \f$ is the element's 1D local coordinate.
154The functions \f$ \psi_{jk}(s) \f$ are the one-dimensional
155Hermite shape functions
156\f[ \psi_{11}(s) = \frac{1}{4}\left(s^3 - 3s + 2\right), \f]
157\f[ \psi_{12}(s) = \frac{1}{4}\left(s^3 - s^2 - s +1 \right), \f]
158\f[ \psi_{21}(s) = \frac{1}{4}\left(2 + 3 s - s^3\right), \f]
159\f[ \psi_{22}(s) = \frac{1}{4}\left(s^3 + s^2 -s -1 \right). \f]
160They have the property that
161- \f$ \psi_{j1} = 1 \f$ at node \f$ j \f$ and \f$ \psi_{j1} = 0 \f$
162 at the other node. Furthermore, \f$ d\psi_{j1}/ds = 0 \f$ at
163 both nodes.
164- \f$ d\psi_{j2}/ds = 1 \f$ at node \f$ j \f$ and \f$ d\psi_{j2}/ds = 0 \f$
165 at the other node. Furthermore, \f$ \psi_{j2} = 0 \f$ at
166 both nodes.
167.
168The mapping (2) therefore provide an
169independent interpolation for the position and its derivative
170with respect to the local coordinate \f$ s \f$. As a result
171we have two types of (generalised) nodal coordinates:
172- \f$ X_{ij1} \f$ represents the \f$ i \f$-th coordinate of the element's
173 local node \f$ j \f$.
174- \f$ X_{ij2} \f$ represents the derivative of \f$ i \f$-th coordinate
175 with respect to the local coordinate \f$ s \f$, evaluated
176 at the element's local node \f$ j \f$.
177.
178This representation ensures the
179\f$ C^1 \f$ continuity of the wall shape, required by
180the variational principle (1) which contains second derivatives
181of \f$ {\bf R}_w \f$. Physically, the inter-element continuity
182of the slope is enforced by the beam's nonzero bending stiffness.
183
184\anchor solid_bound
185The two "types" of "generalised" positional degrees of freedom
186are accessible via the function \c Node::x_gen(k,i), where
187\c Node::x_gen(0,i) \f$ \equiv \f$ \c Node::x(i).
188The two types of positional degrees of freedom correspond to
189the two types of boundary conditions (pinned and clamped) that can be applied
190at the ends of the beam. Mathematically, the number of boundary
191conditions reflects the fact that the Euler-Lagrange equations
192of the variational principle are of fourth-order.
193

319\section results Results
320The animation shown below illustrates the deformation of the beam
321under increasing pressure. An increase in pressure initially deflects
322the beam vertically downwards. The pressure acts as a "follower
323load" since it always acts in the direction normal to the deformed
324beam. This causes the beam to deform into an approximately circular shape

333\section approx An approximate analytical solution
334
335Before discussing the details of the numerical solution, we will derive an
336approximate analytical solution of the problem. The analytical solution
337will be useful to validate the computational results, and the derivation
338will allow us to discuss certain aspects of the theory in more detail.
339
340We start by parametrising the beam's undeformed shape as
341\f[
342{\bf r}_w(\xi) =
343\left(
344\begin{array}{c}
345\xi \\
3460
347\end{array}
348\right)
349\mbox{ \ \ \ \ where $\xi \in [0,L].$}
350\f]
351The 1x1 "metric tensor" associated with this parametrisation is given by
352\f[
353a = \frac{{\bf r}_w(\xi)}{d\xi} \cdot \frac{{\bf r}_w(\xi)}{d\xi} = 1,
354\f]
355consistent with the fact that the Lagrangian coordinate \f$ \xi \f$
356is the arclength along the beam's undeformed centreline.
357
358
359If the beam is thin (so that \f$ h \ll 1 \f$) bending effects
360will be confined to thin boundary layers near its
361ends. The beam will therefore behave (approximately)
362like a "string under tension" and its deformed shape will
363be an arc of a circle. All material line elements will be stretched
364by the same amount so that the tension is spatially uniform. The position
365vector to the material particles on the beam's deformed centreline is
366therefore given by
367\f[
368{\bf R}_w(\xi) =
369\left(
370\begin{array}{c}
371\frac{1}{2}\left(1+\frac{\sin(-\alpha/2+\alpha\xi/L)}
372{\sin(\alpha/2)}\right) \\
373\frac{\cos(-\alpha/2+\alpha\xi/L)-\cos(\alpha/2)}
374{2\sin(\alpha/2)}
375\end{array}
376\right)
377\mbox{ \ \ \ \ where $\xi \in [0,L].$}
378\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (3)
379\f]
380(See the \ref comments for a more detailed discussion of this
381analytical solution.) Here \f$ \alpha \f$ is the opening angle
382of the circular arc as shown in this sketch:
383
384
385\image html string.gif "Sketch illustrating the approximate analytical solution for the problem. "
386\image latex string.eps "Sketch illustrating the approximate analytical solution for the problem. " width=0.75\textwidth
387
388This deformation generates a uniform stretch of
389\f[
390\frac{dS}{ds} = \sqrt{\frac{A}{a}} = \frac{1}{2} \frac{\alpha}{\sin(\alpha/2)},
391\f]
392corresponding to a uniform strain
393\f[
394\gamma = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{4}\frac{\alpha^2}{\sin^2(\alpha/2)}
395- 1 \right).
396\f]
397The incremental Hooke's law predicts a linear relation between
398the (2nd Piola Kirchhoff) stress, \f$ \sigma \f$, and the
399(Green) strain, \f$ \gamma \f$, so that
400\f[
401\sigma = \sigma_0 + \gamma,
402\f]
403where \f$ \sigma_0 \f$ is the axial pre-stress that acts in the undeformed
404configuration.
405
406An elementary force balance shows that the pressure
407\f$ p_{ext} \f$ required to deform the beam into the
408shape specified by (3), is given by "Laplace's law"
409\f[
410p_{ext} =\frac{{\tt T}}{R}
411\f]
412where
413\f[
414R = \frac{L}{2 \sin(\alpha/2)}
415\f]
416is the radius of the circular arc,
417and the axial tension \f$ {\tt T} \f$ is given by
418\f[
419{\tt T} = h \ (\sigma_0 + \gamma) \ \left|\frac{d{\bf R}_w}{d\xi}\right|
420= h \ (\sigma_0 + \gamma) \ \sqrt{A}.
421\f]
422In this expression we have used the fact that the 2nd Piola Kirchhoff
423stress \f$ \sigma \f$ decomposes the (physical) stress vector into the
424vector \f$ d{\bf R}_w/d\xi \f$. This vector is tangent to the
425deformed centreline but not necessarily a unit vector.
426
427The pressure required to deform the beam into a circular arc
428with opening angle \f$ \alpha \f$ is therefore given by
429\f[
430p_{ext} =
431 \frac{\alpha h}{L}
432 \ \left(\sigma_0 +
433\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{4}\frac{\alpha^2}{\sin^2(\alpha/2)} - 1
434\right)
435\right).
436\f]
437
438
439To facilitate comparisons with the numerical solutions, we determine
440the opening angle \f$ \alpha \f$ as a function of the vertical
441displacement \f$ d \f$ of the beam's midpoint by determining the
442auxiliary angle \f$ \beta \f$ from
443 \f[
444\tan\beta = \frac{2d}{L}.
445\f]
446The opening angle then follows from
447\f[
448\tan\left(\frac{\alpha}{2}\right) = \tan(\pi -
449 2\beta) = - \tan 2\beta = \frac{2\tan\beta}{\tan^{2}\beta -1}.
450\f]
451
452Here is a comparison between the computed and analytically predicted
453values for the pressure \f$ p_{ext} \f$, required to deflect
454the beam's midpoint by the specified displacement, \f$ d \f$.
455
456
457\image html trace.gif "Comparison between the computed and analytical solutions. "
458\image latex trace.eps "Comparison between the computed and analytical solutions. " width=0.75\textwidth

463<CENTER><B>Comment: Geometric and constitutive nonlinearities</B></CENTER>
464The comparison between the computational results and the analytical
465predictions is very satisfying but is important to realise that
466the agreement only validates the numerical solution, not the
467physical model. \c oomph-lib's \c HermiteBeamElement is based on a \b geometrically
468\b nonlinear theory, implying that the kinematics of the deformation
469are captured exactly for arbitrarily large displacements and
470rotations. However, the use of \b incrementally \b linear \b constitutive
471\b equations in the variational principle
472(1) can only be justified if the strain is small. This
473is clearly not the case for the deformations shown above. The
474combination of geometric nonlinearity with linear constitutive
475equations can, however, be justified in applications in which the beam
476undergoes large displacements with little extension of its centreline.
477This typically occurs in stability problems, such as the
478<a href="../../steady_ring/html/index.html">
479buckling of a circular ring under external pressure</a>, considered
480in <a href="../../steady_ring/html/index.html">another example</a>.

532\section problem The problem class
533
534The problem class has five member functions, only two of which are
535non-trivial:
536 - the problem constructor, \c ElasticBeamProblem(...), whose
537 arguments specify the number of elements and the beam's
538 undeformed length.
539 - the function \c parameter_study(), which computes the beam's
540 deformation for a range of external pressures.
541.
542
543 In the present problem, the functions
544\c Problem::actions_before_newton_solve() and \c
545Problem::actions_after_newton_solve() are not required, so remain
546empty. The function
547\c ElasticBeamProblem::mesh_pt() overloads the
548(virtual) function \c Problem::mesh_pt() to return a pointer
549to the specific mesh used in this problem. This avoids explicit re-casts
550when member functions of the specific mesh need to be accessed.
551
552 The class also includes three private data members which store
553a pointer to a node at which the displacement is documented,
554the length of the domain, and a pointer to the geometric object
555that specifies the beam's undeformed shape.

620\section param The Parameter Study
621
622 The function ElasticBeamProblem::parameter_study() is used to perform a
623parameter study, computing the beam's deformation for a range
624of external pressures. During the solution of this
625particular problem, the maximum residual in the Newton iteration
626can be greater than the default maximum value of 10.0.
627We increase the default value by assigning a (much) larger value to
628\c Problem::Max_residuals.
629
630Next, we choose the increment in the control parameter (the external pressure),
631set its initial value and open an output file that will contain
632the value of the external pressure, the mid-point displacement and
633external pressure computed from the analytical solution.
634We also create an output stream and a string
635that will be used to write the complete solution for each value of the
636external pressure.
637
638 In the loop, we increment the external pressure \f$ P_{ext} \f$,
639solve the problem, calculate the analytical prediction for the
640pressure that is required to achieve the computed deformation, plot
641the solution and write the pressure, the displacement and exact
642pressure to the trace file.

654-# Modify the code so that one end of the beam is no longer fixed in
655 space. What happens? Why?
656-# Increase the bending effects by increasing the beam's
657 thickness to \f$ h=0.1 \f$, say, and by "clamping" its ends,
658 so that \f$ d({\bf R}_w(\xi)\cdot {\bf e}_y)/d\xi = 0 \f$ at \f$ \xi=0 \f$
659 and \f$ \xi=L \f$. This condition can be enforced by pinning the
660 "type 1" (slope) positional degree of freedom in the vertical
661 (1) direction at both ends; this requires the insertion of the statement
662 \code
663 mesh_pt()->boundary_node_pt(b,0)->pin_position(1,1);
664 \endcode
665 in the loop in the \c Problem constructor.
666 You will have to adjust the number of elements
667 to fully resolve the bending boundary layers.
668-# \c oomph-lib's default \anchor newton_cust nonlinear solver, \c
669 Problem::newton_solve(...), provides an implementation of
670 the Newton method, which has the attractive feature
671 that it converges quadratically -- provided
672 a good initial guess for the solution is available. Good initial guesses
673 can often (usually?) be generated by computing the solution via a
674 sequence of substeps, as in the above example where we started with
675 a known solution (the undeformed beam -- the exact solution
676 for \f$ p_{ext} = p_{ext}^{(0)} =0 \f$ ) and used it as the
677 initial guess for the solution at a small external
678 pressure, \f$ p_{ext}^{(1)} \f$. When the Newton method converged,
679 we used the computed
680 solution as the initial guess for the solution at a
681 slightly larger pressure, \f$ p_{ext}^{(2)} \f$, etc.
682 If the increase in the load (or some other control parameter) is
683 too large, the Newton method will diverge. To avoid unnecessary
684 computations, the Newton iteration is terminated if:
685 - the number of iterations exceeds \c
686 Problem::Max_newton_iterations (which has a default value of 10)
687 - the residual exceeds \c Problem::Max_residuals (which has a
688 default value of 10.0)
689 .
690 The Newton method continues until the maximum residual
691 has been reduced to \c Problem::Newton_solver_tolerance, which has
692 a default value of \f$ 10^{-8} \f$.
693 \n\n
694 All three values are protected data members of the \c Problem base class
695 and can therefore be changed in any specific \c Problem. For
696 instance, in the problem considered above, the undeformed beam
697 provides a poor approximation of its equilibrium shape at the
698 first pressure value.
699 The Newton method still converges (very slowly initially, then
700 quadratically as it approaches the exact solution), even though
701 the initial maximum residual has a relatively large value of
702 19.6. Here are some exercises that explore the convergence
703 characteristics of the Newton method:
704 -# Experiment with the Newton solver and find the largest
705 value for the load increment, \c pext_increment, for which
706 the Newton method still converges.
707 -# Explain why the Newton method converges
708 very slowly for small values of \f$ p_{ext} \f$ and much more rapidly
709 at larger values, even though the load increment, \c
710 pext_increment, remains constant.
711 -# Compare the solutions for different values of
712 \c Problem::Newton_solver_tolerance. Is the default value
713 \f$ 10^{-8} \f$ adequate? Reduce it to \f$ 10^{-12} \f$ and
714 \f$ 10^{-18} \f$. What do you observe?
