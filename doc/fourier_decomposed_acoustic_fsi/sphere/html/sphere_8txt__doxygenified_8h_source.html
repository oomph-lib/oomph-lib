22 In this document we discuss the solution of
23 time-harmonic acoustic fluid-structure interaction problems
24 in cylindrical polar coordinates, using a Fourier decomposition of the
25 solution in the azimuthal direction. These equations are useful to
26 solve problems involving axisymmetric elastic bodies.
27 
28  We start by reviewing the relevant theory and then
29 present the solution of a simple model problem -- the sound radiation
30 from an oscillating sphere that is coated with a compressible
31 elastic layer.
32 
33 This problem combines the problems discussed in the tutorials
34 illustrating
35 - <a href="../../../time_harmonic_fourier_decomposed_linear_elasticity/cylinder/html/index.html">
36 the solution of the time-harmonic equations of
37 linear elasticity in cylindrical polar coordinates</a>
38 .
39 and
40 - <a href="../../../fourier_decomposed_helmholtz/sphere_scattering/html/index.html">the Helmholtz
41 equation in cylindrical polar coordinates</a>
42 .

46 cartesian counterpart</a>. The considerable overlap of material is deliberate

53 \section theory Theory: Time-harmonic acoustic fluid-structure interaction problems in cylindrical polar coordinates
54 
55 The figure below shows a sketch of a representative model problem:
56 a sphere is immersed in an inviscid compressible fluid and
57 performs a prescribed harmonic oscillation of radian
58 frequency \f$\omega\f$. The sphere is coated with a compressible elastic
59 layer. We wish to compute the displacement field in the elastic coating
60 (assumed to be described by the <a href="../../../time_harmonic_fourier_decomposed_linear_elasticity/cylinder/html/index.html">equations of
61 time-harmonic linear elasticity</a>) and the pressure distribution
62 in the fluid (governed by
63 <a href="../../../fourier_decomposed_helmholtz/sphere_scattering/html/index.html">the Helmholtz
64 equation</a>). The two sets of equations interact at the interface
65 between fluid and solid: the fluid pressure exerts a traction
66 onto the elastic layer, while the motion of the elastic layer
67 drives the fluid motion via the non-penetration condition.
68 
69 \image html coated_sphere.gif "Sketch of the model problem: Forced oscillations of a sphere (black) deform an elastic coating layer (pink) which is surrounded by a compressible fluid. "
70 \image latex coated_sphere.eps "Sketch of the model problem: Forced oscillations of a sphere (black) deform an elastic coating layer (pink) which is surrounded by a compressible fluid. " width=0.6\textwidth

72 \subsection fluid The fluid model: the Helmholtz equation
73 We describe the behaviour of the fluid in terms of the displacement
74 field, \f$ {\bf d}^*(r^*,z^*,\varphi,t^*)\f$, of the fluid particles,
75 where \f$r^*, z^* \f$ and \f$ \varphi \f$ are cylindrical polar coordinates.
76 As usual, we use asterisks to distinguish dimensional quantities from
77 their non-dimensional equivalents. The fluid is inviscid
78 and compressible, with a bulk modulus \f$ B \f$, such
79 that the acoustic pressure is given by
80 \f$ P^* = - B \ \nabla^{*} \cdot {\bf d}^* \f$
81 We assume that the fluid motion is irrotational and can be described by a
82 displacement potential \f$\Phi^*\f$, such that \f$ {\bf d}^*= \nabla^*
83 \Phi^*. \f$ We consider steady-state time-harmonic
84 oscillations and write the displacement potential and the pressure
85 as \f$\Phi^*(r^*,z^*,\varphi,t^*) =
86 \mbox{Re} \{\phi^*(r^*,z^*,\varphi) \exp(-{\rm i} \omega t^*)\} \f$ and
87 \f$P^*(r^*,z^*,\varphi,t^*) = \mbox{Re}\{p^*(r^*,z^*,\varphi)
88 \exp(-{\rm i} \omega t^*)\}\f$, respectively,
89 where \f$\mbox{Re}\{\ldots \}\f$ denotes the real part.
90 For small disturbances, the linearised Euler equation reveals that the
91 time-harmonic pressure is related to the displacement potential
92 via \f$ p^* = \rho_{\rm f} \omega^2 \phi^*\f$ where
93 \f$ \rho_{\rm f}\f$ is the ambient fluid density.
94 We non-dimensionalise all lengths
95 on a problem-specific lengthscale \f$ {\cal L}\f$ (e.g. the
96 outer radius of the coating layer) such that
97 \f$ [r^*, z^*] = {\cal L} [r,z], \ {\bf d}^* = {\cal L} {\bf d} \f$
98 and \f$\phi^* = {\cal L}^2 \phi\f$.
99 
100 We then decompose \f$ \phi \f$ into its Fourier components by writing
101 \f[
102 {\phi}(r,\varphi,z) = \sum_{N=-\infty}^{\infty} \phi_N(r,z) \exp({\rm i}
103 N \varphi).
104 \f]
105 Since the governing equations are linear we can compute each Fourier
106 component \f$ \phi_N(r,z) \f$ individually by solving
107 \f[
108 \nabla^2 {\phi_{N}}(r,z) + \left(k^2-\frac{N^2}{r^2}\right) \phi_N(r,z) = 0,
109  \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1)
110 \f]
111 where the square of the non-dimensional wavenumber,
112 \f[
113 k^2 = \frac{\rho_{\rm f} (\omega {\cal L})^2}{B},
114 \f]
115 represents the ratio of the typical
116 inertial fluid pressure induced by the wall oscillation to the
117 `stiffness' of the fluid.
118 
119 \subsection elastic The solid model: the time harmonic equations of linear elasticity
120 
121 We model the coating layer as a linearly elastic solid, described
122 in terms of a displacement field
123 \f$ {\bf U}^*(r^*,z^*,\varphi,t^*)\f$, with stress tensor
124 \f[
125 \mbox{\boldmath$ \tau^*$}=\frac{E}{1+\nu}\left(
126 \frac{\nu}{1-2\nu}(\mbox{\boldmath$\nabla^*\cdot U^*$})\textbf{I}+
127 \frac{1}{2}(\mbox{\boldmath$ \nabla^* U^*$}+
128 \mbox{\boldmath$ \nabla^* U^*$}^{{\rm T}})\right),
129 \f]
130 where \f$E\f$ and \f$\nu\f$ are the material's Young's modulus and Poisson's
131 ratio, respectively.
132 
133  As before, we assume a time-harmonic solution
134 with frequency \f$ \omega\f$ so that
135 \f$ {\bf U}^*(r^*,z^*,\varphi,t^*) = {\rm Re} \{
136 {\bf u}^*(r^*,z^*,\varphi) \exp(-{\rm i}\omega t^*)\}\f$.
137 We non-dimensionalise the displacements on \f${\cal L}\f$ and the
138 stress on Young's modulus, \f$E\f$, so that \f${\bf u}^* = {\cal L}
139 {\bf u} \f$ and \f$ \mbox{\boldmath$ \tau^*$} = E \mbox{\boldmath$
140 \tau$}\f$. The deformation of the elastic coating is then
141 governed by the time-harmonic Navier-Lame equations
142 <CENTER>
143 \f[
144 \mbox{\boldmath$ \nabla\cdot\tau$}+\Omega^2\mbox{\boldmath$ u$} = {\bf
145 0}
146 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (2)
147 \f]
148 </CENTER>
149 where
150 <CENTER>
151 \f[
152 \mbox{\boldmath$ \tau $}=\frac{1}{1+\nu}\left(
153 \frac{\nu}{1-2\nu}(\mbox{\boldmath$\nabla\cdot u$})\textbf{I}+
154 \frac{1}{2}(\mbox{\boldmath$ \nabla u$}+\mbox{\boldmath$ \nabla
155 u$}^{{\rm T}})\right)
156 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (3)
157 \f]
158 </CENTER>
159 is the non-dimensional stress tensor.
160 The (square of the) non-dimensional wavenumber
161 \f[
162 \Omega^2 = \frac{\rho_{\rm s} (\omega {\cal L})^2}{E},
163 \f]
164 where \f$ \rho_{\rm s}\f$ is the solid density,
165 represents the ratio of the typical inertial solid pressure
166 induced by the wall oscillation to the
167 stiffness of the elastic coating. We note that for a `light'
168 coating we have \f$\Omega \ll 1\f$.
169 
170 We then decompose \f$ {\bf u} \f$ into its Fourier components by writing
171 \f[
172 {\bf u}(r,\varphi,z) = \sum_{N=-\infty}^{\infty} {\bf u}_N(r,z) \exp({\rm i}
173 N \varphi).
174 \f]
175 This decomposition allows us to
176 remove the \f$ \theta \f$-dependence from the equations by writing
177 \f$ \partial (.)/\partial\theta={\rm i} N (.) \f$.
178 Since the governing equations are linear, we can solve for each
179 Fourier component separately and specify the Fourier wavenumber
180 \f$ N \f$ as a parameter.
181 
182 \subsection bc Boundary conditions
183 The inner surface of the elastic coating, \f$ \partial D_{\rm s} \f$,
184 is subject to the prescribed
185 displacement imposed by the oscillating cylinder. For instance, if the inner
186 cylinder performs spherically symmetric oscillations of non-dimensional
187 amplitude \f$ \epsilon\f$, we have
188 \f[
189 \mbox{\bf u}_0 = \epsilon \, \mbox{\bf e}_{r_{\rm sphere}}
190 \mbox{\ \ \ \ \ on $\partial D_{\rm s}$},
191 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (4)
192 \f]
193 where
194 \f[
195 \mbox{\bf e}_{r_{\rm sphere}} = \frac{r}{\sqrt{r^2+z^2}} {\bf e}_r +
196 \frac{z}{\sqrt{r^2+z^2}} {\bf e}_z
197 \f]
198 is the unit vector in the (spherically!) radial
199 direction, while the other Fourier components remain zero,
200 \f$ \mbox{\bf u}_j = {\bf 0} \f$ for \f$ j=\pm 1, \pm 2, ...\f$
201 The fluid-loaded surface of the elastic coating, \f$ \partial D_{\rm
202 f} \f$,
203 is subject to the
204 fluid pressure. The non-dimensional traction exerted by the
205 fluid onto the solid (on the solid stress scale) is therefore given by
206 \f[
207 {\bf t}^{\rm [solid]} = \mbox{\boldmath$ \tau $}^{\rm [solid]} \cdot {\bf n} =
208  - \phi \ Q \ {\bf n}
209 \mbox{\ \ \ \ \ on $\partial D_{\rm f}$},
210 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (5)
211 \f]
212 where the \f$ {\bf n} = n_r {\bf e}_r + n_z {\bf e}_z \f$
213 is the outer unit normal on the solid
214 boundary \f$\partial D_{\rm f}\f$ and
215 \f[
216 Q=\frac{\rho_{\rm f}({\cal L}\omega)^2}{E}
217 \f]
218 is the final non-dimensional parameter in the problem. It represents the
219 ratio of the typical inertial fluid pressure induced by the wall
220 oscillation to the stiffness of the elastic coating. The parameter \f$Q\f$
221 therefore provides a measure of the strength of the fluid-structure
222 interaction (FSI) in the sense that for \f$Q \to 0\f$
223 the elastic coating does not `feel' the presence of the fluid.
224 
225 The fluid is forced by the normal displacement of the solid. Imposing
226 the non-penetration condition \f$({\bf d} - {\bf u}) \cdot {\bf n} =
227 0\f$ on \f$\partial
228 D_{\rm f}\f$ yields a Neumann condition for the displacement
229 potential,
230 \f[
231 \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial n} = {\bf u} \cdot {\bf n}
232 \mbox{\ \ \ \ \ on $\partial D_{\rm f}$}.
233 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (6)
234 \f]
235 Finally, the displacement potential for the fluid must satisfy the
236 Sommerfeld radiation condition
237 \f[
238 \lim_{r_{\rm sphere} \to \infty} r_{\rm sphere}
239 \left( \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial r_{\rm sphere} } -
240 {\rm i} k \phi \right) = 0,
241 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (7)
242 \f]
243 where \f$ r_{\rm sphere} = \sqrt{(r^2+z^2)} \f$ is the (spherical) radius.
244 The Sommerfeld radiation condition ensures that the
245 oscillating sphere does not generate any incoming waves.
246 
247 Equations (5), (6) and
248 (7) apply for each Fourier component
249 of the solution.

254 \section impl Implementation
255 The implementation of the coupled problem follows the usual procedure
256 for multi-domain problems in \c oomph-lib. We discretise the
257 constituent single-physics problems using the existing single-physics
258 elements, here \c oomph-lib's
259 - <a href="../../../fourier_decomposed_helmholtz/sphere_scattering/html/index.html">Helmholtz
260 elements</a>
261 .
262 and
263 - <a href="../../../time_harmonic_fourier_decomposed_linear_elasticity/cylinder/html/index.html">
264  time-harmonic linear elasticity elements</a>
265 .
266 for the discretisation of the PDEs (1) and
267 (2), respectively.
268 The displacement boundary condition (4) on the inner surface
269 of the elastic coating is imposed as usual by pinning
270 the relevant degrees of freedom, exactly as in a <a href="../../../time_harmonic_fourier_decomposed_linear_elasticity/cylinder/html/index.html">single-physics
271 solid mechanics problem</a>. Similarly, the
272 Sommerfeld radiation condition (7) on the outer boundary
273 of the fluid domain can be imposed by any of the methods
274 available for the solution of the single-physics
275 Helmholtz equation, such as a <a href="../../../fourier_decomposed_helmholtz/sphere_scattering/html/index.html#DtN">
276 Dirichlet-to-Neumann mapping.</a>
277 
278 The boundary conditions (5) and
279 (6) at the fluid-solid interface are
280 traction boundary conditions for the solid, and Neumann boundary
281 conditions for the Helmholtz equation, respectively. In a single-physics problem
282 we would impose such boundary conditions by attaching suitable \c FaceElements
283 to the appropriate boundaries of the "bulk" elements, as shown in the
284 sketch below: \c
285 TimeHarmonicFourierDecomposedLinearElasticityTractionElement
286 could be used to
287 impose a (given) traction, \f$ {\bf t}_0\f$, onto the solid;
288 \c FourierDecomposedHelmholtzFluxElements could be used to
289 impose a (given) normal derivative,
290 \f$ f_0\f$, on the displacement potential. Both \f$ {\bf t}_0\f$
291 and \f$ f_0 \f$ would usually be specified in a user-defined namespace
292 and accessed via function pointers as indicated in the right half of
293 the sketch.
294 
295 \image html single_physics.gif "Sketch illustrating the imposition of flux and traction boundary conditions in single-physics problems. The continuous problems are shown on the left; the discretised ones on the right. "
296 \image latex single_physics.eps "Sketch illustrating the imposition of flux and traction boundary conditions in single-physics problems. The continuous problems are shown on the left; the discretised ones on the right. " width=0.6\textwidth
297 
298 
299 In the coupled problem, illustrated in the left half of the
300 next sketch, the traction acting on the solid becomes a function
301 of the displacement potential via the boundary condition
302 (5), while the normal derivative of the
303 displacement potential is given in terms of the solid displacement via
304 equation (6). Note that corresponding points on the
305 FSI boundary \f$ \partial D_{\rm f}\f$ are
306 identified by matching values of the boundary
307 coordinate \f$ \zeta \f$ which is assumed to be consistent between
308 the two domains.
309 
310 The implementation of this interaction in the discretised problem
311 is illustrated in the right half of the sketch: We replace the single-physics
312 \c FourierDecomposedHelmholtzFluxElements by
313 \c FourierDecomposedHelmholtzFluxFromNormalDisplacementBCElements,
314 and the \c TimeHarmonicFourierDecomposedLinearElasticityTractionElements by
315 \c FourierDecomposedTimeHarmonicLinElastLoadedByHelmholtzPressureBCElements.
316 (Yes, we like to be verbose...).
317 Both of these \c FaceElements are derived from the \c ElementWithExternalElement
318 base class and can therefore store a pointer to an "external"
319 element that provides the information required to impose the
320 appropriate boundary condition. Thus, the \c
321  FourierDecomposedHelmholtzFluxFromNormalDisplacementBCElements
322 store pointers to
323 the "adjacent" time-harmonic linear elasticity elements (from which
324 they obtain the boundary displacement required for the imposition of
325 (6)),
326 while the \c
327 FourierDecomposedTimeHarmonicLinElastLoadedByHelmholtzPressureBCElements
328 store pointers to the "adjacent" Helmholtz elements that provide the
329 value of the displacement potential required for the evaluation of
330 (5).

436 following any change in the independent parameters.

475 \subsection class The problem class
476 
477 The \c Problem class is templated by the types of the
478 "bulk" elements used to discretise the Fourier-decomposed
479 time-harmonic linear elasticity and Helmholtz equations,
480 respectively. It contains the usual member functions to attach
481 \c FaceElements to the bulk meshes in order to apply the
482 various Neumann boundary conditions. We note that the costly
483 recomputation of the \f$ \gamma- \f$ integral in the
484 Dirichlet-to-Neumann mapping before the Newton convergence check
485 (implemented in \c actions_before_newton_convergence_check())
486 can be avoided by declaring the problem to be linear;
487 see the discussion in the
488 <a href="../../../fourier_decomposed_helmholtz/sphere_scattering/html/index.html#DtN">
489 Helmholtz tutorial</a> and \ref comm.

683 \section comm_ex Comments and Exercises
684 \subsection comm Comments
685 
686 - The application of the Sommerfeld radiation condition via
687  a Dirichlet-to-Neumann mapping is discussed in detail
688  in <a href="../../../fourier_decomposed_helmholtz/sphere_scattering/html/index.html#DtN">another
689  tutorial.</a> We explain there that, because the \f$ \gamma \f$
690  integral depends on the solution,
691  it must be recomputed whenever the unknowns are updated during
692  the Newton iteration. This is best done by adding a call to
693  \c FourierDecomposedHelmholtzDtNMesh::setup_gamma() to
694  \c Problem::actions_before_newton_convergence_check() as done in the
695  driver code listed above.
696  However, if Helmholtz's equation is solved in isolation (or within
697  a coupled, but linear problem as in the present case ), the
698  Newton method is guaranteed to converge
699  in one iteration. In such cases the unnecessary recomputation of
700  \f$ \gamma \f$ after the one-and-only Newton iteration can be
701  suppressed by setting \c Problem::Problem_is_nonlinear to
702  \c false -- in that case, \c oomph-lib's Newton solver
703  skips the convergence check in the Newton iteration and simply
704  accepts the solution obtained after one linear solve.
705 - You may have noticed that, unlike the tutorial for the
706  <a href="../../../acoustic_fsi/acoustic_fsi_annulus/html/index.html">
707  cartesian counterpart</a> of the model problem considered here, we
708  neither demonstrate how to apply the Sommerfeld radiation condition
709  by approximate/absorbing boundary conditions (ABCs), nor do we
710  demonstrate the use of spatial adaptivity.
711  This is because the relevant elements have not (yet!) been written
712  (Any volunteers?). If you wish to enable spatial adaptivity we
713  suggest using an unstructured adaptive mesh, using the methodology
714  demonstrated in
715  <a href="../../../fourier_decomposed_acoustic_fsi/unstructured_sphere/html/index.html#DtN">another tutorial.</a>

721 \subsection ex Exercises
722 - As shown in the \ref appendix, the time-averaged radiated power
723  \f$ \overline{\cal P}\f$ depends
724  on the derivatives of the displacement potential \f$ \phi \f$. This
725  implies that the value for \f$ \overline{\cal P}\f$ computed from the
726  finite-element solution for \f$ \phi \f$ is not
727  as accurate as the displacement potential itself. Computing
728  \f$ \overline{\cal P}\f$ to a certain tolerance (e.g. to
729  "graphical accuracy" as in the plot shown above) therefore tends to
730  require meshes that are much finer than would be required
731  if we were only interested in \f$ \phi \f$ itself.
732  \n\n
733  Investigate the accuracy of the computational predictions for \f$
734  \overline{\cal P}\f$ by:
735  - increasing the spatial resolution e.g. by using
736  the command line flag \c --el_multiplier which controls the number
737  of elements in the mesh.
738  - reducing the outer radius of the computational domain, using
739  the command line flag \c --outer_radius, say.
740  - varying the element type, from the bi-linear
741  \c QFourierDecomposedHelmholtzElement<2>
742  to the bi-cubic \c QFourierDecomposedHelmholtzElement<4>, say.
743  .
744  Which of these approaches gives you the "most accuracy" for a given number of
745  degrees of freedom?

753 \section appendix Appendix: The time-averaged radiated power
754 This appendix provides a brief summary of the computation (and
755 non-dimensionalisation) of the time-averaged power
756 \f$ \overline{\cal P }^* \f$ radiated across the closed surface
757 \f$ \partial V \f$ (with outer unit normal \f$ {\bf n} \f$)
758 of a fluid volume \f$ V \f$.
759 In dimensional terms, \f$ \overline{\cal P }^* \f$ is given by
760 \f[
761 \overline{\cal P }^* = \oint_{\partial V} I^* dA^*,
762 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (9)
763 \f]
764 where the intensity
765 \f[
766 I^* = \frac{1}{{\cal T}} \int_0^{\cal T}
767 {\bf U}^* \cdot P^* {\bf n} \
768 dt^*
769 \f]
770 depends on the fluid velocity \f$ {\bf U}^* =
771 \partial {\bf d}^*/\partial t^* = \partial/\partial t^* (\nabla^*
772 \Phi^*) \f$ and the pressure \f$ P^* = \rho_{\rm f} \omega^2 \Phi^* \f$.
773 The time average is taken over the period of the oscillation,
774 \f$ {\cal T} = 2\pi/\omega. \f$ Using our time-periodic ansatz
775 for the fluid displacement potential, \f$ \Phi^* =
776 Re\{ \phi^* \exp(-{\rm i}\omega t^*) \} \f$, the intensity can
777 be re-written as
778 \f[
779 I^* = \frac{1}{2} \rho_{\rm f} \omega^3
780 \bigg( Im \{\frac{\partial \phi^*}{\partial n^*} \} Re\{\phi^* \} -
781  Re \{\frac{\partial \phi^*}{\partial n^*} \} Im\{\phi^* \}
782 \bigg).
783 \f]
784 Next we express the surface integral in (9) in
785 cylindrical polar coordinates. Assuming the (axisymmetric)
786 boundary of the fluid domain is parametrised (in the \f$
787 (r^*,z^*)\f$ plane) as
788 \f$ r^* = R^*(s^*)\f$ and \f$ z^* = Z^*(s^*)\f$, where \f$ s^* \f$
789 is some curve parameter (e.g. the arclength), we have
790 \f[
791 \overline{\cal P }^* = \int \int_0^{2\pi} I^*(s^*,\varphi)
792 \sqrt{ \left(\frac{\partial R^*}{\partial s^*}\right)^2 +
793  \left(\frac{\partial Z^*}{\partial s^*}\right)^2 }
794  R^*(s^*) \ d\varphi \ ds^*
795 \f]
796 The power associated with the \f$ N-\f$th azimuthal Fourier mode,
797 \f$ \phi^*_N(r^*,z^*) \exp({\rm i}N \varphi) \f$, is then given by
798 \f[
799 \overline{\cal P }^*_N = \pi \rho_{\rm f} \omega^3 \int
800 \bigg( Im \{\frac{\partial \phi_N^*}{\partial n^*} \} Re\{\phi_N^* \} -
801  Re \{\frac{\partial \phi_N^*}{\partial n^*} \} Im\{\phi_N^* \}
802 \bigg)
803 \sqrt{ \left(\frac{\partial R^*}{\partial s^*}\right)^2 +
804  \left(\frac{\partial Z^*}{\partial s^*}\right)^2 }
805  R^*(s^*) \ ds^*.
806 \f]
807 Non-dimensionalising all lengths on \f$ {\cal L}\f$ and the
808 displacement potential on \f$ {\cal L}^2 \f$ then yields the
809 following expression for the non-dimensional time-averaged radiated power
810 \f[
811 \overline{\cal P }_N =
812 \frac{\overline{\cal P }^*_N}{\rho_{\rm f} \omega^3 {\cal L}^5}
813 = \pi \int
814 \bigg( Im \{\frac{\partial \phi_N}{\partial n} \} Re\{\phi_N \} -
815  Re \{\frac{\partial \phi_N}{\partial n} \} Im\{\phi_N \}
816 \bigg)
817 \sqrt{ \left(\frac{\partial R}{\partial s}\right)^2 +
818  \left(\frac{\partial Z}{\partial s}\right)^2 }
819  R(s) \ ds.
820 \f]
821 This quantity is computed by \c oomph-lib on an element-by-element
822 basis, using the function
823 \c FourierDecomposedHelmholtzBCElementBase::global_power_contribution(...).
824 
825 The computation is most easily validated by comparing against
826 analytical results in which the displacement potential is expressed
827 in spherical polar coordinates \f$ (r_{\rm sphere}^*, \theta, \varphi)
828 \f$ where \f$ r_{\rm sphere}^* = \sqrt{r^{*2} + z^{*2}} \f$ and
829 \f$ \theta \f$ is the zenith angle which varies from \f$ \theta=0\f$ at
830 the "North pole" to \f$ \theta=\pi \f$ at the "South pole".
831 If we evaluate the surface integral in (9)
832 on a spherical surface of dimensional radius \f$ a^* \f$, the dimensional
833 time-averaged radiated power is
834 \f[
835 \overline{\cal P }^* = \int_0^\pi \int_0^{2\pi} I^*(r_{\rm sphere}^*=a^*,
836 \theta,\varphi)\ a^{*2} \sin(\theta) \ d\varphi \ d\theta.
837 \f]
838 The power associated with \f$ N-\f$th azimuthal Fourier mode,
839 \f$ \phi^*_N(r^*,z^*) \exp({\rm i}N \varphi) \f$, is then given by
840 \f[
841 \overline{\cal P }^*_N = \pi a^{*2} \rho_{\rm f} \omega^3 \int_0^\pi
842 \left.\bigg( Im \{\frac{\partial \phi_N^*}{\partial n^*} \} Re\{\phi_N^* \} -
843  Re \{\frac{\partial \phi_N^*}{\partial n^*} \} Im\{\phi_N^* \}
844 \bigg)\right|_{r_{\rm sphere}^*=a^*}
845 \sin(\theta) \ d\theta,
846 \f]
847 where \f$ \partial / \partial n^* = \partial / \partial r_{\rm
848 sphere}^* \f$. The non-dimensional time-averaged power, evaluated
849 on a sphere of non-dimensional radius \f$ a = a^*/{\cal L}\f$ is then
850 given by
851 \f[
852 \overline{\cal P }_N =
853 \frac{\overline{\cal P }^*_N}{\rho_{\rm f} \omega^3 {\cal L}^5}
854 = \pi a^2 \int_0^\pi
855 \left.
856 \bigg( Im \{\frac{\partial \phi_N}{\partial n} \} Re\{\phi_N \} -
857  Re \{\frac{\partial \phi_N}{\partial n} \} Im\{\phi_N \}
858 \bigg)\right|_{r_{\rm sphere}=a} \sin(\theta) \ d\theta,
859 \f]
860 where \f$ \partial / \partial n = \partial / \partial r_{\rm
861 sphere} \f$.
862 
863 The derivation of the analytical expression for
864 \f$ \overline{\cal P }_N \f$
865 against which we validated our computational results were performed
866 with maple, using the script
867 <A HREF="../../../../demo_drivers/interaction/fourier_decomposed_acoustic_fsi/exact.map">
868  demo_drivers/interaction/fourier_decomposed_acoustic_fsi/exact.map
869  </A>.
